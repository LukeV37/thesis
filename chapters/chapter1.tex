\section{Collider Experiments}

\subsection{The Large Hadron Collider}

The Large Hadron Collider (LHC)%~\cite{Evans:2008zzb} 
is a proton--proton synchrotron 
operating at CERN, near Geneva, Switzerland, and is currently the highest-energy particle 
collider ever constructed. The primary physics motivation for the LHC was the experimental 
confirmation of the Brout--Englert--Higgs mechanism of electroweak symmetry breaking, the 
discovery of which was announced in July 2012 by the ATLAS and CMS 
collaborations%~\cite{HIGG-2012-27, CMS-HIG-12-028}
. Beyond the Higgs boson, the LHC 
program pursues a broad range of open questions in the Standard Model and beyond, 
including searches for supersymmetric partners, dark matter candidates, and precision 
measurements of the top quark and gauge sector. With detector systems reading out hundreds 
of millions of channels at 40\,MHz and data pipelines processing petabytes of collision 
data per year, the LHC experimental program represents one of the largest and most 
complex data science endeavors ever undertaken.

Protons are sourced from hydrogen gas by stripping electrons with a strong electric field, 
after which a linear accelerator (LINAC\,4) brings them to 160\,MeV. They are then injected 
into the Proton Synchrotron Booster (PSB), which accelerates them to 2\,GeV, before passing 
into the Proton Synchrotron (PS) at 26\,GeV and subsequently the Super Proton Synchrotron 
(SPS) at 450\,GeV, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:CERN_complex}. Finally, two 
counter-rotating beams are injected into the LHC ring, where superconducting NbTi dipole 
magnets operating at 1.9\,K and a peak field of 8.33\,T bend the beams around the 
26.7\,km circumference. Radio-frequency (RF) cavities operating at 400\,MHz accelerate 
the protons to a Lorentz factor of $\gamma \approx 7000$, corresponding to a velocity of 
$v \approx 0.9999991\,c$ and a centre-of-mass energy of $\sqrt{s} = 13.6$\,TeV during 
Run~3, representing the world's highest centre-of-mass energy. The beams are structured 
into bunches spaced 25\,ns apart and are brought to collision at four interaction points 
around the ring, housing the ATLAS, CMS, ALICE, and LHCb experiments.

\subsection{Upgraded Tracking System} % MOVE TO HL-LHC Introduction section or PILEUP section

For the High-Luminosity LHC (HL-LHC), the ATLAS inner detector will be replaced entirely by the all-silicon Inner Tracker (ITk)~\cite{}. The HL-LHC will deliver instantaneous luminosities of up to $7 \times 10^{34}$\,cm$^{-2}$s$^{-1}$\cite{2105.10367}, resulting in an average of $\langle\mu\rangle \approx 200$ simultaneous $pp$ interactions per bunch crossing  (pile-up). The integrated luminosity is expected to reach 4000 $fb^{-1}$ by the end of the HL-LHC program, a significant increase from around 350 $fb^{-1}$ obtained in Run 3. As the current inner detector is inadequate for this radiation environment and track density, the TRT and SCT are fully decommissioned and replaced by the upgraded ITk. The ITk extends the tracking acceptance to $|\eta| < 4.0$, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:ATLAS_ITk}, significantly improving forward track reconstruction for physics processes with boosted topologies and forward jets.

The ITk comprises two subsystems. The ITk Pixel detector forms the innermost region, consisting of five barrel layers and multiple end-cap rings extending to $|z| = 2850$mm, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ATLAS_tracker}. With 5 billion readout channels, it provides high-granularity space-point measurements close to the interaction point, retaining excellent vertex resolution in the high pile-up environment. The ITk Strip detector surrounds the pixel system, consisting of four barrel layers extending to $|\eta| < 2.5$ and six end-cap discs on each side covering $1.1 < |\eta| < 4.0$. The strip detectors have 50 million readout channels, and strip modules use short strips of 24.1mm and 48.2mm length in the barrel and end-cap, respectively.

\subsection{Likelihood Fits}

In order to measure signal strength, $\mu$, we perform a statistical procedure to match the theoretical modeling, MC, to real world data. A likelihood function $\mathcal{L}(\mu,\theta)$ is constructed as the product of Poisson probability terms over the bins of the input distributions where $x_i$ is data counted in bin i and $s_i$ and $b_i$ are signal and background predictions, respectively. The parameter of interest (POI), or signal strength $\mu$, is extracted by maximising the likelihood function $\mathcal{L}$. The effects of systematic uncertainties are quantified as $\theta$, also called Nuissance Parameters NP, and are often assumed to be constrained by gaussian distributions. $\alpha_j$ is known as the global measurement data. Usually assumed mean=0 and var=1. The likelihood fit function is shown below.

\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{L}(\mu,\theta|x)=\prod_i Pois(x_i|\mu \cdot s_i(\theta)+b_i(\theta)) \prod_j C(\alpha_j|\theta_j)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Neural Networks}

Modern experimental high energy physics relies on advnaced data science techniques to perform efficient data analysis. Neural networks have become a very common practice for various data anlaysis techniques. Artificial neurons are the basic computational units of a neural network. Each neuron computes a weighted sum of its inputs and passes the result through a non-linear activation function,
producing a scalar output. By arranging neurons into layers and stacking multiple layers in
sequence, one obtains a deep neural network (DNN) capable of learning hierarchical
representations of the input data. The first layer receives the raw input features; each
subsequent layer operates on the activations of the previous one, progressively extracting more
abstract structure. Given sufficient depth and width, such networks are universal function
approximators %~\cite{Hornik1989}, 
and when trained by minimizing a suitable loss function via
stochastic gradient descent and back-propagation, they can learn highly non-linear decision
boundaries directly from data.

The network consisted of several hidden layers with rectified linear unit (ReLU) activations.
The feed-forward architecture allowed the network to learn non-linear combinations of the input.
The fundamental limitation of NNs, however, remained the same: the
inputs were fixed-size vectors of high-level summary statistics, and any information not
captured by those summaries was discarded before reaching the classifier.

The Deep Sets architecture %~\cite{Zaheer2017} 
is a permutation-invariant network designed to
operate on unordered sets of objects. A shared transformation $\Phi$ is applied independently
to each object, and the resulting representations are aggregated via a permutation-invariant
operation such as a sum or mean, before being passed to a final network $F$. This is
well-motivated for jet inputs, where the tracks and vertices within a jet have no natural
ordering.
