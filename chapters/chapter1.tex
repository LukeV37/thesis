\section{Extremely Brief History of Particle Physics}

% Paragraph 1 -- Quantum Foundations
\begin{itemize}
  \item Classical physics fails to explain blackbody radiation and the photoelectric effect
  \item Planck and Einstein introduce quantization of energy
  \item Bohr model, de Broglie wave-particle duality, Heisenberg uncertainty principle, Schrodinger equation
  \item Dirac equation (1928) unifies QM with special relativity, predicts antimatter
  \item Anderson confirms the positron (1932), validating quantum field theory
\end{itemize}

% Paragraph 2 -- From Particle Zoo to the Quark Model
\begin{itemize}
  \item Accelerators produce a flurry of hadrons ``particle zoo''
  \item Gell-Mann's Eightfold Way (1961) organizes hadrons via SU(3) symmetry
  \item Predicts the $\Omega^-$ baryon as last missing piece, confirmed experimentally in 1964
  \item Quark model (Gell-Mann and Zweig, 1964) explains hadrons as composites of up, down, and strange quarks
\end{itemize}

% Paragraph 3 -- The Standard Model
\begin{itemize}
  \item QED (Feynman, Schwinger, Tomonaga, 1940s--50s) first complete quantum field theory, agrees with experiment to extraordinary precision
  \item Electroweak unification (Glashow, Weinberg, Salam, 1967--68) and QCD (Gross, Politzer, Wilczek, 1973) complete the Standard Model
  \item Higgs mechanism (Brout, Englert, Higgs, 1964) accounts for electroweak symmetry breaking and particle masses
  \item W and Z bosons (CERN, 1983), top quark (Tevatron, 1995), Higgs boson (LHC, 2012) confirm the SM experimentally
  \item SM is remarkably successful yet leaves open questions (dark matter, gravity, matter-antimatter asymmetry), motivating continued LHC program
\end{itemize}

\section{Collider Experiments}

% Paragraph 1 -- Brief History
\begin{itemize}
  \item Cyclotron (Lawrence, 1930) -- first practical particle accelerator, fixed-target
  \item LEP (CERN, 1989--2000) -- $e^+e^-$ collider, precision electroweak measurements, W and Z boson properties
  \item Tevatron (Fermilab, 1987--2011) -- $p\bar{p}$ collider at 1.96 TeV, top quark discovery (1995), immediate predecessor to LHC
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{The Large Hadron Collider}

The Large Hadron Collider (LHC)%~\cite{Evans:2008zzb}
is a proton--proton synchrotron
operating at CERN, near Geneva, Switzerland, and is currently the highest-energy particle
collider ever constructed. The primary physics motivation for the LHC was the experimental
confirmation of the Brout--Englert--Higgs mechanism of electroweak symmetry breaking, the
discovery of which was announced in July 2012 by the ATLAS and CMS
collaborations%~\cite{HIGG-2012-27, CMS-HIG-12-028}
. Beyond the Higgs boson, the LHC
program pursues a broad range of open questions in the Standard Model and beyond,
including searches for supersymmetric partners, dark matter candidates, and precision
measurements of the top quark and gauge bosons.

% Paragraph -- HL-LHC + FCC mention

  \item Requires significant detector and trigger upgrades across all four experiments (ITk, HGTD, etc.)

  \item Beyond HL-LHC: the Future Circular Collider (FCC) proposes a 100\,km ring at CERN capable of reaching 100\,TeV, representing the next energy frontier
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Likelihood Fits}

% Paragraph 2 -- Likelihood Fits and Signal Strength
\begin{itemize}
  \item Signal strength $\mu$ is the parameter of interest: $\mu=0$ is background-only, $\mu=1$ is the SM prediction
  \item Likelihood function $\mathcal{L}(\mu,\theta)$ is the product of Poisson terms over histogram bins, comparing data to signal+background prediction
  \item $\mu$ is extracted by maximising $\mathcal{L}$
  \item Nuisance parameters $\theta$ encode systematic uncertainties (e.g. jet energy scale, luminosity); constrained by Gaussian terms in the likelihood
\end{itemize}

In order to measure signal strength, $\mu$, we perform a statistical procedure to match the theoretical modeling, MC, to real world data. A likelihood function $\mathcal{L}(\mu,\theta)$ is constructed as the product of Poisson probability terms over the bins of the input distributions where $x_i$ is data counted in bin i and $s_i$ and $b_i$ are signal and background predictions, respectively. The parameter of interest (POI), or signal strength $\mu$, is extracted by maximising the likelihood function $\mathcal{L}$. The effects of systematic uncertainties are quantified as $\theta$, also called Nuissance Parameters NP, and are often assumed to be constrained by gaussian distributions. $\alpha_j$ is known as the global measurement data. Usually assumed mean=0 and var=1. The likelihood fit function is shown below.

\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{L}(\mu,\theta|x)=\prod_i Pois(x_i|\mu \cdot s_i(\theta)+b_i(\theta)) \prod_j C(\alpha_j|\theta_j)
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Significance and Limits}

% Paragraph 3 -- Significance and Limits
\begin{itemize}
  \item Frequentist hypothesis testing: null hypothesis $H_0$ (background-only) vs.\ alternative hypothesis $H_1$ (signal+background)
  \item Profile likelihood ratio test statistic $q_\mu$ used to distinguish the two hypotheses
  \item p-value: probability of observing a result at least as extreme as the data under $H_0$; $5\sigma$ ($p < 2.87\times10^{-7}$) is the threshold for discovery in HEP
  \item When no excess is observed, set a 95\% confidence level (CL) upper limit on $\mu$ to exclude signal models
  \item The three-panel figure (signal strength, significance, and 95\% CL limit scanned vs.\ Higgs mass) illustrates all three outcomes simultaneously
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Neural Networks}

% Paragraph 4 -- Neural Networks as Classifiers
\begin{itemize}
  \item Modern HEP analyses use DNNs as signal-vs-background classifiers to maximally separate signal from background
  \item Artificial neuron: weighted sum of inputs passed through a non-linear activation function (e.g.\ ReLU)
  \item Deep networks stack many layers to learn hierarchical, non-linear representations of input features
  \item Trained via stochastic gradient descent and back-propagation to minimise a binary cross-entropy loss
  \item NN output score (discriminant) is used as the histogram input to the likelihood fit, connecting classification directly to the statistical analysis
\end{itemize}

Modern HEP analyses rely on deep neural networks (DNNs) as signal-vs-background classifiers. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:NN}, the fundamental unit is an artificial neuron, which computes a weighted sum of its inputs and passes the result through a non-linear activation function to produce a scalar output. By stacking many such layers in sequence, a DNN learns hierarchical representations of the input data and, given sufficient depth and width, can approximate any function%~\cite{Hornik1989}. In HEP, a DNN is typically trained via stochastic gradient descent to minimise a binary cross-entropy loss, producing an output score that discriminates signal from background. This score then serves as the histogram input to the likelihood fit described above, directly connecting the classifier to the statistical analysis.
