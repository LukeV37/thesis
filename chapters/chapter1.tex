\section{Extremely Brief History of Particle Physics}

Before particle physics, there was quantum mechanics, and before quantum mechanics, there was classical mechanics. Classical mechanics is intuitive, but in the words of Richard Feynman, "I think that I can safely say that nobody understands quantum mechanics" (1964, The Character of Physical Law, Lecture 6). The classical framework began to unravel in 1900 when Planck showed that quantizing the energy emitted from a black body resolved the ultraviolet catastrophe~\cite{Planck1901}. Einstein reinforced this picture in 1905 by using quantization to explain the photoelectric effect~\cite{Einstein1905}. A quantum revolution followed: the Bohr model of the hydrogen atom (1913), wave-particle duality with de Broglie (1924), the Schr\"{o}dinger equation (1926), and the Heisenberg uncertainty principle (1927). Dirac unified quantum mechanics with special relativity in 1928 and, as a consequence, predicted antimatter~\cite{Dirac1928}. The positron was discovered by Anderson in 1932, and particle physics was here to stay~\cite{Anderson1933}.

% Einstein1905: https://doi.org/10.1002/andp.19053220607

% Anderson1933: https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRev.43.491

As particle accelerators improved through the mid-twentieth century, beginning with Lawrence's cyclotron in 1932~\cite{Lawrence1932}, experimental discoveries were abundant. So many new composite particles, known as hadrons, were being found that the era became known as the ``particle zoo.'' Gell-Mann organized these hadrons according to an underlying SU(3) symmetry, an approach known as the Eightfold Way~\cite{GellMann1961}, which predicted the existence of the $\Omega^-$ baryon before its experimental confirmation in 1964~\cite{Barnes1964}. This underlying symmetry gave rise to the quark model, explaining hadrons as composite states of up, down, and strange quarks~\cite{GellMann1964,Zweig1964}.

% GellMann1961: https://inspirehep.net/literature/44998

% GellMann1964: https://inspirehep.net/literature/11880
% Zweig1964: https://cds.cern.ch/record/352337

Meanwhile, Feynman, Schwinger, and Tomonaga were developing Quantum Electrodynamics (QED), the first complete quantum field theory describing the interaction of photons and leptons with extraordinary experimental precision. Building on QED, electroweak unification by Glashow, Weinberg, and Salam~\cite{Glashow1961,Weinberg1967,Salam1968} and Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) by Gross, Politzer, and Wilczek~\cite{Gross1973,Politzer1973} together completed the Standard Model (SM). The last missing piece was the Higgs mechanism, proposed by Brout, Englert, and Higgs in 1964 to account for electroweak symmetry breaking and to give particles mass~\cite{EnglertBrout1964,Higgs1964}. The W and Z bosons were confirmed experimentally at CERN in 1983~\cite{UA1W1983,UA1Z1983}, the top quark was discovered at the Tevatron in 1995~\cite{CDF1995}, and the Higgs boson was confirmed by the LHC in 2012~\cite{ATLAS-Higgs2012,CMS-Higgs2012}. The SM is remarkably successful in describing the fundamental constituents of matter and their interactions, yet it fails to account for dark matter, gravity, and the observed matter-antimatter asymmetry, motivating the continued LHC program.

\section{Collider Experiments}

The development of collider experiments through the latter half of the twentieth century was driven by the need to probe matter at ever smaller scales and higher energies. The Large Electron-Positron Collider (LEP), operating at CERN from 1989 to 2000, collided electrons and positrons at centre-of-mass energies up to 209\,GeV and delivered precision measurements of the W and Z boson properties, providing stringent tests of the electroweak sector of the SM~\cite{LEP2006}. The Tevatron at Fermilab, a proton-antiproton collider operating at $\sqrt{s} = 1.96$\,TeV from 1987 to 2011, pushed the energy frontier further and notably achieved the discovery of the top quark in 1995~\cite{CDF1995}. These experiments established the experimental and technological foundations upon which the Large Hadron Collider was built.
% LEP2006: https://arxiv.org/abs/hep-ex/0509008

\subsubsection{The Large Hadron Collider}

The Large Hadron Collider (LHC)%~\cite{Evans:2008zzb}
is a proton--proton synchrotron operating at CERN, near Geneva, Switzerland, and is currently the highest-energy particle collider ever constructed. The primary physics motivation for the LHC was the experimental confirmation of the Brout--Englert--Higgs mechanism of electroweak symmetry breaking, the discovery of which was announced in July 2012 by the ATLAS and CMS collaborations%~\cite{HIGG-2012-27, CMS-HIG-12-028}
. Beyond the Higgs boson, the LHC program pursues a broad range of open questions in the Standard Model and beyond, including searches for supersymmetric partners, dark matter candidates, and precision measurements of the top quark and gauge bosons.

The CERN accelerator complex, illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:CERN_complex}, feeds two counter-rotating proton beams through a series of pre-accelerators before injecting them into the 26.7\,km LHC ring. The beams are brought to collision at four interaction points, each housing a dedicated experiment. ATLAS and CMS are general-purpose detectors designed to study the full range of LHC physics, from Higgs boson measurements to searches for new phenomena. ALICE is optimized for heavy-ion collisions, studying the quark-gluon plasma produced at extreme temperatures and densities. LHCb is a forward spectrometer dedicated to precision measurements of b-hadron decays and studies of CP violation.

The LHC has operated across three run periods of increasing energy. Run~1 (2010--2012) collected data at $\sqrt{s} = 7$ and 8\,TeV, culminating in the Higgs boson discovery. Run~2 (2015--2018) operated at $\sqrt{s} = 13$\,TeV, significantly expanding the physics reach. Run~3, which began in 2022, operates at $\sqrt{s} = 13.6$\,TeV and continues to accumulate the largest dataset to date.

\subsubsection{The High-Luminosity LHC and Future Colliders}

Collider experiments must collect large datasets to reduce the statistical uncertainty of rare physics processes. These colliders set the rate of collisions to produce a key figure of merit called instantaneous luminosity $\mathcal{L}$. The integrated luminosity, $\int \mathcal{L}\,dt$, sets the total size of the dataset. The High-Luminosity LHC (HL-LHC) is a major upgrade of the LHC program targeting a tenfold increase in instantaneous luminosity over the nominal LHC design, with the goal of collecting 3000\,fb$^{-1}$ of integrated luminosity by the mid-2040s~\cite{Zerlauth:2024PD}. The LHC and HL-LHC run schedule is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:LHC_schedule}.

The primary challenge of the HL-LHC is the significant increase in pileup, with an average of $\langle\mu\rangle \approx 200$ simultaneous proton-proton interactions per bunch crossing, compared to $\langle\mu\rangle \approx 60$ during Run~3. This is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:PhoenixDisplay}, which shows the dramatic increase in track density in the ATLAS inner detector between these two conditions. To cope with this environment, all four LHC experiments require significant detector and trigger upgrades. For ATLAS, this includes the replacement of the inner tracker with the all-silicon Inner Tracker (ITk) and the addition of the High-Granularity Timing Detector (HGTD) to provide precise timing information that separates pileup vertices.

\section{Statistical Methods in High Energy Physics}

% https://atlas.web.cern.ch/Atlas/GROUPS/PHYSICS/PAPERS/HIGG-2012-27/
% https://cds.cern.ch/record/1471016/plots

Measurements in experimental high energy physics are fundamentally counting experiments. Collision data is collected and sorted into histograms of some discriminating variable, and the observed counts in each bin are compared to predictions from Monte Carlo (MC) simulations of signal and background processes. A signal process typically appears as a localized excess over a smoothly varying background, a search strategy often referred to as ``bump hunting.'' A classic example is the $H \to \gamma\gamma$ diphoton mass spectrum, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:higgs_discovery}, where both ATLAS and CMS observed a narrow peak near $m_{\gamma\gamma} = 125$\,GeV over a smooth background in 2012, providing the experimental confirmation of the Higgs boson.

\subsubsection{Likelihood Fits}

To extract physics results from these histograms, a likelihood fit is performed to match the MC prediction to the observed data, as shown in the equation below:
%
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{L}(\mu,\theta \mid x) = \prod_i \mathrm{Pois}\!\left(x_i \mid \mu \cdot s_i(\theta) + b_i(\theta)\right) \prod_j C(\alpha_j \mid \theta_j)
\end{equation}
%
The parameter of interest (POI) is the signal strength $\mu$, defined such that $\mu = 0$ corresponds to the background-only hypothesis and $\mu = 1$ corresponds to the Standard Model signal prediction. The signal and background predictions $s_i(\theta)$ and $b_i(\theta)$ in each bin $i$ depend on the nuisance parameters $\theta$ because systematic uncertainties can modify both the overall normalization and the shape of the predicted distributions. For example, a jet energy scale uncertainty shifts reconstructed energies, moving events between histogram bins. The signal strength is extracted by maximizing $\mathcal{L}$ over both $\mu$ and $\theta$. Each nuisance parameter $\theta_j$ is constrained by an auxiliary observable $\alpha_j$, which represents the result of an independent control measurement. The constraint term $C(\alpha_j \mid \theta_j)$ is the likelihood of observing $\alpha_j$ given $\theta_j$, and is typically modeled as a Gaussian with $\alpha_j = 0$ at the nominal value and unit variance, penalizing large deviations from the central value.



\subsubsection{Significance and Limits}

Once the likelihood fit is performed, the results are interpreted using frequentist hypothesis testing. Two competing hypotheses are considered: the null hypothesis $H_0$, which assumes only background processes are present, and the alternative hypothesis $H_1$, which assumes a signal is present on top of the background. A statistical test is performed to either accept the null hypothesis or reject it. Importantly, in frequentist statistics we never directly accept the alternative hypothesis, therefore, rejecting $H_0$ simply means the data are inconsistent with background alone, not that $H_1$ is proven to be true.

The desired outcome of a search is either a discovery or an exclusion limit. A discovery is claimed when the data show a significant excess over the background-only prediction, quantified by the $p$-value — the probability of observing a result at least as extreme as the data under $H_0$. In particle physics, the threshold for discovery is set at $5\sigma$, corresponding to $p < 2.87 \times 10^{-7}$, an intentionally stringent standard given the history of false excesses in the field. An intermediate threshold of $3\sigma$ is used to indicate evidence for discovery. When no significant excess is observed, an upper limit on the signal strength $\mu$ is set at 95\% confidence level (CL). This limit defines the maximum signal contribution still consistent with the data, and is used to exclude signal models that predict a larger yield than what is observed. A limit is therefore not a null result. It is a quantitative statement that constrains the parameter space of new physics models.

\subsubsection{Neural Networks}

Modern HEP analyses increasingly rely on deep neural networks (DNNs) as signal-vs-background classifiers. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:NN}, the fundamental unit is an artificial neuron, which computes a weighted sum of its inputs and passes the result through a non-linear activation function to produce a scalar output. By stacking many such neurons across multiple layers, a DNN can learn complex, non-linear relationships in the input data and, given sufficient depth and width, can approximate any function~\cite{Hornik1989}.

In HEP, a DNN is typically trained on simulated signal and background MC samples using stochastic gradient descent algorithms to minimize a binary cross-entropy loss. The trained network produces a continuous output score between 0 and 1, where signal-like events are pushed toward 1 and background-like events toward 0. This discriminant score is then histogrammed and passed into the likelihood fit described above, translating the classifier's separation power into a physics measurement.
