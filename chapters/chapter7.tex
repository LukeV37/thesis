\section{The Top Quark and Spin Correlations}

The top quark is the heaviest known elementary particle, with a mass of $m_t \approx 173$~GeV~\cite{PDG2022}. Its large mass corresponds to a Yukawa coupling close to unity, making it the SM fermion most strongly coupled to the Higgs field. At the LHC, top quark pairs are produced predominantly through gluon-gluon fusion with a cross section of approximately 830~pb at $\sqrt{s} = 13$~TeV~\cite{Czakon:2014xsa}. The top quark decays to $Wb$ with a branching fraction near 100\%, giving rise to fully hadronic, semi-leptonic, and dileptonic final states. The top quark decays before it can bind into a hadronic state, as its lifetime $\tau_t \sim 5 \times 10^{-25}$~s is shorter than the QCD hadronization timescale by roughly an order of magnitude. As a result, its spin information is transferred directly to its decay products, making the top quark the only quark whose spin state is experimentally accessible.

The $t\bar{t}$ spin system can be treated as a two-qubit quantum state, with the spin density matrix encoding the full quantum state of the pair~\cite{Afik:2020onf}. Deviations from SM predictions in the spin correlations would signal new physics, and the system provides a unique probe of quantum information at energy scales far beyond any previous test. These correlations are observable as angular correlations between the decay products of the two top quarks. Near the $t\bar{t}$ production threshold, the spin correlations are strong enough that the system is quantum-entangled. Recent measurements by ATLAS and CMS have directly observed this entanglement~\cite{ATLAS:2023fsd,CMS:2024pts}, marking the first observation of quantum entanglement at a hadron collider.

The spin correlations are characterized by the angle $\theta$ between a decay product direction and the top quark polarization direction. The analyzing power $\alpha$ quantifies the sensitivity of a given decay product to the top quark spin. The charged lepton has $\alpha = 1$, while in hadronic top decays the down-type quark from the $W$ decay has $\alpha_d \approx 1.0$ and the bottom quark has $\alpha_b \approx 0.4$~\cite{PDG2022}. The angle $\cos\theta$ is computed via a two-step boost: the top quark unit vector $\hat{p}_{t}^{t\bar{t}}$ is evaluated in the $t\bar{t}$ CM frame, and the spin analyzer quark unit vector $\hat{p}_{q}^{t}$ is evaluated in the top quark rest frame:
\begin{equation}
	\cos\theta = \hat{p}_{t}^{t\bar{t}} \cdot \hat{p}_{q}^{t},
	\label{eq:costheta}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{p}_{t}^{t\bar{t}}$ receives a single boost to the CM frame, and $\hat{p}_{q}^{t}$ receives two sequential boosts: first to the CM frame, then to the top quark rest frame.

\section{Event Simulation and Reconstruction}
\label{sec:analysis}

This analysis targets the semi-leptonic channel, $pp \rightarrow t\bar{t} \rightarrow \ell^{\pm}\nu 2b2j$, where one $W$ boson decays leptonically and the other hadronically. The leptonic side provides a clean trigger through the charged lepton and missing transverse energy. The hadronic top quark decay is captured in a single boosted large-$R$ jet, which contains the full hadronic decay. This rich substructure is the primary target for the regression model. An inclusive phase space is targeted to maximize available statistics.

Three samples of semi-leptonic $t\bar{t}$ events are generated with MadGraph5 at $\sqrt{s} = 14$~TeV. An unpolarized sample is used for training. Left- and right-handed polarized samples are used for evaluation, generated with the following syntax:

\begin{center}
\begin{BVerbatim}
generate p p > t t~ > b j j , t~ > b~ l- vl~
generate p p > t{L} t~ > b j j , t~ > b~ l- vl~
generate p p > t{R} t~ > b j j , t~ > b~ l- vl~
\end{BVerbatim}
\end{center}

A generator-level requirement of $p_{T,top} > 200$~GeV is applied to simulate a boosted topology. Pythia8 is used for parton showering and hadronization. Generator-level validation is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:gen_val}. The characteristic asymmetry in $\cos\theta$ between the left- and right-handed samples confirms the correct implementation of top quark polarization.

Events are reconstructed by first identifying the lepton and neutrino from the anti-top decay. The lepton is required to have $p_T > 30$~GeV and $|\eta| < 3$, and must be isolated with no final state particles within $\Delta R < 0.1$. The neutrino is identified via missing transverse energy with MET $> 30$~GeV. Both are removed before jet clustering. Large-$R$ jets are clustered using the Cambridge-Aachen algorithm with $R = 1.5$, and must satisfy $p_T > 250$~GeV and $|\eta| < 3$. Exactly one large-$R$ jet is required to suppress ISR/FSR contamination. The large-$R$ jet constituents are then removed, and the remaining hadronic activity is clustered into small-$R$ jets using the anti-$k_t$ algorithm with $R = 0.4$. At least one $b$-tagged small-$R$ jet is required with $p_T > 30$~GeV, $|\eta| < 3$, and $0.3 < \Delta R(b\text{-jet}, \ell) < 3.0$. Large-$R$ jet constituents are required to have $p_T > 400$~MeV and be charged, to simulate detector conditions. The full event selection and event yields are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:cutflow}.

Computing $\cos\theta$ requires two boosted unit vectors: the top quark direction $\hat{p}_t^{t\bar{t}}$ in the $t\bar{t}$ CM frame, and the spin analyzer quark direction $\hat{p}_q^t$ in the top quark rest frame. Both are predicted directly from reconstructed event objects using a Transformer regression model. Prior work on hadronic top polarimetry~\cite{Dong_2025} used a ParticleNet-based model applied to a restricted boosted phase space, and reconstructed the hadronic $W$ decay through explicit subjet declustering into three distinct subjets within the large-$R$ jet, highly suppressing statistics. This work avoids explicit subjet reconstruction by processing the full large-$R$ jet substructure directly, and targets a more inclusive phase space to maximize the number of usable events.

The event is encoded as a set of particle-level objects: the large-$R$ jet constituents, lepton, missing transverse energy, and small-$R$ jets, padded to a fixed maximum size for batched training. The Transformer architecture is a natural choice for set-structured input of this kind. The self-attention mechanism learns correlations across all objects simultaneously, without requiring a fixed input geometry, and naturally exploits the point-cloud substructure of the large-$R$ jet constituents, analogous to Transformer-based flavor taggers processing sets of tracks.

Two separate regression targets are defined. The top quark direction in the $t\bar{t}$ CM frame is predicted as a four-vector and trained with MSE loss. The spin analyzer quark direction in the top quark rest frame is predicted as a unit three-vector and trained with cosine similarity loss. MSE is not well suited to unit vector regression: it measures straight-line distance in Cartesian space rather than angle, and does not respect the geometry of the sphere. Cosine similarity directly minimizes the angle between the predicted and true directions on the unit sphere, with no such artifact. This is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:unit_sphere}.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.3\linewidth]{figures/chapter7/unit_sphere.png}
	\caption{Illustration of the cosine similarity loss on the unit sphere. The model is trained to minimize the angle between the truth direction $A_1$ and the predicted direction $A_2$. All directions map continuously onto the sphere, avoiding edge defects.}
	\label{fig:unit_sphere}
\end{figure}

The model is trained on an unpolarized $t\bar{t}$ sample spanning the full phase space after event selection. Two distinct effects limit the spin information available in a given event. First, some large-$R$ jets only partially capture the hadronic decay, reducing the substructure information available to the model. Second, and more fundamentally, the strength of $t\bar{t}$ spin correlations depends on the production kinematics. Near the production threshold, spin correlations are strongest and the $\cos\theta$ distribution is most asymmetric. Far from threshold, correlations weaken and the $\cos\theta$ distribution approaches uniformity regardless of reconstruction quality. The inclusive phase space therefore contains a mixture of events with varying spin sensitivity. The cosine similarity between the predicted and true quark directions provides a per-event quality score that reflects both effects: events with high cosine similarity form a purer subset in which the spin signal is recoverable, as examined in Section~\ref{sec:results}.
