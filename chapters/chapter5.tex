\section{Motivation for \texorpdfstring{$H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$}{H→bb} and \texorpdfstring{$H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$}{H→cc} Measurements at the LHC}

The measurement of Higgs boson Yukawa couplings to fermions provides a direct test of the mass-generation mechanism in the Standard Model, where fermion masses arise from Yukawa interactions with the Higgs field. Among all Higgs decay modes, $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ has the largest branching fraction, $\mathcal{B}(H \rightarrow b\bar{b}) = 58.2 \pm 0.7\%$ for $m_H = 125$ GeV, making it one of the most experimentally accessible. The $b$-quark Yukawa coupling is also one of the most theoretically important to constrain precisely because it drives the dominant contribution to the total Higgs decay width, meaning any BSM modification would have broad consequences for the Higgs sector. The $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ decay, with $\mathcal{B}(H \rightarrow c\bar{c}) = 2.9\%$, presents a far more difficult experimental target. Charm jets are intrinsically harder to distinguish from light-flavor jets than $b$-jets, owing to the relatively short decay lengths and lower masses of charm hadrons. Nevertheless, recent advances in flavor tagging have enabled sufficiently precise charm jet identification to probe the charm Yukawa coupling, which serves as a sensitive proxy for new physics. Many BSM models allow $\kappa_c$ to be enhanced well beyond its SM value, making a direct upper limit experimentally valuable even in the absence of a discovery.

Despite having a smaller production cross section than the VBF mode, the VH channel has yielded the most sensitive ATLAS measurements of both $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ and $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ to date, owing to the powerful background suppression afforded by the leptonic trigger strategy.

\section{Associated Higgs Production with Vector Boson Decaying to B/C Quarks}

The associated production of the Higgs boson with a vector boson ($VH$, $V = W, Z$) provides a uniquely clean environment for studying hadronic Higgs decays. The leptonic decay of the vector boson provides the critical trigger handle that suppresses QCD multijet backgrounds and makes both $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ and $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ experimentally accessible. The $W$ boson is reconstructed via its decays to an electron, muon, or $\tau$-lepton and a neutrino, while the $Z$ boson is reconstructed from electron or muon pairs, or identified through large missing transverse energy in the $Z \rightarrow \nu\nu$ case. These signatures define the 0-, 1-, and 2-lepton analysis channels, and the analysis uses 140 fb$^{-1}$ of proton-proton collision data at $\sqrt{s} = 13$ TeV collected by the ATLAS detector during Run 2. The dominant residual backgrounds are $t\bar{t}$ and $V$+jets production, which are separated from signal using Boosted Decision Trees (BDTs) trained on kinematic and flavor-tagging variables. Events are further categorized by lepton multiplicity, vector boson $p_\mathrm{T}$, and BDT score, and the analysis ultimately constrains the coupling modifiers $\kappa_b$ and $\kappa_c$ and their ratio $|\kappa_c/\kappa_b|$ within the $\kappa$-framework.

\subsection{Fit Regions}

The global likelihood fit comprises 59 signal regions, with 27 targeting $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ and 32 targeting $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$, and 97 control regions, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:VH_regions}. The $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ signal regions require both Higgs candidate jets to be b-tagged using the DL1r algorithm at the 70\% b-jet efficiency working point, while the $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ signal regions require no b-tagged jets and at least one c-tagged jet. C-tagging is performed using the DL1r c-jet discriminant with two working points: a loose working point ($C_L$) at 45\% c-jet efficiency and a tight working point ($C_T$) at 25\% c-jet efficiency. This gives three tagging configurations for the $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ signal regions: tight-tight ($C_T C_T$), tight-loose ($C_T C_L$), and tight-non-tagged ($C_T N$), where $N$ denotes a non-tagged jet. Loose-non-tagged ($C_L N$) events are used exclusively as control regions. Signal regions are binned in BDT score to maximally separate signal from background, while control regions constrain the floating normalization factors for the dominant $t\bar{t}$ and $V$+jets backgrounds. The analysis is further segmented into multiple $p_\mathrm{T}^V$ intervals across all three lepton channels, providing additional sensitivity to the signal across a wide kinematic range in both boosted and resolved cases.

I joined this analysis at a mature stage, with the majority of signal and control regions already finalized. Under the guidance of Yanhui Ma, a postdoc in our group, I studied the impact of including a new low $p_\mathrm{T}$ signal region, $75~\text{GeV} < p_\mathrm{T}^V < 150~\text{GeV}$, in the single-lepton CC channel. A key challenge in this region was that the $C_T N$ and $C_L N$ tagging configurations have very high event statistics but low signal-to-background ratios, causing large pulls on the experimental uncertainties in the fit. These configurations were therefore removed from the low $p_\mathrm{T}$ region to stabilize the fit model. However, removing these regions left insufficient constraining power to freely float the $W$+lf ($W$ boson produced in association with light-flavor jets) normalization factor in the $75~\text{GeV} < p_\mathrm{T}^V < 150~\text{GeV}$ interval. Instead, the $W$+lf normalization was assigned a 20\% Gaussian prior for this $p_\mathrm{T}^V$ bin, while remaining freely floating at higher $p_\mathrm{T}^V$. Both the $W$+lf and $W$+mf ($W$ boson produced in association with medium-flavor, charm jets) normalization factors were decorrelated by jet multiplicity and $p_\mathrm{T}^V$ for the low $p_\mathrm{T}$ region. With the $W$+lf prior in place, the constraint on the freely floating $W$+mf normalization improved significantly, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:NP_FloatNorm}. These changes ultimately helped constrain the background modeling and improve the overall sensitivity to signal.

Both results are in good agreement with the Standard Model prediction. The $VZ, Z \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ process is observed with a significance greater than 10 standard deviations, while the $VZ, Z \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ process yields an observed (expected) significance of 5.2 (5.3) standard deviations, marking the first observation of this process by ATLAS. The inclusion of the new low $p_\mathrm{T}$ single-lepton region was a crucial contribution to achieving this $5\sigma$ threshold.

With the methodology validated through the VZ channel, the final unblinded VH measurement yields signal strengths of:
\begin{align}
	\mu^{bb}_{VH}&=0.92^{+0.16}_{-0.15}=0.92 \pm 0.10 \, \text{(Stat)}^{+0.13}_{-0.11}\text{(Syst)} \\
	\mu^{cc}_{VH}&=1.0^{+5.4}_{-5.2}=1.0^{+4.0}_{-3.9} \, \text{(Stat)}^{+3.7}_{-3.5}\text{(Syst)}

The combined $VH, H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ measurement has an observed (expected) significance of 7.4 (8.0) standard deviations. Individually, the $WH, H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ and $ZH, H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ processes have observed (expected) significances of 5.3 (5.5) and 4.9 (5.6) standard deviations respectively, marking the first observation of the $WH, H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ process. For the $VH, H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ process, the observed (expected) upper limit at 95\% confidence level is 11.5 (10.6) times the Standard Model prediction, corresponding to a constraint on the charm Yukawa coupling modifier of $|\kappa_c| < 4.2$ at 95\% CL.

\section{Vector Boson Fusion Higgs Production Decaying to B/C Quarks}

The vector-boson fusion (VBF) production mode has the second-largest Higgs boson production cross section at the LHC, approximately 3.8 pb at $\sqrt{s} = 13$ TeV, significantly larger than the VH production mode. However, unlike VH, the VBF process is fully hadronic. The two initial-state quarks each radiate a virtual vector boson that fuses to produce the Higgs boson, with the scattered quarks fragmenting into two energetic forward jets with large pseudorapidity separation and high dijet invariant mass. With the Higgs boson also decaying hadronically into either $b\bar{b}$ or $c\bar{c}$, the entire final state consists of jets, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:VBF_Diagrams}, making non-resonant QCD multijet production the overwhelming dominant background at approximately 99\% of the total. Unlike the VH channel, where the leptonic decay of the associated vector boson provides an immediate trigger handle that suppresses QCD backgrounds, the VBF channel has no such leptonic foothold. This makes the VBF analysis considerably more challenging and necessitates a dedicated strategy to extract the Higgs signal from an enormous QCD background.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/chapter5/VBF_Signal_and_Background.png}
	\caption{Representative Feynman diagrams for the VBF $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ signal process (left) and a QCD multijet background process with gluon splitting to a $b\bar{b}$ pair (right).}
	\label{fig:VBF_Diagrams}
\end{figure}

A key enabling development for this analysis is a new inclusive VBF trigger, deployed in ATLAS partway through Run 2 in 2018, which exploits the large dijet invariant mass and rapidity separation characteristic of the VBF topology. Unlike the previous $b$-tagging-based trigger, this inclusive trigger is agnostic to jet flavor, making it the first trigger capable of selecting VBF $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ candidates. The $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ search uses data collected at two center-of-mass energies: 37.5 fb$^{-1}$ at $\sqrt{s} = 13$ TeV from part of the 2018 Run-2 period, and 51.5 fb$^{-1}$ at $\sqrt{s} = 13.6$ TeV during 2022--2023 in Run 3, for a total of 89.0 fb$^{-1}$. The $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ measurement uses only the Run-3 dataset, allowing a direct combination with the previous Run-2 VBF $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ result. Key improvements over previous work include enhanced multivariate techniques and the adoption of the GN2 transformer-based flavor tagger, described in Chapter~4, which provides significantly better $b$- and $c$-jet identification than the DL1r algorithm used previously.

\subsection{Event Simulation and Selection}

Simulated VBF signal events are generated at NLO in QCD and ggF events are generated at NNLO using POWHEG BOX v2. PYTHIA 8 is used for parton showering and hadronization. Although ggF can produce events with a VBF-like topology via gluon radiation into forward jets, its kinematic distribution closely resembles that of the background, resulting in weak sensitivity to the ggF contribution specifically. The signal-strength modifiers $\mu_{bb}$ and $\mu_{cc}$ are therefore defined inclusively, scaling both VBF and ggF contributions together. The dominant background, non-resonant QCD multijet production, accounts for approximately 99\% of the total and is estimated using a data-driven method, as MC simulation of QCD multijet processes lacks the accuracy needed for reliable background modeling. The subdominant $V$+jets background is modeled using simulation, with QCD and electroweak components generated using MadGraph5\_aMC@NLO and SHERPA respectively. Top-quark pair ($t\bar{t}$) production is modeled with POWHEG BOX v2 interfaced with PYTHIA 8. Other Higgs production modes are not considered due to their negligible contributions.

Jets are reconstructed using the particle-flow algorithm, which improves energy resolution by combining inner-detector tracks with calorimeter energy deposits before clustering with the anti-$k_t$ algorithm ($R = 0.4$). To suppress pileup jets, jet-vertex-tagging (JVT) uses track-based variables to identify jets originating from the primary hard-scatter vertex. Jets within the tracker acceptance ($|\eta| < 2.5$) are required to have $p_T > 20$ GeV, while forward jets ($|\eta| > 2.5$), which rely more heavily on calorimeter information, must satisfy $p_T > 30$ GeV.

The inclusive VBF trigger selects events containing at least two jets satisfying $p_T > 70$ GeV ($|\eta| < 3.2$) and $p_T > 50$ GeV ($|\eta| < 4.9$), with additional dijet requirements of $|\Delta\eta_{jj}| > 4$, $|\Delta\phi_{jj}| < 2$, and $m_{jj} > 1000$ (1100) GeV for Run 3 (Run 2). To ensure full trigger efficiency, the offline selection imposes stricter requirements on the two VBF candidate jets: both must satisfy $p_T > 75$ GeV, with the leading jet restricted to $|\eta| < 3.2$ and the subleading jet to $|\eta| < 4.5$, and the dijet system must satisfy $m_{jj} > 1200$ GeV. If multiple jet pairs pass these requirements, the one with the largest $m_{jj}$ is retained.

The remaining jets, not assigned as VBF candidates, are used to form the Higgs candidate jet pair ($j_{h1}$, $j_{h2}$) from the $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ or $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ decay. Both jets must have $p_T > 20$ GeV and $|\eta| < 2.5$, consistent with the inner detector acceptance. To avoid sculpting the background dijet invariant-mass distribution, requirements of $p_{T,h1h2} > 150$ GeV and $|\Delta\phi_{h1h2}| < 2$ are also applied. If multiple pairs qualify, the one with the highest $p_{T,h1h2}$ is chosen, as summarised in Figure~\ref{fig:VBF:cutflow}.

	\centering

	\caption{}
	\label{fig:VBF:cutflow}

The two Higgs candidate jets must each be either b-tagged or c-tagged using the GN2 transformer-based flavor tagging algorithm, described in detail in Chapter~4, which achieves significantly improved separation of $b$-jets, $c$-jets, and light-flavor jets compared to the previously used DL1r tagger. For $b$-jet identification, the 77\% $b$-tagging efficiency working point is used, corresponding to $c$-jet and light-flavor jet efficiencies of 6\% and 0.24\%, respectively, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:VBF:ftag_cuts}. Jets failing the $b$-tagging requirement are classified as $c$-tagged if they satisfy the 50\% $c$-tagging efficiency working point, which has $b$-jet and light-flavor jet efficiencies of 13\% and 5.3\%. Two additional tighter working points at 30\% and 10\% $c$-jet efficiency are also evaluated and provide supplementary inputs to the neural network classifier. All efficiency values are derived from simulated $t\bar{t}$ events.

To recover energy lost in semileptonic heavy-flavor decays, the four-momentum of any muon with $p_T > 3$ GeV reconstructed within a $b$- or $c$-tagged jet is added to the jet prior to the muon isolation requirement. For $b$-tagged jets, an additional $p_T$-dependent energy calibration is applied to correct for residual response differences. Finally, events containing isolated electrons or muons are vetoed to ensure orthogonality with the VH analysis channels, enabling a direct statistical combination across the two production modes.

The selected events are split into three analysis channels based on the flavor tagging outcome of the two Higgs candidate jets: a $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ channel using Run-3 data only, and two $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ channels covering Run-2 and Run-3 separately, each requiring both jets to be $c$-tagged. The $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ measurement is restricted to Run-3 data to allow a direct combination with the previous Run-2 VBF $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ analysis, avoiding any overlap in the datasets used.

\subsection{Adversarial Neural Network Training}

Since the QCD multijet background is estimated from data, the ANN is trained using background events drawn from data sidebands outside the Higgs mass window ($100 < M_{h1h2} < 140$ GeV), within the range $50 < M_{h1h2} < 200$ GeV. Without special treatment, the network could trivially exploit the absence of background events in the signal window as a discriminating feature, making the classifier score correlated with $M_{h1h2}$. This would invalidate the data-driven background estimation, which relies on transferring the background $M_{h1h2}$ shape from the low-score control region to the high-score signal regions. To prevent this, an adversarial neural network (ANN) is employed, in which a secondary adversary network is trained simultaneously to penalize any correlation between the classifier output and $M_{h1h2}$. The combined loss function takes the form:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{clf}} - \lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{adv}}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{clf}}$ is the binary cross-entropy (BCE) loss for signal-background classification, $\mathcal{L}_{\text{adv}}$ is the categorical cross-entropy loss of the adversary predicting the $M_{h1h2}$ bin from the classifier output, and $\lambda$ controls the strength of the decorrelation penalty. The $-\lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{adv}}$ term forces the classifier to ``unlearn'' any mass-dependent discrimination: by maximizing the adversary's loss, the classifier is driven towards outputs that carry no information about $M_{h1h2}$. The ANN is trained separately for each analysis channel using PyTorch with the Adam optimizer, taking event and jet kinematics and flavor-tagging information as inputs, as listed in Figure~\ref{fig:ANN_Features}. The classifier output defines six signal regions per channel (SR1--SR6), ordered by decreasing signal purity ranging from 2\% to 0.02\%, with SR6 serving as the control region from which the background shape is derived. The architecture is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:ANN}.

\subsection{Statistical Fit Model}

Signal production rates are extracted from a binned maximum-likelihood fit to the M$_{h1h2}$ distributions in all regions and channels, using 5 GeV bins over the range 50 < M$_{h1h2}$ < 200 GeV, resulting in a total of 30 bins per region. The expected H $\rightarrow$ cc and H $\rightarrow$ bb yields are scaled by independent signal-strength modifiers, $\mu_{cc}$ and $\mu_{bb}$, defined as the ratios of the observed to the SM-expected signal yields. These modifiers are treated as parameters of interest (POIs) and are extracted by maximizing the likelihood.

The analysis employs six signal regions per channel (SR1-SR6), ordered by decreasing signal purity, which ranges from 2\% to 0.02\%. The least pure region, SR6, serves as a control region. The dominant background, non-resonant QCD multijet production, is estimated from data using a data-driven method. The shape of the background in high-score regions (SR1-SR5) is determined from the signal-depleted low-score region (SR6). This approach is motivated by the adversarial neural network design, which aims to decorrelate the reconstructed mass and classifier score.

The background shape in each signal region is modeled using a data-driven approach. For each channel, the background shape in the high-score regions is taken from the signal-depleted low-score region (SR6). This strategy effectively constrains the multijet background shape in the high-score regions since SR6 contains a much larger number of events and is signal-depleted relative to SR1-SR5. The shape is determined by the classifier score distribution in the sidebands of SR6, which is then applied to all high-score regions.

Systematic uncertainties are incorporated through nuisance parameters (NPs). The dominant systematic uncertainties include those from the non-resonant background bias uncertainty and the effects of limited Monte Carlo sample sizes, particularly those from the QCD V+jets samples. Detector-related uncertainties, primarily those associated with jets, are also included. The bias and residual uncertainties are applied to account for potential distortions in the signal extraction due to the neural network training on sideband data.

The residual uncertainties capture potential correlations: for each signal region, an uncertainty is derived from M$_{h1h2}$ sidebands and applied across the full mass range. A fit to the ratio of the M$_{h1h2}$ distributions in the sidebands of SR6 to those in each of SR1-SR5 is performed, where a linear function adequately describes the observed differences; higher-order polynomials were tested but did not provide any significant improvement. The fitted slopes are implemented as unconstrained shape variations in the likelihood, while the offsets are absorbed by the normalization factors.

The bias uncertainties quantify possible distortions of the M$_{h1h2}$ spectrum within the Higgs mass window ($100 < M_{h1h2} < 140 GeV$) that may arise because the ANN is trained only on sideband events and may bias the signal extraction. They are estimated by performing signal-plus-background fits on multijet MC samples, which are treated as data after being reweighted using sideband data information to improve the modelling. The resulting bias in the fitted signal strength, computed as the quadrature sum of the extracted signal yield and its statistical uncertainty, is taken as a systematic uncertainty. The bias amounts to about 27\%-51\% of the expected statistical uncertainty in the H $\rightarrow$ bb signal and 26\%-56\% of that in the H $\rightarrow$ cc signal. Both the bias and residual uncertainties are applied in SR1-SR5, treated as uncorrelated across regions, leading to 15 nuisance parameters for each set.

The measured signal strengths for the H $\rightarrow$ bb and H $\rightarrow$ cc signals are:

\begin{align}
	\mu_{bb} &= 0.97^{+0.57}_{-0.50} = 0.97 \pm 0.45 \, (\text{stat.}) ^{+0.35}_{-0.22} (\text{syst.}) \\
	\mu_{cc} &= 18 \pm 13 = 18 \pm 12 \, (\text{stat.}) \pm 7 \, (\text{syst.})
\end{align}

with a correlation of -4.5\% between the two measurements. The measurement of $\mu_{bb}$ yields an observed (expected) significance of 2.0 (2.1) standard deviations over the background-only prediction. No significant excess over the background-only hypothesis is observed in the search for H $\rightarrow$ cc. An upper limit on $\mu_{cc}$ is calculated using the CLs method, with the profile-likelihood ratio as the test statistic. The observed 95\% confidence level (CL) upper limit on $\mu_{cc}$ is 41, while a limit of 28 is expected in the case of no H $\rightarrow$ cc process. The result is also interpreted in the 'kappa framework' by reparameterizing $\mu_{cc}$ in terms of the charm Yukawa coupling modifier $\kappa_c$, defined as $\kappa_c^2 = \Gamma_c/\Gamma_{SMc}$, where $\Gamma_c$ is the partial decay width into a charm-quark pair. Assuming that all other couplings follow their SM predictions and only SM decays are considered, the parameterization is:
\begin{equation}
	\kappa_c = \frac{\mu_{cc}}{\sqrt{BR_{SM}(H \rightarrow cc)}}
\end{equation}

where $BR_{SM}(H \rightarrow cc)$ is the SM H $\rightarrow$ cc branching fraction. The expected 95\% CL constraint on the modifier is $|\kappa_c| < 11.6$. No observed constraint can be set at 95\% CL due to the large observed value of $\mu_{cc}$. Instead, a 68\% CL interval of $2.3 < |\kappa_c| < 18.4$ is obtained. An additional fit, performed by considering only the VBF process as signal, while fixing the ggF contribution to its SM prediction within uncertainties, yields a nearly identical result for both the H $\rightarrow$ bb and H $\rightarrow$ cc measurements.

In both cases, the dominant systematic uncertainties arise from the Non-res. bkgd bias uncertainty and the effects of limited MC sample size (particularly those from the QCD V+jets samples), followed by the detector-related uncertainties, primarily those associated with jets.

\subsection{Combinations}

Two combined analyses are performed: one combining the H $\rightarrow$ cc result with the Run-2 VH-channel results, and another combining the Run-3 H $\rightarrow$ bb result with the Run-2 result in the VBF channel and a recently reported Run-2 photon-associated VBF (VBF+$\gamma$) result. For the H $\rightarrow$ cc combination, $\mu_{bb}$ is also allowed to float. For the H $\rightarrow$ bb combination, the H $\rightarrow$ cc channels from this analysis are excluded and $\mu_{cc}$ is fixed to 1, with the ggF contributions fixed to their SM predictions within uncertainties. For both combinations, jet-related experimental uncertainties are correlated, except for those related to jet flavor tagging, where different algorithms and calibration procedures are used; background modeling uncertainties are treated as uncorrelated, while theory uncertainties in signal production cross-sections and branching ratios are correlated.

From the first combination, the observed $\mu_{cc,Comb}$ signal strength relative to the SM prediction is 3.3$^{+5.2}_{-5.0}$, assuming SM VBF, ggF, and VH production rates. The observed (expected) 95\% CL upper limit on $\mu_{cc,Comb}$ is 13 (10), corresponding to an observed (expected) constraint of $|\kappa_c| < 4.7 (3.9)$. This represents an 8\% improvement in the expected limit and a 5\% improvement in the expected $|\kappa_c|$ constraint relative to the VH-only result. The observed result is slightly weaker than that from the VH channel, owing to an upward fluctuation in $\mu_{cc,Comb}$ driven by the VBF contribution. From the second combination, the observed $\mu_{bb,VBF}$ signal strength relative to the SM prediction is 0.87 $\pm$ 0.28, corresponding to an observed (expected) significance of 3.2 (3.6) standard deviations, thus providing the first evidence for the VBF H $\rightarrow$ bb process. The p-value for compatibility of the signal-strength parameters among the three analyses is 65\%, and 95\% between the Run-2 and Run-3 VBF H $\rightarrow$ bb analyses.

\section{Conclusion}

In summary, this thesis presents the first search for SM Higgs boson decay into a pair of charm quarks in the VBF production mode in the ATLAS experiment at the LHC, together with a simultaneous measurement of Higgs boson decay into a bottom quark-antiquark pair. The VBF H $\rightarrow$ cc search is enabled by a new trigger designed to exploit the VBF topology. The observed signal strengths relative to the SM predictions are $\mu_{bb} = 0.97^{+0.57}_{-0.50}$ and $\mu_{cc} = 18 \pm 13$. For the H $\rightarrow$ cc process, this corresponds to an observed (expected) 95\% CL upper limit of 41 (28) on $\mu_{cc}$. When combined with previous Run-2 results, these measurements improve the expected constraint on the Higgs-charm coupling and both the expected and observed constraints on the Higgs-bottom coupling.
