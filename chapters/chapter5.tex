\section{Boosted Jet Tagging with Graph and Attention Neural Networks}

In addition to small-R jet tagging which typically classifies jets as b, c, or light, large-R jet tagging (R=1.0), classifies boosted objects. When an object is boosted, the decay products often overlap in the lab frame. Identifying these boosted jets is crucial for the Higgs program at the LHC since a boosted Higgs can hadronically decay in the following channels: $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ and $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$. The backgrounds considered for are multijet (QCD) processes and fully hadronic top-quark decays. Taking advantage of transformer and graph architecture, GN2X is capable of learning directly on track inputs to learn the correlations within the large-R jet. GN2X produces an embedding of the jet in a 4D output with a softmax function applied across the predictions to enable the output to be interpreted as a probability of four classes: $p_{Hbb}$,$p_{Hcc}$, $p_{top}$, and $p_{QCD}$. The 4D output can be reduced to a single 1D output using the formulas for the discriminant shown below, which roughly resembles log likelihood. 

\begin{align}
	D^{GN2X}_{Hbb} &= ln \left( \frac{p_{Hbb}}{f_{Hcc} \cdot p_{Hcc} + f_{top} \cdot p_{top} + (1-f_{Hcc}-f_{top})\cdot p_{QCD}} \right) \\
	D^{GN2X}_{Hcc} &= ln \left( \frac{p_{Hcc}}{f_{Hbb} \cdot p_{Hbb} + f_{top} \cdot p_{top} + (1-f_{Hbb}-f_{top})\cdot p_{QCD}} \right)
\end{align}

By cutting on the discriminant score, we can construct working points according to how much of the signal is retained by implementing the cut. Working points are defined by certain cuts on signal efficiency, $\epsilon_{sig}$, where signal efficiency is defined as the ratio of signal events passing the cut divided by the total number of signal events. Conventionally, there are 3 (or more) working points: loose, medium, and tight. A loose working point cuts the least amount of signal, but allows for the most background contamination. A tight working point cuts a lot of signal statistics, but supresses the most background. Working points are analysis dependent, and should be chosen empirically but choosing the working point that maximizes signal sensitivity in your signal region. Shown in Fig \ref{fig:GN2X_Discriminant}, we can see a cut at the 50\% signal efficiency working point which, by definition, is cutting 50\% of the signal, but heavily suppressing the background. You can notice that the dominant background in this figure is from Top decay, blue line. Therefore, this background will be important to calibrate to data, more on that later.

These taggers are trained on MC where truth labels exist. MC is not a perfect model for data. There can exist mismodeling effects at multiple different stages including many effects from: generator, showering, detector, and reconstruction. Therefore, a working point defined in MC does not perfectly extrapolate to the same signal and background efficiency in data. To quantify the differences between phase spaces after implementing a cut, we must perform a calibration technique to account lack of excess of events in data.

Top decay is a major source of background for GN2X $H \rightarrow bb$ and $H \rightarrow cc$, therefore, it is an important background to calibrate. As part of my ATLAS authorship qualification task, I investigated the $t\bar{t}$ mistag rate which quantifies the amount of ttbar events passing the predefined working point selections. However, the mistag rate is not the same in MC and data due to mismodeling effects. Therefore, scale factors are derived which is defined as the ratio of efficiency in data and efficiency in MC.

\begin{equation}
	SF=\frac{\epsilon^{data}}{\epsilon^{MC}}
\end{equation}

Efficiency is the number of ttbar events passing selection divided by the total number of ttbar events. Since the number of ttbar events in data is unknown, we must extract it by performing a maximum likelihood fit. In each region (defined later) we can perform a counting experiment based on the simple fact that the number of expected MC events should match the number of events observed in data. Since MC does not perfectly agree with data, we have 2 degrees of freedom: $\mu$ and $SF$. Here $\mu$ is the ttbar normalization factor that accounts for the overall normalization between MC and data, and $SF$ is the factor that accounts for the discrepancy introduced by the tagger. With these two unknowns, we can construct two equations (one for SR and one for CR), to simultaneously extract $\mu$ and $SF$. 

\begin{align}
	N^{data}_{tag} &= \mu \cdot \frac{\epsilon^{data}}{\epsilon^{MC}} \cdot N^{ttbar}_{tag} + N^{other}_{tag} \\
	N^{data}_{untag} &= \mu \cdot \frac{1-\epsilon^{data}}{1-\epsilon^{MC}} \cdot N^{ttbar}_{untag} + N^{other}_{untag}
\end{align}

Or equivalently the formula can be written in terms of scale factors:

\begin{align}
	N^{data}_{tag} &= \mu \cdot SF \cdot N^{ttbar}_{tag} + N^{other}_{tag} \\
	N^{data}_{untag} &= \mu \cdot \frac{1-\epsilon^{MC} \cdot SF}{1-\epsilon^{MC}} \cdot N^{ttbar}_{untag} + N^{other}_{untag}
\end{align}

Since there are no floating normalization factors applied to the backgrounds, we must find a region in phase space where the backgrounds are heavily suppressed and the ttbar signal is dominant. To do so, we target semi-leptonic ttbar events, where the leptonic top decay is considered "tag" and the hadronic top decay is considered "probe".

\subsection{Tag-and-Probe Event Selection}

Physics objects are reconstructed and calibrated before event selection. Electrons are required 
to have $p_\text{T} > 28$ GeV, $|\eta_\text{cluster}| < 2.47$ (excluding the barrel/endcap 
transition region $1.37 < |\eta_\text{cluster}| < 1.52$), pass tight identification, and match 
a track in the inner detector (ID). Muons must satisfy $p_\text{T} > 28$ GeV, $|\eta| < 2.5$, 
and pass medium identification by matching ID and muon spectrometer tracks. Both lepton types 
must meet tight isolation criteria and primary vertex compatibility requirements.

Small-$R$ jets are built via the anti-$k_\text{T}$ algorithm ($R = 0.4$) using particle flow 
inputs, requiring $p_\text{T} > 25$ GeV and $|\eta| < 2.5$, with pile-up suppression via the 
Jet Vertex Tagger. $B$-jets are identified using the DL1r multivariate algorithm. Large-$R$ 
jets ($R = 1.0$) are reconstructed from topological LCTopo clusters using the anti-$k_\text{T}$ 
algorithm, then trimmed to reduce pile-up and soft emission effects --- subjets carrying less 
than 5\% of the large-$R$ jet $p_\text{T}$ are removed. Only calibrated jets with 
$p_\text{T} > 300$ GeV and $|\eta| < 2.0$ are considered. Variable-Radius track-jets 
($p_\text{T} > 7$ GeV, $0.02 \leq R \leq 0.4$) are ghost-associated to large-$R$ jets for 
flavour tagging.

Overlap removal is applied sequentially to prevent double-counting: electrons within 
$\Delta R = 0.01$ of a muon are removed first, followed by jets within $\Delta R = 0.2$ of 
an electron. Electrons within $\Delta R = 0.4$ of a remaining jet are then discarded. Muons 
are rejected if within $\Delta R < 0.04 + 10~\text{GeV}/p_\text{T}(\mu)$ of a jet with three 
or more tracks; otherwise the jet is removed.

The missing transverse momentum ($E_\text{T}^\text{miss}$) is computed as the magnitude of 
the negative vector sum of all calibrated object momenta, plus a track-based soft term matched 
to the primary vertex to ensure resilience against pile-up.

Events must pass a single-electron or single-muon trigger with $p_\text{T} > 28$ GeV, and are 
further required to contain exactly one lepton with $p_\text{T} > 70$ GeV to suppress 
non-prompt lepton backgrounds. $E_\text{T}^\text{miss} > 70$ GeV is imposed to account for 
the neutrino from the leptonic $W$ decay. At least one large-$R$ jet with $p_\text{T} \geq 
300$ GeV must be present, with the leading-$p_\text{T}$ jet designated as the probe-jet 
candidate; its mass is restricted to $50~\text{GeV} \leq m \leq 300~\text{GeV}$ to ensure 
compatibility with the calibrated LCTopo jet mass range. At least one small-$R$ jet, 
specifically the closest to the lepton in $\Delta R$, must pass $b$-tagging at the 77\% DL1r 
working point and serves as the tag-jet, significantly suppressing the $V$+jets background.
