\section{Introduction to the ATLAS Detector}

The ATLAS (\underline{A} \underline{T}oroidal \underline{L}HC \underline{A}pparatu\underline{S}) detector~\cite{PERF-2007-01} is a general-purpose particle detector designed to reconstruct the full final state of $pp$ collisions across a broad physics program. It is situated at a designated interaction point on the LHC, approximately 100\,m below ground near the CERN campus. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ATLAS_detector}, the detector is 44\,m in length, 25\,m in diameter, and weighs approximately 7000 tonnes. It is composed of several concentric subsystems arranged around the interaction point: the inner tracking system, the electromagnetic and hadronic calorimeters, and the muon spectrometer. Each subsystem is designed to measure specific properties of the particles produced in the collision, as described in the following sections.

\subsection{General Purpose Detector Design}

A general-purpose detector is designed around the physics objects it must
measure. Two subsystems form the foundation of this design. The
\textbf{inner tracker} measures the momenta of charged particles by recording
their curved trajectories in a solenoidal magnetic field. It must do so
\textit{non-destructively}, using thin silicon layers that minimally perturb
the particles passing through. The \textbf{calorimeters} take the opposite
approach, and are designed to stop particles completely and absorb their
total energy. Unlike the tracker, the calorimeters respond to both charged
and neutral particles. Together, these two systems provide the complementary
information needed to reconstruct the full event topology.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/chapter3/ATLAS_detector_particles.png}
	\caption{https://atlas.cern/Resources/Schematics}
	\label{fig:detector_slice}
\end{figure}

As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:detector_slice}, each particle species
exploits this architecture differently. \textbf{Electrons}~\cite{EGAM-2018-01} leave a curved
track in the inner detector and deposit their full energy in the EM
calorimeter via a narrow electromagnetic shower. \textbf{Photons}~\cite{EGAM-2018-01} produce
an identical EM shower but leave no track, as they carry no electric charge.
\textbf{Muons}~\cite{MUON-2018-03} are minimum-ionising and pass through the calorimeters
largely unimpeded. They leave matched tracks in both the inner detector and
the outermost muon spectrometer. \textbf{Jets} leave tracks from their
charged constituents and deposit energy across both the EM and hadronic
calorimeters. \textbf{Neutrinos} escape the detector entirely. Their
presence is inferred from the transverse momentum imbalance they leave
behind, reconstructed as missing transverse energy $E_{\text{T}}^{\text{miss}}$.

\subsection{Detector Coordinate System}

ATLAS uses a right-handed coordinate system with its origin at the
interaction point ($z=0$). The $z$-axis points along the beam direction,
the $x$-axis points toward the center of the LHC ring, and the $y$-axis
points upward. The azimuthal angle $\phi$ is measured around the beam axis
in the transverse plane, and the polar angle $\theta$ is measured from the
positive $z$-axis. The polar angle $\theta$ transforms nonlinearly under
longitudinal Lorentz boosts, making it an inconvenient coordinate.
Rapidity, $y = \frac{1}{2}\ln\frac{E+p_z}{E-p_z}$, transforms with a
simple additive shift, so rapidity \textit{differences} between particles
are invariant. Since computing $y$ requires knowledge of the particle mass,
it is approximated by the pseudorapidity:
%
\begin{equation}
	\eta = -\ln\tan\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right),
	\label{eq:pseudorapidity}
\end{equation}
%
which is purely geometric and equals $y$ in the massless limit.
Particle production is approximately uniform in $\eta$, which is why
detector subsystems are segmented uniformly in $\eta$ rather than $\theta$.
The angular separation between two objects is expressed as:
%
\begin{equation}
	\Delta R = \sqrt{(\Delta\eta)^2 + (\Delta\phi)^2},
	\label{eq:deltaR}
\end{equation}
%
which is the standard metric for object matching and isolation. The detector
is divided into a central barrel region and two end-cap regions, with
transverse momentum and energy denoted $p_{\text{T}}$ and $E_{\text{T}}$
respectively.

\subsection{Pixel Detector}

The innermost component is the Pixel detector, which provides the highest granularity acting at a radial distance between 50mm and 150mm. It consists of 1744 silicon pixel modules arranged in three concentric barrel layers and two end-caps of three disks each. Each module has an active area of 16.4\,mm $\times$ 60.8\,mm and contains 47{,}232 pixels of size 50\,$\mu$m $\times$ 400\,$\mu$m, giving a total of approximately 80.4 million readout channels. The innermost barrel layer is the Insertable B-Layer (IBL)~\cite{Abbott:2018ikt}, installed during the Long Shutdown 1 (LS1) between Run 1 and Run 2 at a radius of only 33mm from the beam axis. The IBL was introduced to improve primary vertex resolution and the reconstruction of displaced vertices from $b$-hadron decays, which is critical for $b$-jet identification (\textit{b}-tagging).

IBL: https://cds.cern.ch/record/1291633 \\
Commissioning and calibration: https://arxiv.org/pdf/1004.5293

\subsection{Semiconductor Tracker -- SCT}

Surrounding the Pixel detector is the Semiconductor Tracker (SCT), a silicon detector consisting of 4088 modules of silicon-strip detectors. The SCT is arranged in four concentric barrels and two end-caps of nine disks each, providing space-point measurements across radial distances from 299mm to 560mm. The strips in the barrel are approximately aligned parallel to the solenoid field and beam axis while the strip direction in the end-caps is radial. The strips read out a total of 128 channels giving a total of approximately 6.3 million readout channels. A hit is registered when the pulse height in a channel exceeds a preset threshold, nominally corresponding to a charge of 1fC. Together, the Pixel detector and SCT provide the precision hit measurements used in track reconstruction.

\subsection{Transition Radiation Tracker -- TRT}

The outermost component of the tracking system is the Transition Radiation Tracker (TRT), a gaseous straw-tube detector which covers radial distances from  554mm to 1082mm. The TRT consists of 298,304 proportional drift tubes (straws)  of 4mm diameter, read out by 350,848 channels of electronics. The straws in the barrel region are arranged in three cylindrical layers and 32 $\phi$ sectors, with split anodes read out from each side. In the end-cap regions, the straws are radially oriented. The TRT straw layout is designed such that charged particles with transverse momentum  $p_{\text{T}} > 0.5$\,GeV and pseudorapidity $|\eta| < 2.0$ typically cross more  than 30 straws, contributing significantly to overall track momentum resolution.

\section{The Calorimeter System}

The ATLAS calorimeter system~\cite{PERF-2007-01} surrounds the solenoid magnet, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:ATLAS_calorimeter}. It provides measurements of the energy deposits by both charged and neutral particles, with the exception of muons and neutrinos. It is designed to provide full azimuthal coverage over $|\eta| < 4.9$, capturing the information needed for reliable reconstruction of missing transverse energy $E_{\text{T}}^{\text{miss}}$. The system is divided into an electromagnetic (EM) calorimeter, which measures the energy of electrons and photons via electromagnetic showers, and a hadronic calorimeter, which captures the broader showers produced by pions and other hadronic activity. The overall depth of the calorimeter system exceeds 10 hadronic interaction lengths, $\lambda$, ensuring the energy of the final state particles produced in the collisions is captured effectively.

\subsection{Electromagnetic Calorimeter}

The electromagnetic calorimeter is designed to capture the energy of electrons and photons. It is divided into three components: the LAr electromagnetic barrel (EMB), covering $|\eta| < 1.475$, the LAr electromagnetic end-cap (EMEC), covering $1.375 < |\eta| < 3.2$, and a presampler at $|\eta| < 1.8$ which corrects for upstream energy losses. The calorimeter uses liquid argon (LAr) as the active medium with lead absorber plates in an accordion geometry, and is segmented into three longitudinal sampling layers to enable shower development measurements. The energy resolution is parameterized as:
%
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\sigma_E}{E} = \frac{a}{\sqrt{E}} \oplus b,
	\label{eq:em_resolution}
\end{equation}
%
where $a$ and $b$ are the stochastic and constant terms 
respectively. For the ATLAS EMB, the stochastic term is 
$a \approx 10\%\,\sqrt{\text{GeV}}$ and the constant term $b \approx 0.17\%$.

Calibration: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2309.05471

\subsection{Hadronic Calorimeter}

The hadronic calorimeter is designed to capture the energy of hadrons and hadronic jets. It is divided into two components: the Tile calorimeter (TileCal), covering $|\eta| < 1.7$, and the LAr hadronic end-cap calorimeter (HEC), covering $1.5 < |\eta| < 3.2$. TileCal uses steel absorber plates with scintillating plastic tiles as the active medium, while the HEC uses copper absorber plates interleaved with liquid argon gaps, sharing the end-cap cryostats with the EMEC. The energy resolution is parameterized as:
%
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\sigma_E}{E} \approx \frac{50\%}{\sqrt{E}} \oplus 3\%,
	\label{eq:had_resolution}
\end{equation}
%
where the stochastic term reflects intrinsic fluctuations in hadronic 
shower development and the constant term encodes calibration uncertainties.

https://cds.cern.ch/record/2004868

\subsection{Forward Calorimeter}

The LAr forward calorimeter (FCal) is designed to capture both electromagnetic and hadronic energy in the forward region, extending calorimetric coverage to $3.1 < |\eta| < 4.9$, housed in the same end-cap cryostats as the EMEC and HEC. It consists of three longitudinal modules: the first uses copper absorbers optimized for electromagnetic measurements, while the second and third use tungsten absorbers for hadronic energy measurement. Each module consists of a metal matrix with regularly spaced longitudinal channels filled with concentric rod and tube electrodes, with LAr in the gap serving as the active medium. The FCal is approximately 10 interaction lengths deep, and its high-density design is necessitated by the extremely high particle flux in the forward region close to the beam pipe. The electromagnetic energy resolution is parameterized as:
%
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\sigma_E}{E} \approx \frac{28.5\%}{\sqrt{E}} \oplus 3.5\%,
	\label{eq:fcal_em_resolution}
\end{equation}
%
and for hadronic energy the resolution is approximately
%
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\sigma_E}{E} \approx \frac{94\%}{\sqrt{E}} \oplus 7.5\%,
	\label{eq:fcal_had_resolution}
\end{equation}
%
as measured in test-beam campaigns at the CERN SPS. Together, the EMB, 
EMEC, HEC, TileCal, and FCal provide the ATLAS detector with full 
calorimetric coverage for jet reconstruction and 
$E_{\text{T}}^{\text{miss}}$ measurement across the broad range of 
physics processes accessed at the LHC.

https://iopscience.iop.org/article/10.1088/1748-0221/8/05/P05006/pdf

\section{Muon Spectrometer}

The muon spectrometer is designed to measure the momentum and trajectory of muons, which pass through the calorimeters with minimal energy loss. It is based on the magnetic deflection of muon tracks due to the powerful magnets. The muon spectrometer is instrumented with separate trigger and high-precision tracking chambers. The spectrometer provides coverage over $|\eta| < 2.7$, using monitored drift tubes (MDT) and cathode strip chambers (CSC) for precision tracking, and resistive-plate chambers (RPC) and thin-gap chambers (TGC) for triggering.

\section{Trigger and Data Acquisition Systems} % EXPAND ON THIS SECTION IN INTRO AND IN OBJECT RECONSTRUCTION. FIRST GLANCE AT THE EVENT WHAT OBJECTS HAVE BEEN PRODUCED. PART OF RECONSTRUCTION. 

The ATLAS trigger and data acquisition (TDAQ) system is responsible for selecting events of physics interest from the 40MHz bunch crossing rate delivered by the LHC. It operates in three stages. The Level-1 (L1) trigger, implemented in custom hardware using coarse-granularity calorimeter and muon chamber information, reduces the event rate to approximately 75kHz. The Level-2 (L2) trigger then performs further selections, reducing the rate to approximately 3.5kHz. Finally, the event filter applies full offline-quality reconstruction algorithms to reduce the rate to approximately 200Hz for permanent storage at CERN.

The raw output of the ATLAS detector consists of millions of digitized electronic signals read out across hundreds of millions of channels in the tracking, calorimeter, and muon systems. The central challenge of event reconstruction is the conversion of  these digital signals into physically meaningful four-vectors representing the particles produced in the hard-scatter interaction. Each reconstructed object is the product of algorithms that aggregate information across multiple subsystems, resolve ambiguities from overlapping signals, and suppress backgrounds from pile-up and electronic noise.

\subsection{Track Reconstruction}
%--------------------------------------------------------------

https://acts-project.github.io/tracking.html
https://cds.cern.ch/record/2766886
https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0168900287908874

Track reconstruction is the process of inferring the trajectories of charged particles 
from the discrete space-point measurements, or \textit{hits}, left in the silicon pixel 
and strip layers of the inner detector~\cite{IDTR-2022-04}. A single charged particle 
traversing the ID produces a sequence of $\mathcal{O}(10)$ hits across the pixel and 
SCT layers, and the task is to correctly associate hits belonging to the same particle 
into a \textit{track candidate} in the presence of $\mathcal{O}(10^3)$ additional hits 
per event from pile-up interactions, $\delta$-rays, and detector noise.

Reconstruction proceeds inside-out. First, three-dimensional space-points are formed 
from individual channel hits, exploiting the stereo geometry of the SCT to resolve 
two-dimensional positions from pairs of strip measurements. Track candidates are seeded 
from triplets of space-points in the pixel and first SCT layers, each defining an 
initial helix hypothesis parameterized by the five track parameters
%
\begin{equation}
	\boldsymbol{\tau} = (d_0,\, z_0,\, \phi,\, \theta,\, q/p),
	\label{eq:track_params}
\end{equation}
%
where $d_0$ and $z_0$ are the transverse and longitudinal impact parameters with 
respect to the primary vertex, $\phi$ and $\theta$ are the azimuthal and polar angles 
at the point of closest approach, and $q/p$ is the signed inverse momentum.

Each seed is extended outward through successive detector layers using a 
\textbf{Kalman filter}~\cite{Fruhwirth:1987fm}, which provides an optimal recursive 
estimate of the track parameters in the presence of measurement noise and stochastic 
energy losses from material interactions. At each layer the filter alternates between 
a \textit{prediction} step, propagating the current state estimate forward accounting 
for the magnetic field and energy loss, and an \textit{update} step, incorporating the 
new measurement to refine the state and its covariance. This naturally accounts for 
multiple scattering without biasing the track parameter estimates. Competing candidates 
that share hits are resolved by an ambiguity solver that scores each candidate on its 
number of hits, fit quality $\chi^2/n_{\text{dof}}$, and number of holes (expected 
but missing hits). The final tracks are required to satisfy quality criteria on the 
minimum number of hits and maximum $|d_0|$ and $|z_0|$ to suppress fake tracks from 
random hit combinations. For Run~3, the reconstruction software was significantly 
optimized to handle the higher pile-up environment of $\langle\mu\rangle \approx 50$, 
achieving a factor of two improvement in processing speed while maintaining 
reconstruction efficiency and reducing the fake-track rate by more than a factor of 
two~\cite{IDTR-2022-04}.

\subsection{Jet Reconstruction}
%--------------------------------------------------------------

Quarks and gluons produced in hard-scatter interactions cannot be observed directly 
due to color confinement, and instead fragment and hadronize into collimated sprays 
of stable particles known as \textit{jets}. A jet algorithm must be both infrared- and 
collinear-safe — that is, insensitive to soft wide-angle radiation and to the collinear 
splitting of hard partons~\cite{Cacciari:2008gp, Salam:2009jx}.

\subsubsection{Topological Clustering}

The starting point for calorimeter-based jet reconstruction is the formation of 
\textit{topological clusters} (topo-clusters)~\cite{PERF-2014-07}, which suppress 
electronic noise and pile-up by grouping calorimeter cells according to their 
signal-to-noise ratio $\varsigma = |E_{\text{cell}}| / \sigma_{\text{noise}}$. Cluster 
formation proceeds via a seeded growth algorithm in which cells with $\varsigma > 4$ 
are designated as seeds, neighboring cells with $\varsigma > 2$ are iteratively 
added, and a final ring of adjacent cells with $\varsigma > 0$ is included to capture 
the full shower extent. This \textit{4-2-0} thresholding scheme is illustrated in 
Figure~\ref{fig:topo_cluster} for a simulated dijet event in the FCal. Topo-clusters 
are calibrated at the electromagnetic scale and may be further corrected to the 
hadronic scale using Local Cell Weighting (LCW), which classifies clusters as 
electromagnetic or hadronic based on their shower shape and applies corrections for 
non-compensation, out-of-cluster losses, and dead material.

\subsubsection{Particle Flow and UFO Jets}

A significant limitation of calorimeter-only jet reconstruction is the relatively 
coarse hadronic energy resolution of $\sigma_E/E \sim 50\%/\sqrt{E}$, whereas the 
inner detector provides superior momentum resolution for charged particles below 
approximately 100\,GeV. The \textbf{Particle Flow} (PFlow) 
algorithm~\cite{PERF-2015-09} exploits this complementarity by matching inner detector 
tracks to topo-clusters, subtracting the expected calorimeter deposition of matched 
charged particles, and replacing it with the track four-momentum. The resulting 
\textit{particle flow objects} (PFOs) combine tracks for charged particles with 
residual topo-clusters for neutral particles, yielding improved jet energy and angular 
resolution over calorimeter-only inputs.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/chapter3/ATLAS_pflow.png}
	\caption{https://atlas.cern/Resources/Schematics}
	\label{fig:ATLAS:particles}
\end{figure}

For large-radius jets used in boosted topology searches, \textbf{Unified Flow Objects} 
(UFOs)~\cite{ATL-PHYS-PUB-2021-038} extend this paradigm by combining PFOs with 
additional inner detector tracks not associated to the primary vertex, improving 
sensitivity to the internal substructure of highly boosted $W$, $Z$, Higgs, and top 
quark decays. UFO jets are the preferred large-radius jet input for boosted object 
taggers in ATLAS Run~3 analyses.

In both cases jets are reconstructed using the \textit{anti-}$k_t$ 
algorithm~\cite{Cacciari:2008gp}, which clusters objects according to the distance 
measure
%
\begin{equation}
	d_{ij} = \min\!\left(k_{ti}^{-2},\, k_{tj}^{-2}\right) 
	\frac{\Delta R_{ij}^2}{R^2},
	\label{eq:antikt}
\end{equation}
%
where $k_{ti}$ is the transverse momentum of object $i$, $\Delta R_{ij}$ is their 
angular separation, and $R$ is the radius parameter. Small-radius jets use $R = 0.4$ 
and large-radius jets use $R = 1.0$. The anti-$k_t$ algorithm produces geometrically 
regular jets in the $\eta$--$\phi$ plane and its infrared and collinear safety 
properties make it well-suited for comparison with fixed-order QCD calculations.
