\section{Introduction to the ATLAS Detector}

The ATLAS (\underline{A} \underline{T}oroidal \underline{L}HC \underline{A}pparatu\underline{S}) detector~\cite{PERF-2007-01} is a general-purpose particle detector. It is situated near the CERN campus at a designated interaction point on the LHC 100m below ground. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ATLAS_detector}, the ATLAS detector is 44m in length and 25m in diameter and weighs around 7000 tonnes. At this scale, the ATLAS detector records some of the highest energy particle collisions ever observed. The detector is designed to capture the decay products of physics processes produced in $pp$ collisions. It is composed of several concentric subsystems arranged around the interaction point: the inner tracking system, the electromagnetic and hadronic calorimeters, and the muon spectrometer. A solenoid magnet surrounding the inner detector provides a 2T axial field which bends the trajectories of charged particles and the curvature can be used to determine the momentum of these particles.

\subsection{Detector Coordinate System}

ATLAS uses a right-handed coordinate system with its origin at the interaction point (z=0). The $z$-axis is defined along the beam direction, the $x$-axis points from the interaction point towards the center of the LHC ring, and the $y$-axis points upward. The azimuthal angle $\phi$ is measured in the $x$--$y$ transverse plane around the beam axis, while the polar angle $\theta$ is measured from the positive $z$-axis. In collider coordinate systems it is convenient to replace $\theta$ with the pseudorapidity, defined as:
%
\begin{equation}
	\eta = -\ln\tan\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right),
	\label{eq:pseudorapidity}
\end{equation}
%
which is an approximation to the rapidity $y = \frac{1}{2}\ln\frac{E+p_z}{E-p_z}$ in the massless limit, and whose differences are Lorentz-invariant under boosts along the beam axis. The angular separation between two objects is expressed as:
%
\begin{equation}
	\Delta R = \sqrt{(\Delta\eta)^2 + (\Delta\phi)^2},
	\label{eq:deltaR}
\end{equation}
%
which is the standard metric used for object matching and isolation. The detector is divided into a central barrel region and two end-cap regions. Particle momenta and energies measured transverse to the beam axis are denoted $p_{\text{T}}$ and $E_{\text{T}}$, respectively. Heavy objects, such as top quarks, tend to have high $p_T$ decay products and are biased toward central region ($\eta \approx 0$) while pileup and softer processes tend to be uniformly distributed in $\eta$.

\section{The Tracking System}

\subsection{Current Tracking System}

The ATLAS Inner Detector~\cite{PERF-2007-01} measures electrical readouts as charged particles interact with the silicon layers. The tracking system is composed of three complementary subdetectors: the Pixel detector, the Semiconductor Tracker (SCT), and the Transition Radiation Tracker (TRT) as shown in Figure~\ref{ATLAS_tracker}. 

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/chapter3/ATLAS_track_hits.png}
	\caption{https://atlas.cern/Resources/Schematics}
	\label{fig:ATLAS_tracker}
\end{figure}

The primary goal of the tracking system is to capture the path of charged particles, called tracks, and should minimally affect the momentum of tracks. The tracking system also allows for primary and secondary vertex reconstruction. A 2T solenoidal magnetic field applies an external force to the charged particles. Momentum of the particle can be determined based on the radius of curvature. The tracking system has full coverage over $\phi$ and acceptance over the pseudorapidity range $|\eta| < 2.5$. The momentum resolution of the tracking system scales with $p_T$ implying that more curvature gives more accurate $p_T$ measurement.
%
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\sigma_{p_T}}{p_T}=0.05\% p_T \text{GeV} \oplus 1\%
\end{equation}
%
The innermost component is the Pixel detector, which provides the highest granularity acting at a radial distance between 50mm and 150mm. It consists of 1744 silicon pixel modules arranged in three concentric barrel layers and two end-caps of three disks each. Each module has an active area of 16.4\,mm $\times$ 60.8\,mm and contains 47{,}232 pixels of size 50\,$\mu$m $\times$ 400\,$\mu$m, giving a total of approximately 80.4 million readout channels. providing measurements extremely close to the interaction point. It consists of three concentric barrel layers and two end-cap disks on each side. The innermost barrel layer is the Insertable B-Layer (IBL)~\cite{Abbott:2018ikt}, installed during the Long Shutdown 1 (LS1) between Run 1 and Run 2 at a radius of only 33mm from the beam axis. The IBL was introduced to improve primary vertex resolution and the reconstruction of displaced vertices from $b$-hadron decays, which is critical for $b$-jet identification (\textit{b}-tagging).

IBL: https://cds.cern.ch/record/1291633 \\
Comissioning and calibration: https://arxiv.org/pdf/1004.5293

Surrounding the Pixel detector is the Semiconductor Tracker (SCT), a silicon detector consisting of 4088 modules of silicon-strip detectors. The SCT is arranged in four concentric barrels and two end-caps of nine disks each, providing space-point measurements across radial distances from 299mm to 560mm. The strips in the barrel are approximately aligned parallel to the solenoid field and beam axis while the strip direction in the end-caps is radial. The strips read out a total of 128 channels giving a total of approximately 6.3 million readout channels. A hit is registered when the pulse height in a channel exceeds a preset threshold, nominally corresponding to a charge of 1fC. Together, the Pixel detector and SCT provide the precision hit measurements used in track reconstruction.

The outermost component of the tracking system is the Transition Radiation Tracker (TRT), a gaseous straw-tube detector which covers radial distances from  554mm to 1082mm. The TRT consists of 298,304 proportional drift tubes (straws)  of 4mm diameter, read out by 350,848 channels of electronics. The straws in the barrel region are arranged in three cylindrical layers and 32 $\phi$ sectors, with split anodes read out from each side. In the end-cap regions, the straws are radially oriented. The TRT straw layout is designed such that charged particles with transverse momentum  $p_{\text{T}} > 0.5$\,GeV and pseudorapidity $|\eta| < 2.0$ typically cross more  than 30 straws, contributing significantly to overall track momentum resolution.

\subsection{Upgraded Tracking System}

For the High-Luminosity LHC (HL-LHC), the ATLAS inner detector will be replaced entirely by the all-silicon Inner Tracker (ITk)~\cite{}. The HL-LHC will deliver instantaneous luminosities of up to $7 \times 10^{34}$\,cm$^{-2}$s$^{-1}$\cite{2105.10367}, resulting in an average of $\langle\mu\rangle \approx 200$ simultaneous $pp$ interactions per bunch crossing  (pile-up). The integrated luminosity is expected to reach 4000 $fb^{-1}$ by the end of the HL-LHC program, a significant increase from around 350 $fb^{-1}$ obtained in Run 3. Being inadequate for this radiation environment and track density, the TRT and SCT are fully decommissioned and replaced by the upgraded ITk. The ITk extends the tracking acceptance to $|\eta| < 4.0$, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:ATLAS_ITk}, significantly improving forward track reconstruction for physics processes with boosted topologies and forward jets.

The ITk comprises two subsystems. ITk Pixel detector forms the innermost region, consisting of five barrel layers and multiple end-cap rings extending to $|z| = 2850$mm, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ATLAS_tracker}. With 5 billion readout channels, it provides high-granularity space-point measurements close to the interaction point, retaining excellent vertex resolution in the high pile-up environment. The ITk Strip detector surrounds the pixel system, consisting of four barrel layers extending to $|\eta| < 2.5$ and six end-cap discs on each side covering $1.1 < |\eta| < 4.0$. The strip detectors have 50 million readout channels, and strip modules use short strips of 24.1mm and 48.2mm length in the barrel and end-cap, respectively.

\section{The Calorimeter System}

The ATLAS calorimeter system~\cite{PERF-2007-01} surrounds the solenoid magnet, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:ATLAS_calorimeter}. It provides measurements of the energy deposits by both charged and neutral particles, with the exception of muons and neutrinos. It is designed to provide full azimuthal coverage over $|\eta| < 4.9$, capturing the information needed for reliable reconstruction of missing transverse energy $E_{\text{T}}^{\text{miss}}$. The system is divided into an electromagnetic (EM) calorimeter, which measures the energy of electrons and photons via electromagnetic showers, and a hadronic calorimeter, which captures the broader showers produced by pions and other hadronic activity. The overall depth of the calorimeter system exceeds 10 hadronic interaction lengths, $\lambda$, ensuring the energy of the final state particles produced in the collisions is captured effectively.

	\label{fig:ATLAS_calorimeter}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Electromagnetic Calorimeter}

The electromagnetic calorimeter is designed to capture the energy of electrons and photons. It is divided into three components: the LAr electromagnetic barrel (EMB), covering $|\eta| < 1.475$, the LAr electromagnetic end-cap (EMEC), covering $1.375 < |\eta| < 3.2$, and a presampler at $|\eta| < 1.8$ which corrects for upstream energy losses. The calorimeter uses liquid argon (LAr) as the active medium with lead absorber plates in an accordion geometry, and is segmented into three longitudinal sampling layers to enable shower development measurements. The energy resolution is parameterized as:
%
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\sigma_E}{E} = \frac{a}{\sqrt{E}} \oplus b,
	\label{eq:em_resolution}
\end{equation}
%
where $a$ and $b$ are the stochastic and constant terms 
respectively. For the ATLAS EMB, the stochastic term is 
$a \approx 10\%\,\sqrt{\text{GeV}}$ and the constant term $b \approx 0.17\%$.

Calibration: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2309.05471

\subsection{Hadronic Calorimeter}

The hadronic calorimeter is designed to capture the energy of hadrons and hadronic jets. It is divided into two components: the Tile calorimeter (TileCal), covering $|\eta| < 1.7$, and the LAr hadronic end-cap calorimeter (HEC), covering $1.5 < |\eta| < 3.2$. TileCal uses steel absorber plates with scintillating plastic tiles as the active medium, while the HEC uses copper absorber plates interleaved with liquid argon gaps, sharing the end-cap cryostats with the EMEC. The energy resolution is parameterized as:
%
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\sigma_E}{E} \approx \frac{50\%}{\sqrt{E}} \oplus 3\%,
	\label{eq:had_resolution}
\end{equation}
%
where the stochastic term reflects intrinsic fluctuations in hadronic 
shower development and the constant term encodes calibration uncertainties.

https://cds.cern.ch/record/2004868

\subsection{Forward Calorimeter}

The LAr forward calorimeter (FCal) is designed to capture both electromagnetic and hadronic energy in the forward region, extending calorimetric coverage to $3.1 < |\eta| < 4.9$, housed in the same end-cap cryostats as the EMEC and HEC. It consists of three longitudinal modules: the first uses copper absorbers optimised for electromagnetic measurements, while the second and third use tungsten absorbers for hadronic energy measurement. Each module consists of a metal matrix with regularly spaced longitudinal channels filled with concentric rod and tube electrodes, with LAr in the gap serving as the active medium. The FCal is approximately 10 interaction lengths deep, and its high-density design is necessitated by the extremely high particle flux in the forward region close to the beam pipe. The electromagnetic energy resolution is parameterized as:
%
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\sigma_E}{E} \approx \frac{28.5\%}{\sqrt{E}} \oplus 3.5\%,
	\label{eq:fcal_em_resolution}
\end{equation}
%
and for hadronic energy the resolution is approximately
%
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\sigma_E}{E} \approx \frac{94\%}{\sqrt{E}} \oplus 7.5\%,
	\label{eq:fcal_had_resolution}
\end{equation}
%
as measured in test-beam campaigns at the CERN SPS. Together, the EMB, 
EMEC, HEC, TileCal, and FCal provide the ATLAS detector with full 
calorimetric coverage for jet reconstruction and 
$E_{\text{T}}^{\text{miss}}$ measurement across the broad range of 
physics processes accessed at the LHC.

https://iopscience.iop.org/article/10.1088/1748-0221/8/05/P05006/pdf

\section{Muon Spectrometer}

The muon spectrometer is designed to measure the momentum and trajectory of muons, which pass through the calorimeters with minimal energy loss. It is based on the magnetic deflection of muon tracks due to the powerful magnets. The muon spectrometer is instrumented with separate trigger and high-precision tracking chambers. The spectrometer provides coverage over $|\eta| < 2.7$, using monitored drift tubes (MDT) and cathode strip chambers (CSC) for precision tracking, and resistive-plate chambers (RPC) and thin-gap chambers (TGC) for triggering.

\section{Trigger and Data Acquisition Systems}

The ATLAS trigger and data acquisition (TDAQ) system is responsible for selecting events of physics interest from the 40MHz bunch crossing rate delivered by the LHC. It operates in three stages. The Level-1 (L1) trigger, implemented in custom hardware using coarse-granularity calorimeter and muon chamber information, reduces the event rate to approximately 75kHz. The Level-2 (L2) trigger then performs further selections, reducing the rate to approximately 3.5kHz. Finally, the event filter applies full offline-quality reconstruction algorithms to reduce the rate to approximately 200Hz for permanent storage at CERN.

\section{Object Reconstruction}

The raw output of the ATLAS detector consists of millions of electronic signals — 
analogue pulses shaped and digitised by dedicated front-end electronics across hundreds 
of millions of readout channels in the tracking, calorimeter, and muon systems. 
Converting these digital signals into physically meaningful four-vectors is the central 
challenge of event reconstruction. Each reconstructed object — a track, a jet, an 
electron, a muon — is the product of sophisticated algorithms that must aggregate 
information across multiple subsystems, resolve ambiguities from overlapping signals, 
and suppress the overwhelming backgrounds from pile-up and electronic noise, all within 
the stringent constraints of the ATLAS computing model.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/chapter3/ATLAS_detector_particles.png}
	\caption{https://atlas.cern/Resources/Schematics}
	\label{fig:detector_slice}
\end{figure}

A key guiding principle of object reconstruction is that each particle species produces 
a characteristic and distinct signature as it traverses the detector, as illustrated 
in Figure~\ref{fig:detector_slice}. Charged particles such as electrons and muons leave 
curved tracks in the inner detector due to the solenoidal magnetic field, with the 
curvature of the track inversely proportional to the transverse momentum $p_{\text{T}}$ 
of the particle. \textbf{Electrons} deposit their energy in the electromagnetic 
calorimeter via bremsstrahlung and pair production, producing a narrow, deep 
electromagnetic shower matched to an inner detector track. \textbf{Photons} produce 
an identical electromagnetic shower but leave no associated track, as they are 
electrically neutral. \textbf{Muons}, being approximately 200 times heavier than 
electrons, lose only a small fraction of their energy in the calorimeters via ionisation 
and traverse the full detector, producing a track in both the inner detector and the 
muon spectrometer with minimal calorimeter deposition. \textbf{Hadrons} such as protons 
and neutrons initiate broad hadronic showers in the hadronic calorimeter; charged 
hadrons additionally leave an inner detector track, while neutral hadrons such as 
neutrons produce no track and deposit energy only in the calorimeter. 
\textbf{Neutrinos} are invisible to the detector entirely, and their presence is 
inferred from an imbalance in the vectorial sum of transverse momenta of all 
reconstructed objects, known as the missing transverse energy $E_{\text{T}}^{\text{miss}}$. 
Reconstructing each of these objects therefore requires the coherent combination of 
information from multiple subsystems into a single unified four-vector, with the 
precise methodology depending on the particle species.

\subsection{Track Reconstruction}
%--------------------------------------------------------------

https://acts-project.github.io/tracking.html
https://cds.cern.ch/record/2766886
https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0168900287908874

Track reconstruction~\cite{ATL-SOFT-PUB-2007-007} is the process of inferring the 
trajectories of charged particles from the discrete space-point measurements, or 
\textit{hits}, left in the silicon pixel and strip layers of the inner detector. A 
single charged particle traversing the ID typically produces a sequence of 
$\mathcal{O}(10)$ hits across the pixel and SCT layers. The challenge is to correctly 
associate hits belonging to the same particle into a \textit{track candidate} in the 
presence of $\mathcal{O}(10^3)$ additional hits per event from pile-up interactions, 
$\delta$-rays, and detector noise.

Track reconstruction in ATLAS proceeds in several stages. First, three-dimensional 
space-points are formed from individual channel hits, exploiting the stereo geometry 
of the SCT to resolve the two-dimensional position from pairs of strip measurements. 
Track candidates are then seeded from triplets of space-points in the pixel layers 
and the first SCT layer. Each seed defines an initial helix hypothesis, 
parameterised by the five track parameters
%
\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{\tau} = (p_T, \eta, \phi, q, d_0, z_0),
	\label{eq:track_params}
\end{equation}
%
where $d_0$ and $z_0$ are the transverse and longitudinal impact parameters with 
respect to the primary vertex, $\phi_0$ and $\theta$ are the azimuthal and polar 
angles of the track at the point of closest approach, and $q/p$ is the signed inverse 
momentum.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/chapter3/ATLAS_track_reconstruction.png}
	\caption{https://atlas.cern/Resources/Schematics}
	\label{fig:ATLAS:particles}
\end{figure}

Each seed is then extended outward through the detector layers using a 
\textbf{Kalman filter}~\cite{Fruhwirth:1987fm}, which provides an optimal recursive 
estimator for the track parameters in the presence of measurement noise and 
stochastic energy losses from material interactions. At each detector layer, the 
Kalman filter performs two steps: a \textit{prediction} step, which propagates the 
current track state estimate to the next layer accounting for the magnetic field 
geometry and energy loss, and an \textit{update} step, which incorporates the 
measurement at that layer to refine the state estimate and its covariance matrix. 
This procedure naturally handles multiple scattering and energy loss in detector 
material, which would otherwise bias the track parameter estimates. Ambiguities 
arising from competing track candidates that share hits are resolved by a dedicated 
ambiguity solver that assigns a score to each candidate based on the number of hits, 
the fit quality $\chi^2/n_{\text{dof}}$, and the number of holes (expected but missing 
hits). The final set of reconstructed tracks is required to satisfy quality criteria 
including a minimum number of hits and a maximum $|d_0|$ and $|z_0|$ to suppress 
fake tracks from random hit combinations.

\subsection{Jet Reconstruction}
%--------------------------------------------------------------

Quarks and gluons produced in hard-scatter interactions cannot be observed directly 
due to colour confinement, and instead fragment and hadronise into collimated sprays 
of stable particles known as \textit{jets}. Reconstructing jets requires first 
defining a scheme for aggregating calorimeter and tracking signals into a single 
composite object, and then applying a jet algorithm that is both infrared- and 
collinear-safe — that is, insensitive to soft wide-angle radiation and to the 
collinear splitting of hard partons~\cite{Salam:2009jx}.

\subsubsection{Topological Clustering}

The starting point for calorimeter-based jet reconstruction is the formation of 
\textit{topological clusters} (topo-clusters)~\cite{PERF-2014-07}. Rather than 
using calorimeter cells directly, topo-clusters suppress electronic noise and pile-up 
by grouping cells based on their signal-to-noise ratio $\varsigma = |E_{\text{cell}}| 
/ \sigma_{\text{noise}}$. Cluster formation proceeds via a seeded growth algorithm: 
cells with $\varsigma > 4$ are designated as seeds, around which neighbouring cells 
with $\varsigma > 2$ are iteratively added. A final ring of all adjacent cells with 
$\varsigma > 0$ is then included to capture the full shower extent. This 
\textit{4-2-0} thresholding scheme ensures that genuine energy deposits from particle 
showers are retained while suppressing noise fluctuations. Topo-clusters are 
subsequently calibrated at the electromagnetic scale and may be further corrected 
to the hadronic scale using Local Cell Weighting (LCW), which classifies clusters 
as electromagnetic or hadronic in nature based on their shower shape and applies 
appropriate corrections for hadronic shower non-compensation, out-of-cluster 
energy, and dead material losses.

\subsubsection{Particle Flow and UFO Jets}

A significant limitation of purely calorimeter-based jet reconstruction is the 
relatively coarse hadronic energy resolution of $\sigma_E/E \sim 50\%/\sqrt{E}$, 
compared to the momentum resolution of the inner detector, which is superior for 
charged particles below approximately 100\,GeV. The \textbf{Particle Flow} (PFlow) 
algorithm~\cite{PERF-2015-09} exploits this complementarity by replacing the 
calorimeter energy deposits of charged hadrons with their corresponding inner 
detector track measurements, which provide superior momentum resolution at low 
$p_{\text{T}}$. PFlow proceeds by matching high-quality inner detector tracks to 
topo-clusters, subtracting the expected calorimeter energy deposition of the matched 
charged particle, and replacing it with the track four-momentum. The resulting 
\textit{particle flow objects} (PFOs) — a combination of tracks for charged particles 
and residual topo-clusters for neutral particles — are then passed to the jet 
algorithm, yielding improved jet energy and angular resolution compared to 
calorimeter-only inputs.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/chapter3/ATLAS_pflow.png}
	\caption{https://atlas.cern/Resources/Schematics}
	\label{fig:ATLAS:particles}
\end{figure}

Building further on this paradigm, \textbf{Unified Flow Objects} 
(UFOs)~\cite{ATL-PHYS-PUB-2021-038} extend the particle flow concept to the 
reconstruction of large-radius jets used in searches for boosted hadronic topologies. 
UFOs are formed by combining PFOs with reconstructed tracks from the inner detector 
that have not been associated to the primary vertex, allowing sensitivity to the 
substructure of highly boosted jets such as those arising from hadronically decaying 
$W$, $Z$, and Higgs bosons, as well as top quarks. The use of tracking information 
provides superior angular resolution within the jet cone and improved pile-up 
suppression compared to calorimeter-only large-radius jets, making UFO jets the 
preferred input for boosted object taggers in ATLAS Run~3 analyses.

In both cases, the jet algorithm of choice in ATLAS is the \textit{anti-}$k_t$ 
algorithm~\cite{Cacciari:2008gp}, which clusters PFOs or UFOs by iteratively 
combining pairs of objects according to the distance measure
%
\begin{equation}
	d_{ij} = \min\!\left(k_{ti}^{-2}, k_{tj}^{-2}\right) 
	\frac{\Delta R_{ij}^2}{R^2},
	\label{eq:antikt}
\end{equation}
%
where $k_{ti}$ is the transverse momentum of object $i$, $\Delta R_{ij}$ is their 
angular separation, and $R$ is the radius parameter. For small-radius jets used in 
most ATLAS analyses $R = 0.4$, while large-radius jets used for boosted topologies 
use $R = 1.0$. The anti-$k_t$ algorithm produces geometrically regular, circular jets 
in the $\eta$--$\phi$ plane, and its infrared and collinear safety properties make 
it well-suited for comparison with perturbative QCD calculations.
