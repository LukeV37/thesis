\section{Outline}

\section{The Large Hadron Collider}

The Large Hadron Collider (LHC)%~\cite{Evans:2008zzb} 
is a proton--proton synchrotron 
operating at CERN, near Geneva, Switzerland, and is currently the highest-energy particle 
collider ever constructed. The primary physics motivation for the LHC was the experimental 
confirmation of the Brout--Englert--Higgs mechanism of electroweak symmetry breaking, the 
discovery of which was announced in July 2012 by the ATLAS and CMS 
collaborations%~\cite{HIGG-2012-27, CMS-HIG-12-028}
. Beyond the Higgs boson, the LHC 
programme pursues a broad range of open questions in the Standard Model and beyond, 
including searches for supersymmetric partners, dark matter candidates, and precision 
measurements of the top quark and gauge sector. With detector systems reading out hundreds 
of millions of channels at 40\,MHz and data pipelines processing petabytes of collision 
data per year, the LHC experimental programme represents one of the largest and most 
complex data science endeavours ever undertaken.

	\centering
	%\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/chapter3/CERN_complex.png}
	\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/chapter3/CERN_complex_reduced.png}
	\caption{https://atlas.cern/Resources/Schematics}
	\label{fig:CERN_complex}
\end{figure}

Protons are sourced from hydrogen gas by stripping electrons with a strong electric field, 
after which a linear accelerator (LINAC\,4) brings them to 160\,MeV. They are then injected 
into the Proton Synchrotron Booster (PSB), which accelerates them to 2\,GeV, before passing 
into the Proton Synchrotron (PS) at 26\,GeV and subsequently the Super Proton Synchrotron 
(SPS) at 450\,GeV, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:CERN_complex}. Finally, two 
counter-rotating beams are injected into the LHC ring, where superconducting NbTi dipole 
magnets operating at 1.9\,K and a peak field of 8.33\,T bend the beams around the 
26.7\,km circumference. Radio-frequency (RF) cavities operating at 400\,MHz accelerate 
the protons to a Lorentz factor of $\gamma \approx 7000$, corresponding to a velocity of 
$v \approx 0.9999991\,c$ and a centre-of-mass energy of $\sqrt{s} = 13.6$\,TeV during 
Run~3, representing the world's highest centre-of-mass energy. The beams are structured 
into bunches spaced 25\,ns apart and are brought to collision at four interaction points 
around the ring, housing the ATLAS, CMS, ALICE, and LHCb experiments.

\section{The ATLAS Detector}

The ATLAS (A Toroidal LHC ApparatuS) detector~\cite{PERF-2007-01} is a general-purpose 
particle detector situated at Interaction Point 1 of the LHC. As shown in 
Figure~\ref{fig:ATLAS_detector}, it is approximately 44\,m in length and 25\,m in diameter, 
weighing around 7000 tonnes, making it one of the largest particle detectors ever 
constructed. The detector is designed with near-hermetic coverage to capture the full 
range of physics processes produced in $pp$ collisions, and is composed of several 
concentric subsystems arranged around the interaction point: the inner tracking detector, 
the electromagnetic and hadronic calorimeters, and the muon spectrometer. A solenoid 
magnet surrounding the inner detector provides a 2\,T axial field for charged-particle 
momentum measurement, while a system of three air-core toroid magnets — one barrel and 
two end-caps — provides the bending power for the muon spectrometer.

\subsubsection{Coordinate System}

ATLAS uses a right-handed coordinate system with its origin at the nominal interaction 
point. The $z$-axis is defined along the beam direction, the $x$-axis points from the 
interaction point towards the centre of the LHC ring, and the $y$-axis points upward. 
The azimuthal angle $\phi$ is measured in the $x$--$y$ transverse plane around the 
beam axis, while the polar angle $\theta$ is measured from the positive $z$-axis. In 
hadron collider experiments it is convenient to replace $\theta$ with the 
pseudorapidity, defined as
%
\begin{equation}
	\eta = -\ln\tan\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right),
	\label{eq:pseudorapidity}
\end{equation}
%
which is an approximation to the rapidity $y = \frac{1}{2}\ln\frac{E+p_z}{E-p_z}$ in 
the massless limit, and whose differences are Lorentz-invariant under boosts along the 
beam axis. The angular separation between two objects is therefore expressed as
%
\begin{equation}
	\Delta R = \sqrt{(\Delta\eta)^2 + (\Delta\phi)^2},
	\label{eq:deltaR}
\end{equation}
%
which is the standard metric used for object matching and isolation throughout this 
thesis. The detector is divided into a central \textit{barrel} region and two 
\textit{end-cap} regions. Particle momenta and energies measured transverse to the beam 
axis are denoted $p_{\text{T}}$ and $E_{\text{T}}$ respectively, as the large and 
unknown longitudinal boost of the partonic centre-of-mass frame renders longitudinal 
quantities less useful for event characterisation.

\subsection{Tracking System}

\subsubsection{Inner Detector}

The ATLAS Inner Detector (ID)~\cite{PERF-2007-01} provides charged-particle tracking 
and primary and secondary vertex reconstruction within a 2\,T solenoidal magnetic field 
over the pseudorapidity range $|\eta| < 2.5$. It is composed of three complementary 
subdetectors: the Pixel detector, the Semiconductor Tracker (SCT), and the Transition 
Radiation Tracker (TRT).

The innermost component is the Pixel detector, which provides the highest granularity 
measurements closest to the interaction point. It consists of four concentric barrel 
layers and three end-cap discs on each side. The innermost barrel layer is the 
Insertable B-Layer (IBL)~\cite{Abbott:2018ikt}, installed during the Long Shutdown 1 
(LS1) between Run~1 and Run~2 at a radius of only 33\,mm from the beam axis. The IBL 
was introduced to improve primary vertex resolution and the reconstruction of displaced 
vertices from $b$-hadron decays, which is critical for $b$-jet identification 
(\textit{b}-tagging). IBL modules use two sensor technologies: planar $n$-on-$n$ silicon 
pixels in the central region and 3D sensors at higher $|\eta|$, with a pixel pitch of 
$50 \times 250\,\mu$m$^2$.

Surrounding the Pixel detector is the Semiconductor Tracker (SCT), a microstrip silicon 
detector consisting of four barrel layers and nine end-cap discs on each side, providing 
space-point measurements up to a radius of approximately 520\,mm. Each SCT module 
consists of two layers of silicon strips with a small stereo angle of 40\,mrad between 
them, enabling two-dimensional hit reconstruction. Together, the Pixel detector and SCT 
provide the precision hit measurements used in track reconstruction.

The outermost component of the ID is the Transition Radiation Tracker (TRT), a 
gaseous straw-tube detector that extends tracking coverage out to a radius of 
approximately 1050\,mm. The TRT consists of 4\,mm diameter straw tubes filled with a 
Xe/CO$_2$/O$_2$ gas mixture, interleaved with polypropylene fibres and foils that 
act as transition radiation material. As highly relativistic particles traverse boundaries 
between media of different dielectric constants, they emit transition radiation X-rays, 
with the intensity strongly dependent on the Lorentz factor $\gamma$ of the particle. 
This provides intrinsic electron identification capability, complementing the 
calorimeter-based measurements. While the TRT offers lower precision per hit compared 
to the silicon detectors, the large number of hits per track (typically 30--35) 
contributes significantly to overall track momentum resolution.

\subsubsection{Inner Tracker Upgrade}

For the High-Luminosity LHC (HL-LHC), expected to begin operation around 2029, the 
ATLAS ID will be replaced entirely by the all-silicon Inner Tracker 
(ITk)~\cite{ATL-PHYS-PUB-2021-012}. The HL-LHC will deliver instantaneous luminosities 
of up to $7.5 \times 10^{34}$\,cm$^{-2}$s$^{-1}$, resulting in an average of 
$\langle\mu\rangle \approx 200$ simultaneous $pp$ interactions per bunch crossing 
(pile-up). The TRT and SCT, being inadequate for this radiation environment and track 
density, are fully decommissioned and replaced. The ITk extends the tracking acceptance 
to $|\eta| < 4.0$, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:itk}, significantly improving 
forward track reconstruction for physics processes with boosted topologies and 
forward jets.

The ITk comprises two subsystems. The \textbf{ITk Pixel} detector forms the innermost 
region, consisting of five barrel layers and multiple end-cap rings extending to 
$|z| = 2850$\,mm, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ATLAS_tracker}. It provides high-granularity 
space-point measurements close to the interaction point, retaining excellent vertex 
resolution in the high pile-up environment. The \textbf{ITk Strip} detector surrounds 
the pixel system, consisting of four barrel layers extending to $|\eta| < 2.5$ and 
six end-cap discs on each side covering $1.1 < |\eta| < 4.0$. The strip modules use 
short strips of 24.1\,mm and 48.2\,mm length in the barrel and end-cap respectively, 
with a strip pitch of approximately 75\,$\mu$m, providing two-dimensional measurements 
via a small stereo angle between paired strip layers.

\subsection{Calorimeter}

\subsection{Calorimeter System}

The ATLAS calorimeter system~\cite{PERF-2007-01} surrounds the solenoid magnet and 
provides measurements of the energy deposited by both charged and neutral particles, 
with the exception of muons and neutrinos. It is designed to provide full azimuthal 
coverage over $|\eta| < 4.9$, ensuring sufficient hermiticity for reliable missing 
transverse energy $E_{\text{T}}^{\text{miss}}$ reconstruction. The system is divided 
into an electromagnetic (EM) calorimeter, which measures the energy of electrons and 
photons via electromagnetic showers, and a hadronic calorimeter, which captures the 
broader hadronic showers produced by strongly interacting particles. The overall 
depth of the calorimeter system exceeds 10 hadronic interaction lengths $\lambda$, 
ensuring good shower containment and limiting punch-through into the muon 
spectrometer, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:ATLAS_calorimeter}.

	\centering

	\caption{https://atlas.cern/Resources/Schematics}
	\label{fig:ATLAS_calorimeter}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Electromagnetic Calorimeter}

The electromagnetic calorimeter uses liquid argon (LAr) as the active sampling medium 
throughout, with lead absorber plates, and is housed in three cryostats — one barrel 
and two end-caps. The \textbf{LAr electromagnetic barrel} (EMB) covers $|\eta| < 1.475$ 
and the \textbf{LAr electromagnetic end-cap} (EMEC) extends coverage over 
$1.375 < |\eta| < 3.2$. Both employ an accordion geometry for the lead absorber and 
kapton electrodes, which provides complete and uniform $\phi$ coverage without 
azimuthal cracks. The EM calorimeter is divided into three longitudinal sampling layers 
of varying granularity. The first layer, with fine $\eta$ segmentation of 
$\Delta\eta = 0.003$, provides precise shower-shape discrimination between prompt 
photons and $\pi^0 \to \gamma\gamma$ decays. The second layer collects the bulk of 
the shower energy, while the third layer samples the shower tail. A presampler layer 
at $|\eta| < 1.8$ corrects for energy losses upstream of the calorimeter in the 
solenoid and inner detector material. The energy resolution of the EM calorimeter 
is parameterised as
%
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\sigma_E}{E} = \frac{a}{\sqrt{E}} \oplus \frac{b}{E} \oplus c,
	\label{eq:em_resolution}
\end{equation}
%
where $a$ is the stochastic term, $b$ the noise term, and $c$ the constant term 
representing non-uniformities and calibration uncertainties, and $\oplus$ denotes 
addition in quadrature. For the ATLAS EMB, the stochastic term is 
$a \approx 10\%\,\sqrt{\text{GeV}}$ and the constant term $c \approx 0.2\%$.

\subsubsection{Hadronic Calorimeter}

The hadronic calorimeter employs two distinct technologies depending on the detector 
region. In the central region $|\eta| < 1.7$, the \textbf{Tile calorimeter} (TileCal) 
uses steel as the absorber and scintillating plastic tiles as the active medium, with 
wavelength-shifting fibres reading out the scintillation light to photomultiplier tubes. 
It is divided into a barrel covering $|\eta| < 1.0$ and two extended barrels covering 
$0.8 < |\eta| < 1.7$. In the end-cap region $1.5 < |\eta| < 3.2$, the \textbf{LAr 
	hadronic end-cap calorimeter} (HEC) uses copper absorber plates interleaved with 
LAr active gaps, sharing the end-cap cryostats with the EMEC. The hadronic energy 
resolution is approximately
%
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\sigma_E}{E} \approx \frac{50\%}{\sqrt{E}} \oplus 3\%,
	\label{eq:had_resolution}
\end{equation}
%
which is typical for a sampling hadronic calorimeter and reflects the intrinsic 
fluctuations in hadronic shower development, including the invisible energy carried 
by nuclear excitations and slow neutrons.

\subsubsection{Forward Calorimeter}

The \textbf{LAr forward calorimeter} (FCal) extends the calorimetric coverage to 
$3.1 < |\eta| < 4.9$, housed in the same end-cap cryostats as the EMEC and HEC. 
It consists of three longitudinal modules: the first uses copper absorbers optimised 
for electromagnetic measurements, while the second and third use tungsten absorbers 
for hadronic energy measurement. The high density of the FCal modules is necessitated 
by the extremely high particle flux in the forward region close to the beam pipe. 
Together, the EMB, EMEC, HEC, TileCal, and FCal provide the ATLAS detector with 
the full calorimetric coverage required for jet reconstruction and 
$E_{\text{T}}^{\text{miss}}$ measurement across the broad range of physics processes 
accessed at the LHC.
