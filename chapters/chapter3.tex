\section{Introduction to the ATLAS Detector}

The ATLAS (\underline{A} \underline{T}oroidal \underline{L}HC \underline{A}pparatu\underline{S}) detector~\cite{atlas} is a general-purpose particle detector designed to reconstruct the full final state of $pp$ collisions across a broad physics program.
It is situated at a designated interaction point on the LHC, approximately 100\,m below ground near the CERN campus.
As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ATLAS_detector}, the detector is 44\,m in length, 25\,m in diameter, and weighs approximately 7000 tonnes.
It is composed of several concentric subsystems arranged around the interaction point: the inner tracking system, the electromagnetic and hadronic calorimeters, and the muon spectrometer.
Each subsystem is designed to measure specific properties of the particles produced in the collision, as described in the following sections.

	\label{fig:ATLAS_detector}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Detector Coordinate System}

ATLAS uses a right-handed coordinate system with its origin at the interaction point ($z=0$). 
The $z$-axis points along the beam direction, the $x$-axis points toward the center of the LHC ring, and the $y$-axis points upward. 
The azimuthal angle $\phi$ is measured around the beam axis in the transverse plane, and the polar angle $\theta$ is measured from the positive $z$-axis. 
The polar angle $\theta$ transforms nonlinearly under longitudinal Lorentz boosts, making it an inconvenient coordinate.
Rapidity, $y = \frac{1}{2}\ln\frac{E+p_z}{E-p_z}$, transforms with a simple additive shift, so rapidity \textit{differences} between particles are invariant. 
Since computing $y$ requires knowledge of the particle mass, it is approximated by the pseudorapidity:
%
\begin{equation}
	\eta = -\ln\tan\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right),
	\label{eq:pseudorapidity}
\end{equation}
%
which is purely geometric and equals $y$ in the massless limit.
Particle production is approximately uniform in $\eta$, which is why detector subsystems are segmented uniformly in $\eta$ rather than $\theta$.
The angular separation between two objects is expressed as:
%
\begin{equation}
	\Delta R = \sqrt{(\Delta\eta)^2 + (\Delta\phi)^2},
	\label{eq:deltaR}
\end{equation}
%
which is the standard metric for object matching and isolation.

\section{General Purpose Detector Design}

A general-purpose detector is designed around the physics objects it must measure. 
Two subsystems form the foundation of this design. 
The \textbf{inner tracker} measures the momenta of charged particles by recording their curved trajectories in a solenoidal magnetic field. 
It must do so non-destructively, using thin silicon layers that minimally perturb the particles passing through. 
The \textbf{calorimeters} take the opposite approach, and are designed to stop particles completely and absorb their total energy. 
Unlike the tracker, the calorimeters respond to both charged and neutral particles. 
Together, these two systems provide the complementary information needed to reconstruct the full event topology.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/chapter3/ATLAS_detector_particles.png}
	\caption{Cross-sectional slice of the ATLAS detector illustrating the characteristic signatures of electrons, photons, muons, jets, and neutrinos in each subsystem~\cite{atlas}.}
	\label{fig:detector_slice}
\end{figure}

As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:detector_slice}, different types of particles are captured differently.
\textbf{Electrons}~\cite{EGAM-2018-01} leave a curved track in the inner detector and deposit their full energy in the EM calorimeter via a narrow electromagnetic shower. 
\textbf{Photons}~\cite{EGAM-2018-01} produce an identical EM shower but leave no track, as they carry no electric charge.
\textbf{Muons}~\cite{MUON-2021-03} are minimum-ionising and pass through the calorimeters largely unimpeded. 
They leave matched tracks in both the inner detector and the outermost muon spectrometer. 
\textbf{Jets} leave tracks from their charged constituents and deposit energy across both the EM and hadronic calorimeters. 
\textbf{Neutrinos} escape the detector entirely. 
Their presence is inferred from the transverse momentum imbalance they leave behind, reconstructed as missing transverse energy $E_{\text{T}}^{\text{miss}}$.

\subsection{The Tracking System}

The ATLAS Inner Detector (ID) reconstructs the trajectories of charged particles while minimally perturbing them. 
Every 25\,ns, hundreds of charged particles emerge from the interaction point within $|\eta| < 2.5$, demanding fine detector granularity and radiation-hard sensors. 
As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ATLAS_tracker}, the ID comprises three complementary subdetectors arranged concentrically outward from the beam axis: the silicon Pixel detector, the Semiconductor Tracker (SCT), and the Transition Radiation Tracker (TRT).

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/chapter3/ATLAS_track_hits.png}
	\caption{The ATLAS Inner Detector showing hits from a charged particle traversing the Pixel, SCT, and TRT subdetectors~\cite{atlas}.}
	\label{fig:ATLAS_tracker}
\end{figure}

The system is immersed in a 2\,T solenoidal magnetic field. 
This curves charged-particle trajectories in the transverse plane, allowing momenta to be inferred from the radius of curvature. 
The ID provides full azimuthal coverage over $|\eta| < 2.5$ with a nominal $p_{\text{T}}$ threshold of 0.5\,GeV. 
The momentum resolution is parameterized as:
%
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\sigma_{p_T}}{p_T} = 0.05\%\cdot p_{\text{T}}\,[\text{GeV}]
	\oplus 1\%,
	\label{eq:id_momentum_res}
\end{equation}
%
where $\oplus$ denotes addition in quadrature. 
The first term grows with $p_{\text{T}}$ as high-momentum tracks curve less; the second is a constant term from alignment and calibration uncertainties~\cite{atlas}.

\subsubsection{Pixel Detector}

The Pixel detector is the innermost component of the ID. 
It is positioned as close as 50.5\,mm from the beam axis in the barrel. 
Its fine granularity and proximity to the interaction point enable precise track impact parameter measurements. 
These are essential for reconstructing displaced secondary vertices from $b$-hadron decays, which is critical for $b$-jet identification and $\tau$-tagging.

The detector consists of 1744 silicon pixel modules arranged in three concentric barrel layers and two end-caps of three disks each~\cite{atlas}. 
The barrel layers span radii from 50.5\,mm to 122.5\,mm and the end-cap disks span 88.8\,mm to 149.6\,mm. 
Each pixel measures 50\,$\mu$m $\times$ 400\,$\mu$m in $R\phi$ and $z$ respectively, providing two-dimensional space-point measurements at each layer. 
The intrinsic accuracy is 10\,$\mu$m in $R\phi$ and 115\,$\mu$m in $z$. 
The detector has approximately 80.4 million readout channels in total.

The innermost barrel layer is the Insertable B-Layer (IBL)~\cite{Abbott:2018ikt}, added during Long Shutdown 1 between Run 1 and Run 2 at a radius of 33\,mm. 
The IBL improves impact parameter resolution and displaced vertex reconstruction, directly enhancing $b$-tagging performance.

\subsubsection{Semiconductor Tracker -- SCT}

The SCT extends precision silicon tracking to intermediate radii, providing additional measurements that complement the Pixel detector in track reconstruction~\cite{atlas}.
It consists of 4088 silicon strip modules arranged in four concentric barrel layers and two end-caps of nine disks each. 
The barrel layers span radii from 299\,mm to 514\,mm and the end-cap disks extend from 275\,mm to 560\,mm~\cite{atlas}.

Unlike pixels, strip sensors measure position in only one dimension. 
A strip is a long thin conductor, and a hit identifies which strip fired but not where along its length the particle crossed. 
To recover the second coordinate, each SCT module pairs two strip sensors at an angle.
One sensor is oriented parallel to the beam axis, the other is rotated by 40\,mrad. 
Combining hits from both sensors yields a two-dimensional space point. 
The strip pitch is approximately 80\,$\mu$m, giving an intrinsic accuracy of 17\,$\mu$m in $R\phi$ and 580\,$\mu$m in $z$. 
The poorer $z$ resolution relative to the Pixel detector is a direct consequence of this geometry. 
Strip sensors are cheaper to produce per unit area than pixel sensors, making them well suited to the larger radii of the SCT where lower track density makes coarser granularity acceptable. 
A hit is registered when the signal exceeds a threshold of 1\,fC, and the detector has approximately 6.3 million readout channels in total~\cite{atlas}.

\subsubsection{Transition Radiation Tracker -- TRT}

The TRT is the outermost component of the tracking system, covering radii from 554\,mm to 1082\,mm~\cite{atlas}.
It consists of 298,304 proportional drift tubes (straws) of 4\,mm diameter. 
The barrel is divided into three rings of 32 modules each, with straws arranged axially and interleaved with polypropylene fibers. 
In the end-cap regions, the straws are oriented radially. 
Each straw provides a drift-time measurement with an intrinsic accuracy of 130\,$\mu$m. 
All charged tracks with $p_{\text{T}} > 0.5$\,GeV and $|\eta| < 2.0$ traverse at least 36 straws, except in the transition region between the barrel and end-caps ($0.8 < |\eta| < 1.0$), where this decreases to a minimum of 22. 
The large number of measurements contributes to the overall track momentum resolution.

The TRT also provides electron identification through transition radiation. 
Transition radiation is emitted when a charged particle crosses a boundary of changing dielectric constant, producing X-ray photons. 
The number of photons produced is proportional to the Lorentz factor $\gamma = E/mc^2$. 
Electrons have a much higher $\gamma$ than pions at the same momentum due to their smaller mass, so they produce significantly more transition radiation than hadrons. 
These photons are absorbed by the Xe-based gas mixture and registered as high-threshold hits. 
The fraction of high-threshold hits along a track provides discrimination between electrons and pions~\cite{atlas}.

\subsection{The Calorimeter System}

The ATLAS calorimeter system surrounds the solenoid magnet, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:ATLAS_calorimeter}. 
Unlike the inner detector, which measures particle trajectories non-destructively, the calorimeters are designed to stop particles completely and absorb their total energy. 
Dense absorber material initiates a shower of secondary particles. 
An interleaved active medium then collects a representative sample of the deposited energy. 
The calorimeters respond to both charged and neutral particles, making them essential for reconstructing photons, jets, and missing transverse energy $E_{\text{T}}^{\text{miss}}$.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/chapter3/ATLAS_calorimeter.jpg}
	\caption{The ATLAS calorimeter system, comprising the electromagnetic (EMB, EMEC), hadronic (TileCal, HEC), and forward (FCal) calorimeters~\cite{atlas}.}
	\label{fig:ATLAS_calorimeter}
\end{figure}

The system provides full azimuthal coverage over $|\eta| < 4.9$ and is divided into three regions: the electromagnetic (EM) calorimeter, the hadronic calorimeter, and the forward calorimeter (FCal). 
The total depth exceeds 10 hadronic interaction lengths $\lambda$, ensuring shower containment and minimizing hadronic leakage into the muon spectrometer~\cite{atlas}.

The majority of ATLAS calorimeters use liquid argon (LAr) as the active medium. 
LAr was chosen for its intrinsic linear response, stable behavior over time, and radiation hardness. 
These properties are critical given the high particle flux at the LHC. 
It is used in the electromagnetic barrel and end-cap calorimeters, the hadronic end-cap, and the forward calorimeter. 
The hadronic barrel calorimeter (TileCal) uses scintillating plastic tiles instead, which is more cost-effective at the larger radii where the radiation environment is less severe. 

\subsubsection{Electromagnetic Calorimeter}

Electrons and photons lose energy in matter primarily through bremsstrahlung and pair production, producing compact cascades known as electromagnetic showers. 
Lead is used as the absorber material because its short radiation length ($X_0 = 0.56$\,cm)~\cite{PDG2022} allows full shower containment within a manageable depth. 
Fine spatial granularity is also required to resolve nearby clusters and identify individual photons.

The lead absorber plates and LAr electrodes are shaped into an accordion fold, alternating between axial and radial orientations in the barrel and end-caps respectively. 
This eliminates uninstrumented gaps in $\phi$, ensuring every particle traverses active material regardless of azimuthal angle. 
The calorimeter is divided into the LAr electromagnetic barrel (EMB), covering $|\eta| < 1.475$, and the LAr electromagnetic end-cap (EMEC), covering $1.375 < |\eta| < 3.2$. 
The total active thickness exceeds $22\,X_0$ in the barrel and $24\,X_0$ in the end-caps~\cite{atlas}.

The energy resolution of the EMB is parameterized as:
%
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\sigma_E}{E} \approx \frac{10\%}{\sqrt{E\,[\text{GeV}]}} \oplus 0.17\%,
	\label{eq:em_resolution}
\end{equation}
%
where the stochastic term reflects statistical fluctuations in shower sampling, and the constant term encodes non-uniformities and calibration uncertainties~\cite{atlas}.

\subsubsection{Hadronic Calorimeter}

Hadronic showers are broader and deeper than electromagnetic showers, and exhibit larger intrinsic fluctuations in energy deposition. 
The hadronic interaction length in iron ($\lambda \approx 16.77$\,cm) is nearly ten times larger than the radiation length ($X_0 \approx 1.757$\,cm)~\cite{PDG2022}, so hadronic calorimeters must be physically much larger to achieve comparable shower containment.
% PDG 2024: https://pdg.lbl.gov/2024/AtomicNuclearProperties/HTML/iron_Fe.html

The hadronic calorimeter is divided into two components. 
The Tile calorimeter (TileCal) covers $|\eta| < 1.7$ using steel absorber plates with scintillating plastic tiles as the active medium, providing approximately $7.4\,\lambda$ of depth~\cite{atlas}. 
Steel and scintillator are well suited to the large volume at these radii, where the radiation environment is less severe and cost-effective construction is important.
The LAr hadronic end-cap calorimeter (HEC) extends coverage to $1.5 < |\eta| < 3.2$ using copper absorber plates interleaved with LAr gaps. 
The HEC shares the end-cap cryostats with the EMEC, and its overlap with TileCal in the range $1.5 < |\eta| < 1.7$ ensures continuous coverage across the barrel-to-end-cap transition.

The combined hadronic energy resolution is parameterized as:
%
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\sigma_E}{E} \approx \frac{50\%}{\sqrt{E\,[\text{GeV}]}} \oplus 3\%,
	\label{eq:had_resolution}
\end{equation}
%
where the large stochastic term reflects the irreducible fluctuations in hadronic shower development. 
The constant term encodes calibration uncertainties and non-compensation effects~\cite{atlas}.

\subsubsection{Forward Calorimeter}

The region close to the beam axis is exposed to an extremely high particle flux, demanding a compact, high-density detector design.
Extending calorimetric coverage into this region is essential for reliable $E_{\text{T}}^{\text{miss}}$ reconstruction and for forward jet reconstruction in processes such as vector boson fusion (VBF), discussed further in Chapter~5.

The LAr forward calorimeter (FCal) extends coverage to $3.1 < |\eta| < 4.9$, housed in the same end-cap cryostats as the EMEC and HEC. 
The FCal consists of three longitudinal modules, approximately $10\,\lambda$ deep in total. 
The first module uses copper absorbers optimized for electromagnetic measurements. 
The second and third modules use tungsten absorbers, which provide the density needed to contain hadronic showers in the limited available space.

The electromagnetic energy resolution is parameterized as:
%
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\sigma_E}{E} \approx \frac{28.5\%}{\sqrt{E\,[\text{GeV}]}} \oplus 3.5\%,
	\label{eq:fcal_em_resolution}
\end{equation}
%
and the hadronic energy resolution as:
%
\begin{equation}
	\frac{\sigma_E}{E} \approx \frac{94\%}{\sqrt{E\,[\text{GeV}]}} \oplus 7.5\%,
	\label{eq:fcal_had_resolution}
\end{equation}
%
as measured in test-beam campaigns at the CERN SPS~\cite{atlas}.

\subsection{Muon Spectrometer}

Muons are minimum-ionising particles that pass through the calorimeters with little energy loss and exit the detector largely intact. 
Unlike electrons and hadrons, they cannot be measured by the calorimeters alone. 
A dedicated outer tracking system is therefore required to measure their momentum and identify them as clean signatures. 
Precise muon reconstruction was a key design goal of ATLAS, motivated in part by Higgs boson searches in the $H \to ZZ \to 4\ell$ decay channel.

The muon spectrometer~\cite{atlas} uses a system of air-core toroid magnets to bend muon tracks for momentum measurement. 
One barrel toroid and two end-cap toroids provide the bending field, distinct from the solenoid used by the inner detector. 
Three concentric cylindrical layers of chambers in the barrel, at radii of approximately 5, 7.5, and 10\,m, together with end-cap wheel structures, provide coverage over $|\eta| < 2.7$. 
The curvature of the track across these three layers yields a sagitta measurement from which the muon momentum is derived.

The spectrometer uses two classes of detector technology. 
Monitored drift tube (MDT) chambers provide precision tracking over $|\eta| < 2.7$, achieving a spatial resolution of approximately 35\,$\mu$m per chamber. 
Cathode strip chambers (CSC) are used in the innermost end-cap layer for $2.0 < |\eta| < 2.7$, where the higher particle flux demands faster response. 
Resistive plate chambers (RPC) in the barrel and thin-gap chambers (TGC) in the end-caps provide fast trigger signals within a few tens of nanoseconds, covering $|\eta| < 2.4$. 
The standalone transverse momentum resolution is approximately 10\% at 1\,TeV, while combined reconstruction with the inner detector significantly improves resolution at lower momenta.

\subsection{Trigger and Data Acquisition Systems}

The LHC delivers proton-proton bunch crossings at a rate of 40\,MHz~\cite{atlas}. 
At LHC energies, the total inelastic cross-section is many orders of magnitude larger than the cross-sections for processes of physics interest. 
Higgs boson production, for example, is suppressed by a factor of approximately $10^9$ relative to the total multijet rate. 
The vast majority of collisions are therefore soft QCD processes with no hard-scatter content. 
Recording every collision is not feasible. 
Each event represents several megabytes of raw detector data, and storing output at the full 40\,MHz rate would require a data bandwidth far beyond what is technically achievable. 
A trigger system is therefore required to identify the small fraction of events containing signatures of physics interest and discard the rest in real time.

The ATLAS trigger and data acquisition (TDAQ) system~\cite{atlas} operates in two stages. 
The Level-1 (L1) trigger is implemented in custom hardware. 
It uses reduced-granularity information from the calorimeters and muon trigger chambers to search for candidate high-$p_T$ objects: electrons, photons, muons, jets, $\tau$-leptons, and events with large missing transverse energy. 
The L1 decision is formed within a fixed latency of 2.5\,$\mu$s and reduces the event rate to approximately 100\,kHz~\cite{ATLASTriggerRun2}. 
For each accepted event, the L1 system records the detector coordinates of candidate objects as regions of interest. 
These seed the second stage: the High Level Trigger (HLT). 
The HLT runs software-based reconstruction algorithms using the full detector granularity. 
Inner detector tracking is included at this stage, enabling particle identification beyond what coarse calorimeter information alone can provide. 
The HLT reduces the output rate to approximately 1\,kHz for recording to permanent storage~\cite{ATLASTriggerRun2}.

The selection criteria applied at each stage are encoded in a trigger menu. 
The menu contains hundreds of distinct trigger chains, each targeting a specific physics signature, calibration dataset, or monitoring stream. 
Further offline reconstruction techniques are described in the following section.

\section{Object Reconstruction}

Raw detector output consists of digitized signals across the tracking, calorimeter, and muon systems. 
The goal of event reconstruction is to convert these signals into the physics objects used in every ATLAS analysis. 
This section describes two foundational algorithms.
Track reconstruction converts inner detector signals into charged-particle trajectories.
Jet reconstruction clusters calorimeter and tracking signals into objects originating from hard-scatter hadrons.
Both provide inputs to downstream identification of $b$-jets, electrons, photons, muons, and missing transverse energy ($E_{\mathrm{T}}^{\mathrm{miss}}$).
$E_{\mathrm{T}}^{\mathrm{miss}}$ is computed as the magnitude of the negative vector sum of the transverse momenta of all calibrated physics objects, with an additional soft term derived from inner detector tracks not associated to any reconstructed object~\cite{ATLASMETRun2}.
It serves as the primary proxy for invisible particles such as neutrinos or BSM candidates that escape the detector without interaction.

%--------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Track Reconstruction}
\label{sec:track_reco}
%--------------------------------------------------------------

Charged particles traversing the inner detector deposit ionization signals across the pixel and strip layers. 
Track reconstruction converts these raw signals into measurements of charged-particle trajectories, each parameterized by five quantities:
%
\begin{equation}
	\boldsymbol{\tau} = (d_0,\, z_0,\, \phi,\, \theta,\, q/p),
	\label{eq:track_params}
\end{equation}
%
where $d_0$ and $z_0$ are the transverse and longitudinal impact parameters with respect to the primary vertex, $\phi$ and $\theta$ are the azimuthal and polar angles at the point of closest approach, and $q/p$ is the signed inverse momentum.
Reconstructed tracks form the basis of primary vertex reconstruction, $b$-jet identification, and the Particle Flow jet algorithm.

Track Reconstruction in Run 3 employs two complementary strategies, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:track_reco_chain}. 
\textbf{Primary tracking} proceeds inside-out. 
Raw channel signals are converted into three-dimensional space-points.
Triplets of space-points in the innermost layers seed track candidates, which are extended outward through successive layers using a Kalman filter~\cite{Fruhwirth:1987fm} that accounts for the magnetic field, energy loss, and multiple Coulomb scattering. 
An ambiguity solver scores competing candidates on hit count and fit quality, rejecting fakes and resolving shared-hit conflicts. 
Accepted tracks are extended into the TRT and globally refit. 
\textbf{Back-tracking} proceeds outside-in, seeding from TRT segments and extending inward through the silicon detectors, recovering tracks from photon conversions and long-lived particle decays.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/chapter3/track_reconstruction_run3.png}
	\caption{The ATLAS track reconstruction chain~\cite{ATLASTrackRecoRun3, ATLASTrackRecoDense}.
		Primary tracking (top) proceeds inside-out from space-point
		formation through seed finding, Kalman-filter track extension,
		ambiguity resolution, and TRT refit. Back-tracking (bottom)
		proceeds outside-in, seeding from TRT segments and extending inward.}
	\label{fig:track_reco_chain}
\end{figure}

Reconstructed tracks serve as inputs to primary vertex reconstruction~\cite{ATLASPrimaryVertexRun1}. 
The hard-scatter primary vertex is selected as the vertex with the largest $\sum p_{\mathrm{T}}^2$ of associated tracks. 
Correct vertex-track association underpins pile-up suppression in all downstream reconstruction, including the Particle Flow algorithm described in Section~\ref{sec:jet_reco}.

%--------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Jet Reconstruction}
\label{sec:jet_reco}
%--------------------------------------------------------------

Quarks and gluons produced in hard-scatter interactions cannot be observed directly due to color confinement, and instead hadronize into collimated sprays of stable particles known as \textit{jets}. 
Jets serve as proxies for hard-scatter partons and are central to a wide range of ATLAS analyses. 
A jet algorithm must be infrared- and collinear-safe (IRC-safe), meaning insensitive to soft wide-angle radiation and to the collinear splitting of hard partons~\cite{Cacciari_2008, Salam_2010}.
Jet reconstruction in ATLAS proceeds in stages: calorimeter signals are first grouped into topological clusters, combined with inner detector tracks via the Particle Flow algorithm, and then clustered into jets.

%--------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Topological Clustering -- Step 1}
%--------------------------------------------------------------

The baseline inputs to jet reconstruction are \textit{topological clusters} (topo-clusters)~\cite{ATLASTopoCluster}, which group calorimeter cells according to their signal significance $\varsigma = |E_{\text{cell}}|/\sigma_{\text{noise}}$. 
Cluster formation follows a seeded growth algorithm: cells with $\varsigma > 4$ seed clusters, neighboring cells with $\varsigma > 2$ are iteratively added, and a final ring of cells with $\varsigma > 0$ captures the full shower extent. 
This \textit{4-2-0} scheme suppresses electronic noise and pile-up while retaining genuine shower energy, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:topo_cluster}. 
Topo-clusters are formed at the electromagnetic energy scale and may be further corrected to the hadronic scale using Local Cell Weighting (LCW)~\cite{ATLASTopoCluster}, which classifies each cluster as electromagnetic or hadronic based on shower shape and applies scale corrections accordingly.

%--------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Particle Flow and UFO Jets -- Step 2}
%--------------------------------------------------------------

Inner detector tracking provides a more precise momentum measurement for charged particles at low transverse momenta, where hadronic calorimeter resolution degrades.
The \textbf{Particle Flow} (PFlow) algorithm~\cite{ATLASParticleFlow} exploits this by matching inner detector tracks to topo-clusters, subtracting the expected calorimeter deposit cell-by-cell, and retaining the track momentum for matched charged particles.
The resulting \textit{particle flow objects} (PFOs) combine charged-particle tracks with residual topo-clusters for neutral particles, yielding improved jet energy and angular resolution.
The algorithm is summarized in Figure~\ref{fig:ATLAS:particles}.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/chapter3/ATLAS_pflow.png}
	\caption{Diagram of the ATLAS Particle Flow algorithm~\cite{ATLASParticleFlow}.
		Tracks are matched to topo-clusters, the expected calorimeter deposit is
		subtracted cell-by-cell. The outputs are tracks for charged particles and
		residual clusters for neutral particles.}
	\label{fig:ATLAS:particles}
\end{figure}

For large-radius jets used in boosted topology searches, \textbf{Unified Flow Objects} (UFOs)~\cite{ATLASLargeRJet} extend PFlow by combining PFOs with additional inner detector tracks, improving reconstruction of the substructure of highly boosted $W$, $Z$, Higgs, and top quark decays.
UFO jets with $R = 1.0$ are the standard large-radius jet input for boosted object taggers in ATLAS Run~3 analyses.

%--------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{The Anti-$k_t$ Algorithm -- Step 3}
%--------------------------------------------------------------

All jets in ATLAS are reconstructed using the \textit{anti-}$k_t$ algorithm~\cite{Cacciari_2008}, which clusters inputs according to the distance measure
%
\begin{equation}
	d_{ij} = \min\!\left(k_{ti}^{-2},\, k_{tj}^{-2}\right)
	\frac{\Delta R_{ij}^2}{R^2},
	\label{eq:antikt}
\end{equation}
%
where $k_{ti}$ is the transverse momentum of object $i$, $\Delta R_{ij}$ is their angular separation, and $R$ is the radius parameter. 
Hard particles dominate the clustering, causing soft particles to accumulate around them and producing geometrically regular, cone-like jets in the $\eta$--$\phi$ plane. This leads to IRC-safe clustering behavior.
In ATLAS, small-R jets use $R = 0.4$ and large-R jets use $R = 1.0$.

%--------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Jet Energy Calibration -- Step 4}
%--------------------------------------------------------------

Reconstructed jets are defined at the \textit{electromagnetic energy scale}, from which a sequential calibration chain restores the jet energy scale (JES) to the stable-particle level~\cite{ATLASJESCalib}. 
An area-based correction first subtracts the average energy density from pile-up interactions. 
A simulation-based correction then accounts for detector non-uniformity, hadronization effects, and out-of-cone energy losses. 
Finally, \textit{in situ} techniques balance the jet $p_{\mathrm{T}}$ against a well-measured reference object (a photon, $Z$ boson, or multijet system) to validate and correct the JES in data. 
Calibrated jets form the inputs to all downstream reconstruction and analysis steps described in this thesis.
