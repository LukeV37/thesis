\section{The Standard Model}

The Standard Model (SM) is the theoretical framework that describes all known fundamental particles and three of the four fundamental forces of nature except gravity. 
The SM organizes its particles into two broad categories: fermions and bosons. 
Fermions are particles with half-integer spin and make up the matter content of the universe. 
Bosons are particles with integer spin and are responsible for mediating the fundamental forces.

The fermions are divided into two families: quarks and leptons, each arranged into three generations of increasing mass. 
All ordinary matter is composed of the lightest and most stable first-generation particles. 
Quarks carry electric charge, weak charge, and color charge, and therefore interact via all three SM forces.
Due to color confinement, quarks are never observed in isolation and are always found confined inside composite particles called hadrons.
The leptons follow the same three-generation pattern, with the electron, muon, and tau each paired with a corresponding neutrino. 
Leptons carry electric and weak charge but no color charge. 
Neutrinos carry only weak charge and interact exclusively through the weak force.

The force carriers are spin-1 gauge bosons. 
The massless photon mediates the electromagnetic force. 
The $W^\pm$ and $Z$ bosons mediate the weak force and are massive, at 80 and 91 GeV, respectively. 
Eight massless gluons mediate the strong force. 
The Higgs boson, with mass of 125 GeV~\cite{PDG2022}, is distinct from the gauge bosons as it is the only spin-0 fundamental particle and does not mediate a force. 
Instead it plays a central role in mass generation, as described in Section~\ref{sec:higgs_mechanism}. 
The Standard Model is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:SM}.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/chapter2/SM.jpg}
	\caption{The fundamental particles organized by generation and type.}
	\label{fig:SM}
\end{figure}

In essence, the SM is a theory explaining the underlying symmetries of nature. 
A symmetry in physics is an invariance under some transformation. 
A classical example being translational symmetry, where physics does not depend on where an experiment is performed. 
In the SM, the relevant symmetries are gauge symmetries, also called local symmetries. 
To understand the distinction between a global and a local symmetry, consider a vector field $\vec{\psi}(x)$ spread across space. 
A global symmetry $\alpha$ is like rotating every vector by the same fixed angle simultaneously, $\vec{\psi}(x) \rightarrow \alpha \cdot \vec{\psi}(x)$. 
A gauge, or local, symmetry $\alpha(x)$ is more constraining: each vector is rotated by a different angle at each point in space independently, $\vec{\psi}(x) \rightarrow \alpha(x) \cdot \vec{\psi}(x)$. 
In the language of quantum field theory, this corresponds to a local phase rotation, also called a U(1) transformation, of the fermion field:%~\cite{Peskin:1995ev}
\begin{equation}
    \psi(x) \rightarrow e^{i\alpha(x)}\psi(x).
    \label{eq:U1_gauge_transform}
\end{equation}
For the physics to remain consistent under this position-dependent transformation, a new field $A_\mu(x)$ must be introduced via the covariant derivative,
\begin{equation}
    D_\mu = \partial_\mu + igA_\mu(x),
    \label{eq:covariant_derivative}
\end{equation}
where $g$ is the coupling strength. 
The field $A_\mu$ is precisely a gauge boson. 
In this way, the forces of nature emerge as a local gauge symmetry.

The SM Lagrangian is invariant under the gauge symmetry group $\text{SU}(3)_C \times \text{SU}(2)_L \times \text{U}(1)_Y$. 
The $\text{SU}(3)_C$ symmetry governs the strong force, with the subscript $C$ denoting color charge; its generators, the Gell-Mann matrices, correspond to the eight gluons. 
The $\text{SU}(2)_L \times \text{U}(1)_Y$ symmetry governs the electroweak force, with generators given by the three Pauli matrices and a single hypercharge generator $Y$. 
The subscript $L$ indicates that the weak interaction acts only on left-handed fermions as experimentally observed by Wu et al.~\cite{Wu:1957}.
% DUMMY CITE KEY — Wu:1957: C.S. Wu et al., Phys. Rev. 105, 1413 (1957)
% NOTE: Why does SU(2)_L only interact with left-handed fermions?
% This is an experimental observation, not derived from a deeper principle.
% The 1956 Wu experiment showed beta decay violates parity; subsequent work
% established the weak interaction is V-A (vector minus axial-vector), which
% projects onto left-handed chirality only. The SM encodes this by placing
% left-handed fermions in SU(2)_L doublets (e.g. (nu_e, e^-)_L, (u,d)_L)
% and right-handed fermions in singlets (e_R, u_R, d_R). Singlets do not
% transform under SU(2)_L, so the W bosons do not appear in their covariant
% derivative — right-handed fermions are simply invisible to the weak force.
% Note: the weak force couples to chirality (the gamma^5 eigenvalue), which
% coincides with helicity only for massless particles.
% Right-handed neutrinos do not appear in the SM at all as a consequence.
% The SM offers no explanation for WHY the weak force is chiral — it is an input. 
Together these produce four gauge bosons: the $W^+$, $W^-$, $W^0$, and $B^0$. 
After electroweak symmetry breaking, discussed in Section~\ref{sec:higgs_mechanism}, these mix to form the physical $W^\pm$, $Z$, and $\gamma$.

The dynamics of all SM particles and their interactions are encoded in the SM Lagrangian density~\cite{PDG2022}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{SM} =
  \underbrace{-\frac{1}{4}F^{a}_{\mu\nu}F^{a\mu\nu}}_{\text{gauge kinetic}}
  + \underbrace{i\bar{\psi} \gamma^\mu D_\mu \psi}_{\text{fermion kinetic}}
  + \underbrace{|D_\mu\phi|^2 - V(\phi)}_{\text{Higgs sector}}
  + \underbrace{y_{ij}\bar{\psi}_{Li}\phi\psi_{Rj} + \text{h.c.}}_{\text{Yukawa}}
  \label{eq:SMlagrangian}
\end{equation}
% Note on indices:
% F^a_{\mu\nu}: a = gauge group generator index (summed over SU(3), SU(2), U(1)); mu,nu = Lorentz indices
% psi: implicit sum over all fermion flavors; mu = Lorentz index on gamma matrix
% D_mu phi: covariant derivative of Higgs doublet; |...|^2 = (D_mu phi)^dag (D^mu phi)
% y_{mn}: 3x3 Yukawa matrix; m,n = generation indices (chosen to avoid clash with imaginary unit i)
% Reference: PDG Review, Workman et al. PTEP 2022, 083C01 (Electroweak Model chapter)

The gauge kinetic term $-\frac{1}{4}F^a_{\mu\nu}F^{a\mu\nu}$ governs the propagation and self-interactions of all gauge bosons, where the index $a$ runs over all generators of the gauge group. 
The fermion kinetic term $i\bar{\psi}\gamma^\mu D_\mu\psi$ describes how fermions propagate and couple to the gauge fields through the covariant derivative $D_\mu$. 
The Higgs sector $|D_\mu\phi|^2 - V(\phi)$ contains the Higgs field and its couplings to the gauge bosons; the potential $V(\phi)$ drives the spontaneous symmetry breaking discussed in Section~\ref{sec:higgs_mechanism}. 
Finally, the Yukawa term $y_{ij}\bar{\psi}_{Li}\phi\psi_{Rj}$ couples fermions to the Higgs field, generating fermion masses after symmetry breaking, as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:yukawa}.

\section{The Higgs Mechanism and Gauge Boson Mass Generation}
\label{sec:higgs_mechanism}

% ------------------------------------------------------------
% OUTLINE FOR THIS SECTION:
% Para 1 — The mass problem
%   - SM gauge invariance forbids explicit mass terms for W, Z
%   - Explicit m_W^2 W_mu W^mu breaks gauge invariance
%   - Yet W (~80 GeV) and Z (~91 GeV) are observed to be massive
% Para 2 — The Higgs potential and spontaneous symmetry breaking
%   - Introduce complex scalar doublet phi with V(phi) = mu^2|phi|^2 + lambda|phi|^4
%   - For mu^2 < 0: unstable at origin, degenerate ring of minima at |phi| = v/sqrt(2)
%   - v ~ 246 GeV is the vacuum expectation value
%   - Field settles into one minimum: SSB of SU(2)_L x U(1)_Y -> U(1)_EM
%   - Reference figure: Higgs potential (fig:HiggsPotential)
% Para 3 — Consequences of SSB: gauge boson masses and the Higgs boson
%   - Three broken generators -> three Goldstone bosons
%   - Goldstone bosons absorbed by W+, W-, Z -> longitudinal modes -> mass
%   - Photon corresponds to unbroken generator -> remains massless
%   - Remaining physical DOF: real scalar Higgs boson h
%   - Optional: show W and Z mass formulae m_W = gv/2, m_Z = sqrt(g^2+g'^2)v/2
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The gauge symmetry of the SM places a strict constraint on the Lagrangian in which explicit mass terms for the gauge bosons, of the form $m_W^2 W_\mu W^\mu$, break gauge invariance and are therefore forbidden. 
The theory predicts that the $W$ and $Z$ bosons should be massless, analogous to the photon.
However, the $W$ and $Z$ bosons are experimentally observed to be massive at 80 and 91 GeV, respectively. 
This contradiction points to a missing mechanism, one that generates gauge boson masses without introducing explicit mass terms and without breaking the gauge invariance the SM is built upon.

The solution is provided by the Brout-Englert-Higgs mechanism~\cite{EnglertBrout1964, Higgs1964}.
This mechanism introduces a complex scalar field $\phi$, called the Higgs field, with the potential $V(\phi) = \mu^2|\phi|^2 + \lambda|\phi|^4$, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:HiggsPotential}. 
For $\mu^2 < 0$, the origin is an unstable maximum and the true minima form a degenerate ring at $|\phi| = v/\sqrt{2}$, where $v \approx 246$~GeV is the vacuum expectation value (VEV). 
The system must settle into a single point on this ring, spontaneously breaking $\text{SU}(2)_L \times \text{U}(1)_Y \rightarrow \text{U}(1)_{EM}$ and leaving electromagnetism as the unbroken symmetry of the vacuum.
This is the essence of spontaneous symmetry breaking (SSB): the Lagrangian retains its full gauge symmetry, but the ground state of the Higgs field does not.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figures/chapter2/higgs_potential.png}
	\caption{The Higgs potential $V(\phi) = \mu^2|\phi|^2 + \lambda|\phi|^4$ for $\mu^2 < 0$.
	Point A is the unstable maximum at $\phi = 0$. Point B is a stable minimum at $|\phi| = v/\sqrt{2}$.
	The ground state spontaneously selects one minimum, breaking $\text{SU}(2)_L \times \text{U}(1)_Y \rightarrow \text{U}(1)_\text{EM}$.}
	\label{fig:HiggsPotential}
\end{figure}

Before SSB, the four gauge fields $W^+$, $W^-$, $W^0$, and $B^0$ generated by $\text{SU}(2)_L \times \text{U}(1)_Y$ do not directly become the physical particles observed in experiment. 
After SSB, three of the four electroweak generators are broken, each producing a massless Goldstone boson that is absorbed as a longitudinal polarization mode by $W^+$, $W^-$, and a mixture of $W^0$ and $B^0$, giving the $W^+$, $W^-$, and $Z$ bosons mass.%~\cite{Peskin:1995ev} 
The remaining orthogonal mixture of $W^0$ and $B^0$ corresponds to the unbroken $\text{U}(1)_{EM}$ generator and becomes the massless photon $\gamma$. 
The resulting masses $m_W = gv/2$ and $m_Z = \sqrt{g^2 + g'^2}\,v/2$ are determined entirely by the VEV and reproduce the observed values~\cite{PDG2022}. 
One physical particle emerges from this process: the Higgs boson, $h$, representing oscillations of the field about the potential minimum.
Its discovery and properties are discussed in Section~\ref{sec:higgs_production}.
While the Higgs mechanism accounts for gauge boson masses, fermion masses require a further ingredient, discussed in Section~\ref{sec:yukawa}.

\section{Yukawa Couplings and Fermion Mass Generation}
\label{sec:yukawa}

% ------------------------------------------------------------
% OUTLINE FOR THIS SECTION:
% Para 1 — Fermion mass generation via Yukawa interactions
%   - Gauge invariance also forbids explicit fermion mass terms before SSB
%   - Yukawa term: L_Y = -y_f psi_L phi psi_R + h.c.
%   - After SSB: m_f = y_f * v / sqrt(2)
%   - Key SM prediction: Higgs coupling to any fermion proportional to its mass
% Para 2 — Experimental status of Yukawa couplings
%   - Third-generation (t, b, tau) measured, consistent with SM
%   - Top quark special: m_t ~ 173 GeV -> y_t ~ 1, strongest Higgs coupling
%   - Introduce kappa-framework: kappa_f = y_f / y_f^SM as BSM probe
%   - Reference figure: ATLAS coupling vs mass plot (fig:HiggsCouplings)
% Para 3 — Second-generation gap and thesis motivation
%   - Charm and muon Yukawa couplings largely unmeasured
%   - Many BSM models predict enhanced kappa_c
%   - VH(H->cc) analysis in this thesis directly probes y_c
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Just as gauge invariance forbids explicit mass terms for the gauge bosons, it also forbids explicit mass terms for fermions. 
Fermion masses are instead generated through Yukawa interactions, where each fermion couples to the Higgs field via the term $\mathcal{L}_Y = -y_f \bar{\psi}_L \phi \psi_R + \text{h.c.}$.%~\cite{Peskin:1995ev}
After SSB, each fermion obtains a mass $m_f = y_f v/\sqrt{2}$, proportional to the VEV, and the SM predicts that the Higgs coupling to any fermion is directly proportional to its mass.

The $\kappa$-framework provides a convenient way to test this prediction~\cite{LHCHiggsXSWG3}.
% DUMMY CITE KEY — LHCHiggsXSWG3: LHC Higgs XS WG Yellow Report 3, arXiv:1307.1347
Each coupling modifier $\kappa_f$ is defined as the ratio of the measured coupling to the SM prediction, so that $\kappa_f = 1$ corresponds to the SM.
Figure~\ref{fig:HiggsCouplings} shows the measured coupling modifiers as a function of particle mass. 
The red line shows the SM prediction. 
Third-generation couplings to the top quark, bottom quark, and tau lepton have been measured and are consistent with the SM~\cite{ATLASHiggsTenYears}.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{figures/chapter2/higgs_couplings.png}
	\caption{Reduced Higgs boson coupling strength modifiers as a function of particle mass, measured
		by ATLAS with the full Run 2 dataset~\cite{ATLASHiggsTenYears}. The red line shows the SM
		prediction that the Higgs coupling scales linearly with mass. % TODO: replace cite key ATLASHiggsTenYears with correct key for arXiv:2207.00092
	}
	\label{fig:HiggsCouplings}
\end{figure}

The second-generation couplings tell a different story. 
The charm quark appears with large uncertainties and the muon coupling is only weakly constrained. 
These couplings remain largely untested. 
Many BSM models predict enhanced second-generation couplings, making them sensitive probes of new physics~\cite{BSMCharmCouplings}.
% DUMMY CITE KEY — BSMCharmCouplings: replace with relevant BSM reference(s)
This thesis contributes to efforts dedicated to measure the charm Yukawa coupling through the $VH(H \rightarrow c\bar{c})$ and $\text{VBF}(H \rightarrow c\bar{c})$ channels, described in Sections~\ref{sec:VH_analysis} and~\ref{sec:VBF_analysis}.

The Higgs boson was discovered by ATLAS and CMS in 2012 using approximately 10~fb$^{-1}$ of proton-proton collision data at $\sqrt{s} = 7$--8~TeV~\cite{ATLASDiscovery, CMSDiscovery}.
The observation was made in two decay channels: $H \rightarrow \gamma\gamma$ and $H \rightarrow ZZ^* \rightarrow 4\ell$, the latter known as the golden channel due to its clean four-lepton final state and excellent mass resolution.
The Higgs boson mass was measured to be $m_H \approx 125$~GeV.
Subsequent measurements confirmed spin-0 and CP-even quantum numbers~\cite{ATLASSpinCP, CMSSpinCP}.

All coupling measurements to date are consistent with SM predictions~\cite{ATLASHiggsTenYears}.

The dominant decay is $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ at 58\%, followed by $H \rightarrow WW^*$ at 21\%.
The $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ branching ratio is 2.9\% and has not yet been observed directly.
Both $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ and $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ are overwhelmed by QCD multijet backgrounds in inclusive searches.
The VH production mode provides trigger level suppression through lepton or MET requirements through the associated $W$ or $Z$ boson and strongly suppresses QCD backgrounds.
The VBF mode provides an additional handle through its characteristic signature of two energetic forward jets.

The SM is remarkably successful, yet it leaves several fundamental questions unanswered. 
It provides no explanation for the three-generation structure of quarks and leptons, nor for the large mass hierarchies between generations.
Gravity is not incorporated into the SM framework. 
The SM offers no candidate for dark matter, which accounts for approximately 27\% of the energy content of the universe~\cite{Planck:2018vyg}. 
The observed matter-antimatter asymmetry of the universe also remains unexplained~\cite{Sakharov:1967dj}. 
In the neutrino sector, the SM predicts massless neutrinos, however, neutrino oscillation experiments have conclusively demonstrated that neutrinos have nonzero mass~\cite{Fukuda:1998mi, Ahmad:2002jz}, in direct contradiction with the SM. 
The SM contains no right-handed neutrinos, leaving no natural mechanism to generate their masses. 
The hierarchy problem presents a further theoretical tension: quantum corrections drive $m_H$ toward the Planck scale ($\sim 10^{19}$~GeV), requiring fine-tuned cancellations absent new physics at the TeV scale~\cite{Giudice:2008bi}.

Beyond these open problems, the LHC opens an entirely new frontier: probing quantum mechanical phenomena such as spin entanglement at collider energies.
Top quark pair production provides a uniquely clean system for this, as the top quark decays before hadronization and its spin information is preserved in its decay products. 
This thesis introduces a new machine learning approach to top polarimetry to target top quark entanglement at the LHC, described in Chapter~\ref{chap:top_polarimetry}.
