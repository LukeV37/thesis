\section{The Standard Model}

The Standard Model (SM) is the theoretical framework that describes all
known fundamental particles and three of the four fundamental forces of
nature -- except gravity. The SM organizes its particles into two broad categories: fermions and bosons.
Fermions are particles with half-integer spin and make up the matter
content of the universe. Bosons are particles with integer spin and
are responsible for mediating the fundamental forces.

The fermions are divided into two families: quarks and leptons, each
arranged into three generations of increasing mass. All ordinary matter is composed
of the lightest and most stable first-generation particles. Quarks carry electric charge, weak charge, and color charge, and therefore
interact with all three SM forces. Due to color confinement, quarks are never observed in isolation
and are always found confined inside composite particles called hadrons.
The leptons follow the same three-generation pattern, with the electron,
muon, and tau each paired with a corresponding neutrino. Leptons carry
electric and weak charge but no color charge. Neutrinos carry only weak
charge and interact exclusively through the weak force.

The force carriers are spin-1 gauge bosons. The massless photon mediates the
electromagnetic force. The $W^\pm$ and $Z$ bosons mediate
the weak force and are massive, at 80 and 91 GeV, respectively. Eight massless gluons mediate the strong force. The Higgs boson, with mass of 125 GeV, is distinct from the gauge bosons as it is the
only spin-0 fundamental particle and does not mediate a force. Instead it
plays a central role in mass generation, as described in
Section~\ref{sec:higgs_mechanism}. The Standard Model is shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:SM}.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/chapter2/SM.jpg}
	\caption{The fundamental particles organized by generation and type.}
	\label{fig:SM}
\end{figure}

In essence, the SM is a theory explaining the underlying symmetries of
nature. A symmetry in physics is an invariance under some transformation. A classical example being translational symmetry, where physics does not
depend on where an experiment is performed. In the SM, the relevant
symmetries are gauge symmetries, also called local symmetries. To
understand the distinction between a global and a local symmetry, consider a vector field $\vec{\psi}(x)$ spread across space. A global symmetry $\alpha$ is like
rotating every vector by the same fixed angle simultaneously,
$\vec{\psi}(x) \rightarrow \alpha \cdot \vec{\psi}(x)$. A gauge, or
local, symmetry $\alpha(x)$ is more constraining: each vector is rotated
by a different angle at each point in space independently,
$\vec{\psi}(x) \rightarrow \alpha(x) \cdot \vec{\psi}(x)$. In the
language of quantum field theory, this corresponds to a local phase
rotation, also called a U(1) transformation, of the fermion field~\cite{Peskin:1995ev}:
\begin{equation}
    \psi(x) \rightarrow e^{i\alpha(x)}\psi(x).
    \label{eq:U1_gauge_transform}
\end{equation}
For the physics to remain consistent under this position-dependent
transformation, a new field $A_\mu(x)$ must be introduced via the
covariant derivative,
\begin{equation}
    D_\mu = \partial_\mu + igA_\mu(x),
    \label{eq:covariant_derivative}
\end{equation}
where $g$ is the coupling strength. The field $A_\mu$ is precisely a gauge
boson. In this way, the
forces of nature emerge as a local gauge
symmetry.

The SM Lagrangian is invariant under the gauge symmetry group
$\text{SU}(3)_C \times \text{SU}(2)_L \times \text{U}(1)_Y$. The
$\text{SU}(3)_C$ symmetry governs the strong force, with the subscript $C$
denoting color charge; its generators, the Gell-Mann matrices,
correspond to the eight gluons. The $\text{SU}(2)_L \times \text{U}(1)_Y$
symmetry governs the electroweak force, with generators given by the three
Pauli matrices and a single hypercharge generator $Y$. The subscript $L$
indicates that the weak interaction acts only on left-handed fermions as experimentally observed by Wu (1956).
% NOTE: Why does SU(2)_L only interact with left-handed fermions?
% This is an experimental observation, not derived from a deeper principle.
% The 1956 Wu experiment showed beta decay violates parity; subsequent work
% established the weak interaction is V-A (vector minus axial-vector), which
% projects onto left-handed chirality only. The SM encodes this by placing
% left-handed fermions in SU(2)_L doublets (e.g. (nu_e, e^-)_L, (u,d)_L)
% and right-handed fermions in singlets (e_R, u_R, d_R). Singlets do not
% transform under SU(2)_L, so the W bosons do not appear in their covariant
% derivative — right-handed fermions are simply invisible to the weak force.
% Note: the weak force couples to chirality (the gamma^5 eigenvalue), which
% coincides with helicity only for massless particles.
% Right-handed neutrinos do not appear in the SM at all as a consequence.
% The SM offers no explanation for WHY the weak force is chiral — it is an input. 
Together these produce four gauge bosons: the $W^+$, $W^-$, $W^0$, and
$B^0$. After electroweak symmetry breaking, discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:higgs_mechanism}, these mix to form the physical
$W^\pm$, $Z$, and $\gamma$.

The dynamics of all SM particles and their interactions are encoded in the
SM Lagrangian density~\cite{PDG2022}:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{SM} =
  \underbrace{-\frac{1}{4}F^{a}_{\mu\nu}F^{a\mu\nu}}_{\text{gauge kinetic}}
  + \underbrace{i\bar{\psi} \gamma^\mu D_\mu \psi}_{\text{fermion kinetic}}
  + \underbrace{|D_\mu\phi|^2 - V(\phi)}_{\text{Higgs sector}}
  + \underbrace{y_{ij}\bar{\psi}_{Li}\phi\psi_{Rj} + \text{h.c.}}_{\text{Yukawa}}
  \label{eq:SMlagrangian}
\end{equation}
% Note on indices:
% F^a_{\mu\nu}: a = gauge group generator index (summed over SU(3), SU(2), U(1)); mu,nu = Lorentz indices
% psi: implicit sum over all fermion flavors; mu = Lorentz index on gamma matrix
% D_mu phi: covariant derivative of Higgs doublet; |...|^2 = (D_mu phi)^dag (D^mu phi)
% y_{mn}: 3x3 Yukawa matrix; m,n = generation indices (chosen to avoid clash with imaginary unit i)
% Reference: PDG Review, Workman et al. PTEP 2022, 083C01 (Electroweak Model chapter)

The gauge kinetic term $-\frac{1}{4}F^a_{\mu\nu}F^{a\mu\nu}$ governs the propagation and self-interactions of all gauge bosons, where the index $a$ runs over all generators of the gauge group. The fermion kinetic term $i\bar{\psi}\gamma^\mu D_\mu\psi$ describes how fermions propagate and couple to the gauge fields through the covariant derivative $D_\mu$. The Higgs sector $|D_\mu\phi|^2 - V(\phi)$ contains the Higgs field and its couplings to the gauge bosons; the potential $V(\phi)$ drives the spontaneous symmetry breaking discussed in Section~\ref{sec:higgs_mechanism}. Finally, the Yukawa term $y_{ij}\bar{\psi}_{Li}\phi\psi_{Rj}$ couples fermions to the Higgs field, generating fermion masses after symmetry breaking, as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:yukawa}.
% References: PDG (Workman et al. 2022), Glashow 1961, Weinberg 1967

\section{The Higgs Mechanism}
\label{sec:higgs_mechanism}

% ------------------------------------------------------------
% OUTLINE FOR THIS SECTION:
% Para 1 — The mass problem
%   - SM gauge invariance forbids explicit mass terms for W, Z
%   - Explicit m_W^2 W_mu W^mu breaks gauge invariance
%   - Yet W (~80 GeV) and Z (~91 GeV) are observed to be massive
% Para 2 — The Higgs potential and spontaneous symmetry breaking
%   - Introduce complex scalar doublet phi with V(phi) = mu^2|phi|^2 + lambda|phi|^4
%   - For mu^2 < 0: unstable at origin, degenerate ring of minima at |phi| = v/sqrt(2)
%   - v ~ 246 GeV is the vacuum expectation value
%   - Field settles into one minimum: SSB of SU(2)_L x U(1)_Y -> U(1)_EM
%   - Reference figure: Higgs potential (fig:HiggsPotential)
% Para 3 — Consequences of SSB: gauge boson masses and the Higgs boson
%   - Three broken generators -> three Goldstone bosons
%   - Goldstone bosons absorbed by W+, W-, Z -> longitudinal modes -> mass
%   - Photon corresponds to unbroken generator -> remains massless
%   - Remaining physical DOF: real scalar Higgs boson h
%   - Optional: show W and Z mass formulae m_W = gv/2, m_Z = sqrt(g^2+g'^2)v/2
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The gauge symmetry of the SM places a strict constraint on the Lagrangian.
Explicit mass terms for the gauge bosons, of the form $m_W^2 W_\mu W^\mu$,
break gauge invariance and are therefore forbidden. The theory thus predicts
that the $W$ and $Z$ bosons should be massless, analogous to the photon.
However, the $W$ and $Z$ bosons are experimentally observed to be massive
at 80 and 91 GeV respectively. This contradiction points to a missing
mechanism, one that generates gauge boson masses without introducing
explicit mass terms and without breaking the gauge invariance the SM is
built upon.

The resolution is provided by the Higgs mechanism~\cite{Englert:1964et, Higgs:1964pj, Guralnik:1964eu}.
% DUMMY CITE KEYS — correct later:
%   Englert:1964et  — Englert & Brout, PRL 13, 321 (1964)
%   Higgs:1964pj    — Higgs, PRL 13, 508 (1964)
%   Guralnik:1964eu — Guralnik, Hagen & Kibble, PRL 13, 585 (1964)
A complex scalar field $\phi$, called the Higgs field, is introduced with
the potential $V(\phi) = \mu^2|\phi|^2 + \lambda|\phi|^4$, shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:HiggsPotential}. For $\mu^2 < 0$, the origin is an
unstable maximum and the true minima form a degenerate ring at
$|\phi| = v/\sqrt{2}$, where $v \approx 246$ GeV is the vacuum expectation
value (vev). The field must settle into one point on this ring. The ground
state spontaneously selects one minimum, breaking
$\text{SU}(2)_L \times \text{U}(1)_Y \rightarrow \text{U}(1)_{EM}$.
Importantly, the Lagrangian itself retains its full symmetry. It is only
the ground state that breaks it, hence the term spontaneous symmetry
breaking (SSB).

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figures/chapter2/higgs_potential.png}
	\caption{The Higgs potential $V(\phi) = \mu^2|\phi|^2 + \lambda|\phi|^4$ for $\mu^2 < 0$.
	Point A is the unstable maximum at $\phi = 0$. Point B is a stable minimum at $|\phi| = v/\sqrt{2}$.
	The ground state spontaneously selects one minimum, breaking $\text{SU}(2)_L \times \text{U}(1)_Y \rightarrow \text{U}(1)_\text{EM}$.}
	\label{fig:HiggsPotential}
\end{figure}

Breaking three of the four electroweak generators produces three massless
Goldstone bosons. These are absorbed by the $W^+$, $W^-$, and $Z$ bosons
as their longitudinal polarisation modes, giving them mass. The resulting
masses $m_W = gv/2$ and $m_Z = \sqrt{g^2 + g'^2}\,v/2$ are determined
entirely by the vev and reproduce the observed values. The photon
corresponds to the unbroken $\text{U}(1)_{EM}$ generator and remains
massless. The remaining physical degree of freedom in the scalar doublet
is the Higgs boson $h$, a real scalar particle representing oscillations
about the potential minimum. While the Higgs mechanism accounts for gauge
boson masses, fermion masses require a further ingredient, discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:yukawa}.

A landmark achievement in experimental particle physics was obtained at the LHC in 2012 with the discovery of the Higgs Boson. This particle is a quantization of the Higgs field which is responsible for mass generation in the standard model, which up until then was a crucial missing piece. The Higgs field has a wine bottle shape that leads to spontaneous symmetry breaking which is a crucial part of the Higgs mechanism giving rise to the mass of vector bosons. Since its discovery, many of the Higgs properties have been studied including its mass, spin, coupling to gauge bosons, and coupling to third generation fermions. As of date, all experimental observations are in line with the Standard Model predictions.

\begin{itemize}
  \item The SM does not describe gravity, dark matter, or the matter-antimatter asymmetry of the universe.
  % NOTE: Three-generation structure — the SM contains exactly three generations of quarks and leptons
  % of increasing mass, but provides no explanation for why this pattern repeats exactly three times.
  % This is flagged in Section 2.1 as an open question.
  \item The hierarchy problem: quantum corrections drive $m_H$ toward the Planck scale, requiring fine-tuned cancellation unless new physics exists at the TeV scale. The dominant contribution to these corrections comes from the top quark loop, which enters with a coefficient proportional to $y_t^2$. Because $y_t \approx 1$ is so large compared to all other SM fermion Yukawa couplings, the top quark is responsible for the largest single quantum correction to the Higgs mass. This is why many BSM theories targeting the hierarchy problem, such as supersymmetry, predict a top quark superpartner near the TeV scale.
  \item EW vacuum stability: the measured $m_H$ and $m_t$ place the vacuum in a metastable region, suggesting the universe may reside in a false vacuum~\cite{Buttazzo:2013uya}.
