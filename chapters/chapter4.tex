\section{Introduction to B-Tagging}

Heavy particles produced in high-energy collisions decay with significant branching fractions to heavy-flavor quarks: the top quark decays almost exclusively via $t \to Wb$, while the Higgs boson has sizable branching fractions to both $b\bar{b}$ and $c\bar{c}$ pairs. Identification of jets originating from b-quarks (b-jets) and c-quarks (c-jets) is therefore a critical task for the LHC physics program. The b quark undergoes weak decay $b \to cW^-$, with a rate governed by the CKM matrix element $V_{cb}$. Since $|V_{cb}|$ is small, the decay proceeds slowly relative to those of lighter quarks, resulting in a comparatively long B-hadron lifetime of $\sim$1.5~ps. This is sufficient for a B hadron produced at a typical LHC interaction point to travel several millimeters before decaying, giving rise to a secondary vertex spatially displaced from the primary interaction vertex.

The displacement of the secondary vertex can be characterized by the impact parameters of its associated tracks. The transverse impact parameter $d_0$ is the distance of closest approach between a track and the primary vertex in the transverse plane, while the longitudinal impact parameter $z_0$ is the corresponding distance along the beam axis. Tracks originating from a displaced B-hadron decay will exhibit statistically significant non-zero values of $d_0$ and $z_0$, providing a powerful discriminant against light-flavor jets whose tracks are consistent with the primary vertex. Charm hadrons also undergo displaced decays, though with a shorter mean lifetime ($\sim$0.5~ps) and correspondingly smaller average displacement, making c-jets a more challenging background to reject than light-flavor jets.

Early ATLAS b-tagging algorithms exploited these displaced-vertex signatures by combining track impact parameter significances, inclusive secondary vertex properties, and decay chain topology into multivariate discriminants, progressing from boosted decision trees to shallow and then deep neural networks. The following section describes the subsequent transition to end-to-end architectures that process the full set of tracks within a jet simultaneously, enabling substantial improvements in both light-jet and c-jet rejection.

\section{Overview of Architectures for Modeling Jets}

Jet flavor identification at the LHC is a multi-class classification problem: given a jet, a tagger must discriminate between b-jets, c-jets, and light-flavor jets. Deep neural networks (DNNs) are well suited to this task, learning non-linear decision boundaries in high-dimensional feature spaces directly from MC-simulated training samples where truth-level flavor labels are available. Crucially, architectures that operate directly on the full set of tracks within a jet, treating the jet as a variable-length set of objects, preserve information that is inevitably lost when constructing fixed-size high-level summary variables. The following subsections describe the graph- and attention-based architectures that exploit this, and the performance gains they deliver in both b-jet and c-jet identification.

\subsection{Graph Neural Networks and GN1}

A graph neural network (GNN) represents a set of objects as nodes in a graph, with edges
encoding pairwise relationships between them. Message passing operations allow each node to
iteratively aggregate information from its neighbors, building representations that capture
both individual object properties and the collective structure of the ensemble. Formally, the
updated representation of node $i$ at layer $k$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{x}_i^{(k)} = \gamma^{(k)} \!\left( \mathbf{x}_i^{(k-1)},\; \bigoplus_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} \phi^{(k)}\!\left( \mathbf{x}_i^{(k-1)}, \mathbf{x}_j^{(k-1)}, \mathbf{e}_{j,i} \right) \right)
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x}_i^{(k-1)} \in \mathbb{R}^F$ are the node features of node $i$, $\mathbf{e}_{j,i} \in \mathbb{R}^D$
are optional edge features, $\bigoplus$ denotes a differentiable permutation-invariant aggregation
function such as a sum, mean, or max, and $\gamma$ and $\phi$ are differentiable functions
implemented as MLPs. This is a natural fit for the tracks within a jet, which form an unordered
set with meaningful pairwise relationships such as angular separation or compatibility with a
common decay vertex.

https://pytorch-geometric.readthedocs.io/en/latest/notes/create\_gnn.html

\texttt{GN1} represented a fundamental architectural shift, moving away from the paradigm of
combining outputs of separately optimized low-level tagging algorithms towards a single
end-to-end graph-based representation of the jet. Tracks are treated as nodes in a fully
connected graph, with message passing used to build representations encoding both individual
track properties and the collective structure of the track ensemble. Rather than taking
pre-computed high-level discriminants as inputs, \texttt{GN1} operates directly on low-level
track parameters including impact parameter significances, track kinematics, and hit-level
variables such as pixel and SCT hit counts, exploiting information that is otherwise compressed
or discarded when constructing high-level summary variables.

\texttt{GN1} also introduced multi-task training with two auxiliary objectives alongside the
primary jet-level $b$/$c$/light classification. The first predicts the truth origin of each
individual track, classifying it as originating from the primary vertex, a $b$-hadron decay,
a $c$-hadron decay, pileup, or other secondary processes. The second predicts the vertex
compatibility of each pair of tracks, determining whether two tracks originate from the same
vertex and thereby performing implicit secondary vertex finding without a dedicated vertexing
algorithm. Together, these auxiliary objectives act as powerful regularizers, encouraging the
network to build physically interpretable representations that generalize well across jet
kinematics. The graph-based message passing combined with multi-task learning produced
substantial improvements in both light-jet and $c$-jet rejection over \texttt{DL1r}.

\subsection{Attention Mechanism and Transformers and GN2}\label{sec:attention}

The attention mechanism computes updated representations of each element in a set by learning
dynamic edge weights that reflect the correlations, or local context, between all pairs of
elements. In scaled dot-product attention, each object's representation is projected into three
vectors: a query $\mathbf{q}$, a key $\mathbf{k}$, and a value $\mathbf{v}$. Collecting these
projections across all $N$ objects into matrices $Q$, $K$, and $V$, the updated representations
are given by:
\begin{equation}
	\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V
\end{equation}
where $d_k$ is the dimension of the key vectors, introduced to prevent saturation of the softmax
at large dot-product magnitudes. For a jet with $N$ tracks, this produces an $N \times N$
attention matrix at each layer, where entry $(i,j)$ reflects how strongly track $i$ attends to
track $j$ when updating its representation. A transformer encoder block consists of one such
self-attention operation followed by a position-wise feed-forward network, with residual
connections and layer normalization around both. Multi-head attention extends this by running
$h$ independent attention operations in parallel with separate learned projections, allowing the
network to simultaneously capture different aspects of the track relationships.

Compared to the message passing approach of \texttt{GN1}, self-attention offers several
advantages for jet flavor tagging. In message passing, all neighboring tracks contribute to
the node update through a fixed aggregation function, regardless of their relevance to the jet.
Self-attention replaces this with a fully learned, input-dependent weighting, allowing the
network to dynamically identify which track pairs are most discriminating on a per-jet basis.
This enables the capture of long-range correlations across the full track ensemble without any
locality bias. The learned attention weights also provide direct interpretability: they can be
inspected to understand which track pairs the network considers most informative for a given
tagging decision, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:attention}.

\texttt{GN2} extended \texttt{GN1} by replacing the message passing aggregation with a
transformer encoder applied directly to the set of tracks within each jet. The multi-task
training objectives of \texttt{GN1} were retained, and the input feature set was extended to
include additional track quality and vertex variables. A global jet representation is formed by
an attention-weighted sum over the final track representations and passed to the jet-level
classification head. \texttt{GN2} currently represents the state-of-the-art resolved jet
flavor tagger deployed in ATLAS, achieving the best light-jet and $c$-jet rejection of any
tagger in the sequence, and forming the architectural basis for the boosted jet tagger
\texttt{GN2X} described in Section~\ref{sec:boosted}.

\subsection{Benchmarks and Results -- Resolved}

The performance of a flavor tagger is evaluated using a receiver operating characteristic (ROC)
curve. The tagger produces a continuous discriminant score for each jet, and a working point is
defined by placing a threshold on this score. By scanning the threshold continuously, one traces
out a curve in the space of signal efficiency and background rejection, where the signal
efficiency is the fraction of true $b$-jets passing the threshold and the background rejection
is the inverse of the false positive rate. In HEP it is conventional to plot the light-jet
rejection $1/\varepsilon_\text{light}$ and $c$-jet rejection $1/\varepsilon_c$ as functions of
the $b$-jet efficiency $\varepsilon_b$. The area under the curve (AUC) provides a single
threshold-independent summary of overall tagger performance. Specific working points ---
typically $\varepsilon_b = 60\%$, $70\%$, $77\%$, and $85\%$ --- are defined by fixing the
$b$-jet efficiency and quoting the corresponding background rejection, allowing direct comparison
between taggers and across analyses.

\subsection{Boosted Jet Tagging with Graph and Attention Neural Networks}
\label{sec:boosted}

In addition to small-$R$ jet tagging, large-$R$ jet tagging ($R=1.0$) is used to identify boosted
objects whose decay products are sufficiently collimated that they cannot be resolved as individual
small-$R$ jets. Identifying these boosted jets is crucial for the Higgs program at the LHC, where
a boosted Higgs boson can hadronically decay via $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ and
$H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$, with multijet (QCD) processes and fully hadronic top-quark decays
forming the primary backgrounds. The previous state-of-the-art tagger, DXbb, combined
flavor-tagging discriminants from individual variable-radius (VR) subjets in a feed-forward
neural network, limiting its ability to exploit correlations between the decay products of both
$b$- and $c$-hadrons within the jet.

Building upon the transformer architecture of \texttt{GN2}, \texttt{GN2X} extends this approach
to large-$R$ jet tagging by operating directly on charged particle tracks associated with the
large-$R$ jet, reconstructed from Unified Flow Objects (UFOs). Up to 100 tracks are used as
input, sorted by decreasing transverse impact parameter significance, with each track described
by 20 variables including impact parameter significances, track kinematics, and hit-level
quantities. The network retains the multi-task training objectives of \texttt{GN1} and
\texttt{GN2}, simultaneously training track origin classification and vertex grouping auxiliary
tasks alongside the primary jet-level classification. Track representations are processed through
a transformer encoder consisting of 6 encoder blocks with 4 attention heads, and a global jet
representation is formed via an attention-weighted sum over the final track representations.
\texttt{GN2X} produces four probability scores via a softmax output layer: $p_{Hbb}$, $p_{Hcc}$,
$p_{\text{top}}$, and $p_{\text{QCD}}$.

These four probabilities are combined into a discriminant score taking the form of a log-likelihood
ratio:

\begin{align}
	D^{\text{GN2X}}_{Hbb} &= \ln \left( \frac{p_{Hbb}}{f_{Hcc} \cdot p_{Hcc} + f_{top} \cdot 
		p_{top} + (1 - f_{Hcc} - f_{top}) \cdot p_{QCD}} \right) \\
	D^{\text{GN2X}}_{Hcc} &= \ln \left( \frac{p_{Hcc}}{f_{Hbb} \cdot p_{Hbb} + f_{top} \cdot 
		p_{top} + (1 - f_{Hbb} - f_{top}) \cdot p_{QCD}} \right)
\end{align}

where $f_{Hcc}$ and $f_{\text{top}}$ are free parameters controlling the relative rejection weights
of the $H(c\bar{c})$ and top backgrounds with respect to multijet, optimized to maximize background
rejection at a given signal efficiency. For $H(b\bar{b})$ tagging, values of $f_{Hcc} = 0.02$ and
$f_{\text{top}} = 0.25$ are used~\cite{ATL-PHYS-PUB-2023-021}.

\subsubsection{Mass Sculpting of Background}

An important consideration when deploying any tagger is the potential for mass sculpting, 
where correlations between the tagger score and the large-$R$ jet mass cause the background 
mass distribution to resemble that of the signal after applying a discriminant cut. This is 
particularly undesirable for data-driven background estimation techniques which rely on 
smoothly-falling mass distributions.

\section{Calibration of B-Tagging Algorithms}

Since GN2X is trained on Monte Carlo simulation, its tagging efficiency may differ from that
observed in data due to mismodeling effects arising from the matrix element generator, parton
shower, underlying event tune, and detector simulation. GN2X requires a process-based
calibration where scale factors are derived separately for distinct physics processes in order
to extract the signal efficiency and background mistag rate independently. The efficiency scale
factor for a given process is defined in data and MC as:

\begin{equation}
	\varepsilon^{\text{data}} = \frac{N^{\text{data}}_{\text{passed}}}{N^{\text{data}}_{\text{total}}}, \qquad
	\varepsilon^{\text{MC}} = \frac{N^{\text{MC}}_{\text{passed}}}{N^{\text{MC}}_{\text{total}}}
\end{equation}

where $N_{\text{passed}}$ is the number of events passing the tagger working point requirement
and $N_{\text{total}}$ is the total number of events in the process. The scale factor is then:

\begin{equation}
	SF = \frac{\varepsilon^{\text{data}}}{\varepsilon^{\text{MC}}}
	\label{eq:SF}
\end{equation}

Two calibrations are described in this section. First, a signal efficiency calibration
using $Z \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ events as a kinematic proxy for $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$. Second,
a top-quark mistag rate calibration using semi-leptonic $t\bar{t}$ events.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{$Z \rightarrow b\bar{b}$}{Z->bb} Signal Efficiency Calibration}

The signal efficiency calibration measures the rate at which GN2X correctly identifies
large-$R$ jets originating from an $X \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ decay, where $X$ is some
heavy mass resonance. Since the Higgs boson is itself the signal of interest in the analyses that use
these scale factors, calibrating directly on $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ events is not
feasible as this would lead to circular dependence. Any sample used for calibration should 
not contain the signal being searched for. Instead,
$Z \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ events serve as a kinematic proxy. The $Z$ boson
($m_Z \approx 91$~GeV) is a color-singlet resonance with a mass reasonably close to
the Higgs ($m_H \approx 125$~GeV), and the internal structure of $Z \rightarrow b\bar{b}$
and $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ jets are expected to be sufficiently similar that scale
factors derived from the $Z$ sample can be extrapolated to the Higgs signal.

Since $N^{\text{data}}_{\text{total}}$ is not directly observable in the hadronic channel,
the scale factor is extracted via a double ratio of signal strengths measured before and
after applying the tagger requirement:

\begin{equation}
	SF = \frac{\varepsilon^{\text{data}}}{\varepsilon^{\text{MC}}} = \frac{N^{\text{data}}_{\text{passed}}/N^{\text{data}}_{\text{total}}}{N^{\text{MC}}_{\text{passed}}/N^{\text{MC}}_{\text{total}}} = \frac{N^{\text{data}}_{\text{passed}}/N^{\text{MC}}_{\text{passed}}}{N^{\text{data}}_{\text{total}}/N^{\text{MC}}_{\text{total}}} = \frac{\mu_{\text{post-tag}}}{\mu_{\text{pre-tag}}}
\end{equation}

where $\mu$ is the ratio of observed to predicted signal yields. Overwhelming multijet background
makes a direct measurement of $\mu_{\text{pre-tag}}$ using $Z \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ events
before tagging unfeasible. Instead, $\mu_{\text{pre-tag}}$ is obtained from the
$Z \rightarrow \ell^+\ell^-$ channel, which has a well-understood background composition.
The number of $Z \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ events in data before tagging is estimated as:

\begin{equation}
	N^{\text{data}}_{Z \rightarrow b\bar{b}} = N^{\text{MC}}_{Z \rightarrow b\bar{b}} \cdot \frac{N^{\text{data}}_{\ell\ell} - N^{\text{MC}}_{\text{bkg},\ell\ell}}{N^{\text{MC}}_{Z \rightarrow \ell^+\ell^-}}
\end{equation}

where $N^{\text{MC}}_{Z \rightarrow b\bar{b}}$ is the MC prediction before tagging,
$N^{\text{data}}_{\ell\ell}$ is the total number of selected dilepton events in data,
$N^{\text{MC}}_{\text{bkg},\ell\ell}$ is the MC background prediction in the dilepton
channel, and $N^{\text{MC}}_{Z \rightarrow \ell^+\ell^-}$ is the MC prediction for
$Z \rightarrow \ell^+\ell^-$ signal events. Thus $\mu_{\text{pre-tag}}$ depends only
on the dilepton channel yields:

\begin{equation}
	\mu_{\text{pre-tag}} = \frac{N^{\text{data}}_{\ell\ell} - N^{\text{MC}}_{\text{bkg},\ell\ell}}{N^{\text{MC}}_{Z \rightarrow \ell^+\ell^-}}
\end{equation}

The post-tag signal strength $\mu_{\text{post-tag}}$ is extracted from a binned
likelihood fit to the large-$R$ jet mass distribution after applying the working point
requirement, exploiting the $Z \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ mass peak as a discriminating
feature against the smoothly-falling multijet background.

Two complementary event topologies are used depending on the transverse momentum of
the $Z$ candidate. At moderate boosts ($200 < p_\text{T} < 450$~GeV),
$Z(\rightarrow b\bar{b})\gamma$ events are selected, where an associated photon
provides a clean single-photon trigger and a well-defined recoil object. At higher
boosts ($p_\text{T} > 450$~GeV), $Z(\rightarrow b\bar{b})$+jets events are used,
where the hadronic decay products are sufficiently collimated to be fully captured
within the $R = 1.0$ jet cone.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{$t\bar{t}$}{ttbar} Mistag Calibration}

Top quark pair production is one of the dominant backgrounds in boosted
$H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ and $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ searches, and correctly modeling
its contribution after tagger requirements is critical for reliable background estimation
in these analyses. While the signal efficiency calibration corrects for data-MC differences
in the tagger's ability to identify $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ jets, a separate calibration
is required to correct for mismodeling of the top-jet mistag rate, which is the rate at
which hadronic top-quark jets pass the GN2X working point. An underestimate of the top
rejection in data would lead to a residual excess of top background
after tagging, directly biasing any analysis relying on this tagger. The $t\bar{t}$
mistag calibration described in this section was performed as part of the author's ATLAS
authorship qualification task.

Scale factors are derived following Equation~\ref{eq:SF}, where the efficiency
$\varepsilon$ represents the mistag rate: the fraction of $t\bar{t}$ probe jets passing
the tagger working point. Since the $t\bar{t}$ yield in data cannot be measured directly,
as it is contaminated with contributions from $W$+jets, single-top, and diboson processes,
both the scale factor and an overall $t\bar{t}$ normalization factor $\mu_{t\bar{t}}$
must be extracted simultaneously. Writing the expected yields in the signal region (SR),
where the probe jet passes the tagger, and the control region (CR), where it fails,
in terms of the efficiencies:

\begin{align}
	N^{\text{data}}_{\text{SR}} &= \mu_{t\bar{t}} \cdot \frac{\varepsilon^{\text{data}}}{\varepsilon^{\text{MC}}} \cdot N^{t\bar{t}}_{\text{SR}} + N^{\text{other}}_{\text{SR}} \\
	N^{\text{data}}_{\text{CR}} &= \mu_{t\bar{t}} \cdot \frac{1 - \varepsilon^{\text{data}}}{1 - \varepsilon^{\text{MC}}} \cdot N^{t\bar{t}}_{\text{CR}} + N^{\text{other}}_{\text{CR}}
\end{align}

where $N^{t\bar{t}}$ and $N^{\text{other}}$ are the MC-predicted $t\bar{t}$ and
non-$t\bar{t}$ event yields respectively. Substituting $SF = \varepsilon^{\text{data}} /
\varepsilon^{\text{MC}}$ from Equation~\ref{eq:SF}, these expressions become:

\begin{align}
	N^{\text{data}}_{\text{SR}} &= \mu_{t\bar{t}} \cdot SF \cdot N^{t\bar{t}}_{\text{SR}} + N^{\text{other}}_{\text{SR}} \\
	N^{\text{data}}_{\text{CR}} &= \mu_{t\bar{t}} \cdot \frac{1 - \varepsilon^{\text{MC}} \cdot SF}{1 - \varepsilon^{\text{MC}}} \cdot N^{t\bar{t}}_{\text{CR}} + N^{\text{other}}_{\text{CR}}
\end{align}

These two equations are solved simultaneously per probe-jet $p_\text{T}$ bin to extract
$\mu_{t\bar{t}}$ and $SF$ via a maximum likelihood fit to the probe-jet mass distribution.
The normalization factor $\mu_{t\bar{t}}$ absorbs the known data-MC discrepancy in the
$t\bar{t}$ $p_\text{T}$ spectrum, while $SF$ isolates the efficiency correction
introduced by the tagger itself. Since no floating normalization is applied to the
non-$t\bar{t}$ backgrounds, a phase space region must be identified where these
contributions are heavily suppressed and the $t\bar{t}$ yield dominates. Semi-leptonic
$t\bar{t}$ events are targeted for this purpose, where the leptonically decaying top
quark provides the event ``tag'' and the hadronically decaying top quark provides the
tagger ``probe''.

\subsubsection{Tag-and-Probe Event Selection}

Semi-leptonic $t\bar{t}$ decay produces a distinctive detector signature: one top quark
decays leptonically ($t \rightarrow Wb \rightarrow \ell\nu b$) and the other hadronically
($t \rightarrow Wb \rightarrow qqb$), yielding a final state containing an isolated lepton,
significant $E_\text{T}^\text{miss}$ from the neutrino, a $b$-tagged small-$R$ jet from
the leptonic side, and a boosted large-$R$ jet from the hadronic side, as illustrated in
Figure~\ref{fig:semileptonic_ttbar_diagram}. In the tag-and-probe approach, the
leptonically decaying top quark provides the event ``tag'' and the hadronic large-$R$ jet
serves as the tagger ``probe''. The dominant backgrounds are $W$+jets, single-top, and
diboson production. Requiring a $b$-tagged small-$R$ jet on the tag side heavily suppresses
the $W$+jets contribution, while the back-to-back topology of the two top quarks provides
further discrimination against non-$t\bar{t}$ processes.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.4\linewidth]{figures/chapter4/ttbar_diagram.png}
	\caption{Feynman diagram of semi-leptonic $t\bar{t}$ decay. One top quark decays
		leptonically via $t \rightarrow Wb \rightarrow \ell\nu b$, providing the event tag,
		while the other decays hadronically via $t \rightarrow Wb \rightarrow qqb$, with the
		hadronic decay products reconstructed as a large-$R$ probe jet.}
	\label{fig:semileptonic_ttbar_diagram}
\end{figure}

Physics objects are reconstructed and calibrated before event selection. Electrons are required
to have $p_\text{T} > 28$ GeV, $|\eta_\text{cluster}| < 2.47$ (excluding the barrel/endcap
transition region $1.37 < |\eta_\text{cluster}| < 1.52$), pass tight identification, and match
a track in the inner detector (ID). Muons must satisfy $p_\text{T} > 28$ GeV, $|\eta| < 2.5$,
and pass medium identification by matching ID and muon spectrometer tracks. Both lepton types
must meet tight isolation criteria and primary vertex compatibility requirements.

Small-$R$ jets are built via the anti-$k_\text{T}$ algorithm ($R = 0.4$) using particle flow
inputs, requiring $p_\text{T} > 25$ GeV and $|\eta| < 2.5$, with pile-up suppression via the
Jet Vertex Tagger. $B$-jets are identified using the DL1d multivariate algorithm. Large-$R$
jets ($R = 1.0$) are reconstructed from UFO~\cite{PLACEHOLDER:UFO} using the anti-$k_\text{T}$
algorithm and trimmed to reduce pile-up and soft radiation effects, with subjets carrying less
than 5\% of the large-$R$ jet $p_\text{T}$ removed. Only large-$R$ jets with
$p_\text{T} > 300$ GeV and $|\eta| < 2.0$ are considered. GN2X is applied directly to the
large-$R$ jet, operating on the charged particle tracks associated to it.

Overlap removal is applied sequentially to prevent double-counting: electrons within
$\Delta R = 0.01$ of a muon are removed first, followed by jets within $\Delta R = 0.2$ of
an electron. Electrons within $\Delta R = 0.4$ of a remaining jet are then discarded. Muons
are rejected if within $\Delta R < 0.04 + 10~\text{GeV}/p_\text{T}(\mu)$ of a jet with three
or more tracks; otherwise the jet is removed. The missing transverse momentum
($E_\text{T}^\text{miss}$) is computed as the magnitude of the negative vector sum of all
calibrated object momenta, plus a track-based soft term matched to the primary vertex to
ensure resilience against pile-up.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{figures/chapter4/Reco_Event.png}
	\caption{Illustration of the reconstructed semi-leptonic $t\bar{t}$ event topology used
		in the tag-and-probe calibration. The leptonic tag side consists of an isolated lepton
		and a $b$-tagged small-$R$ jet, while the hadronic probe side is reconstructed as a
		single large-$R$ jet candidate for GN2X tagging.}
	\label{fig:reco_event}
\end{figure}

Events must pass a single-electron or single-muon trigger with $p_\text{T} > 28$ GeV, and
are further required to contain exactly one lepton with $p_\text{T} > 70$ GeV to suppress
non-prompt lepton backgrounds. $E_\text{T}^\text{miss} > 70$ GeV is imposed to account for
the neutrino from the leptonic $W$ decay. At least one large-$R$ jet with $p_\text{T} \geq
300$ GeV must be present; the leading-$p_\text{T}$ large-$R$ jet is designated as the
probe-jet candidate and its mass is restricted to $50~\text{GeV} \leq m \leq 300~\text{GeV}$
to ensure compatibility with the calibrated jet mass range. The small-$R$ jet closest to the
lepton in $\Delta R$ must pass $b$-tagging at the 77\% DL1d working point and serves as
the tag-jet, significantly suppressing the $V$+jets background. To further enforce the
back-to-back topology expected from $t\bar{t}$ production, the azimuthal separation between
the tag-jet and probe-jet is required to satisfy $|\Delta\phi(\text{tag}, \text{probe})| > 1.0$.
Additionally, the invariant mass of the lepton and tag-jet system is required to satisfy
$m_{\ell j} < 150$~GeV, exploiting the kinematic constraint that the lepton and $b$-jet
from the leptonic top decay must have an invariant mass below $m_t - m_W \approx 155$~GeV,
thereby further suppressing non-$t\bar{t}$ contributions. The resulting event topology is
illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:reco_event}, and the effect of each successive selection

\subsubsection{Constructing Fit Regions}

The mistag efficiency $\varepsilon^{\text{data}}_{t\bar{t}}$ is measured as a function of
probe-jet $p_\text{T}$ by simultaneously extracting the $t\bar{t}$ normalization factor
$\mu_{t\bar{t}}$ and the scale factor $SF$ from a maximum likelihood fit in each $p_\text{T}$
bin. The signal region is populated by events where the probe jet passes the GN2Xv01 tagger
working point, and the control region by events where it fails. At the time of this study,
the GN2Xv01 working points had not yet been finalized; a flat threshold of 1.76 on the
discriminant was therefore used to approximate the 70\% signal efficiency working point.
Subsequent developments have resulted in working points defined as two-dimensional functions
of the discriminant score, jet mass, and jet $p_\text{T}$, in order to reduce mass sculpting
effects as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:MASS_SCULPTING}.

\section{Conclusion}

This chapter has traced the evolution of jet flavor tagging algorithms in ATLAS from early
multivariate approaches through to the graph- and attention-based architectures deployed for
Run~3. Each successive generation has moved closer to the raw detector information, replacing
hand-engineered summary variables with learned representations operating directly on tracks
and hits. The performance gains from this progression are substantial: as summarized in
Figure~\ref{fig:history}, each new architecture delivers measurable improvements in both
light-jet and $c$-jet rejection at fixed $b$-jet efficiency, with direct implications for
the sensitivity of analyses relying on heavy-flavor identification.

The extension of this paradigm to boosted jet tagging, realized in \texttt{GN2X}, demonstrates
that the same end-to-end track-based approach scales naturally to large-$R$ jets. By operating
directly on all charged particle tracks within the large-$R$ jet rather than on high-level
subjet discriminants, \texttt{GN2X} achieves significant improvements over the previous
state-of-the-art DXbb tagger in both $H(b\bar{b})$ and $H(c\bar{c})$ identification, while
maintaining acceptable mass sculpting behavior in the multijet background.

A tagger trained on simulation is only useful in data analyses if its efficiency differences
relative to data are understood and corrected. The two calibrations presented in this chapter
together provide the complete set of scale factors needed to deploy \texttt{GN2X} in physics
analyses. The $Z \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ signal efficiency calibration establishes the correction
to the tagger's ability to identify genuine $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ candidates, while the
$t\bar{t}$ mistag rate calibration corrects for mismodeling of the dominant top-quark
background. Together, these calibrations ensure that both the signal acceptance and background
rejection of the tagger are accurately described in simulation.

The $t\bar{t}$ mistag calibration, performed as part of the author's ATLAS authorship
qualification task, demonstrated good data-MC agreement across all probe-jet $p_\text{T}$
bins following the simultaneous maximum likelihood fit. Scale factors consistent with unity
were extracted for both the MC16 and MC20 simulation campaigns, with good mutual consistency
between the two despite notable differences in the $t\bar{t}$ normalization factors. These
results are based on statistical uncertainties only; a complete result would require the
inclusion of systematic uncertainties from jet energy and mass scale, $t\bar{t}$ modeling,
and flavor tagging sources. The methodology and infrastructure developed here provide the
foundation for a full \texttt{GN2Xv01} calibration to be deployed in future ATLAS analyses
targeting boosted $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ and $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ signatures at Run~3.
