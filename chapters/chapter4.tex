\section{Introduction to Tagging B-Jets}

Heavy particles produced in high-energy collisions, such as top quark and Higgs boson, decay with significant branching fractions to bottom quarks. Identification of jets originating from b-quarks is a critical task for the LHC physics program. The b quark decays via the weak force $b \to c W$.  The CKM matrix dictates lifetime of the decay. Since the $V_{cb}$ matrix element is small, the weak decay proceeds slowly relative to lighter quarks producing comparatively long lifetime ($\sim$1.5~ps). This lifetime is long enough that a B hadron produced at a typical LHC collision point travels a measurable distance, on the order of a few millimeters, before decaying, giving rise to a secondary vertex that is spatially displaced from the primary interaction vertex.

The displacement of the secondary vertex can be characterised by the impact parameters of its associated tracks. The transverse impact parameter $d_0$ is the distance of closest approach between a track and the primary vertex in the transverse plane, while the longitudinal impact parameter $z_0$ is the corresponding distance along the beam axis. Tracks originating from a displaced B-hadron decay will exhibit statistically significant non-zero values of $d_0$ and $z_0$, providing a powerful discriminant against light-flavour jets whose tracks are consistent with the primary vertex.

\section{Overview of Architectures for Modeling Jets}

The identification of b-jets at the LHC reduces to a binary classification problem. Modern machine learning methods, and in particular deep neural networks, are well suited to perform b-tagging. Given a set of input observables such as track kinematics and secondary vertex properties, NNs can learn non-linear decision boundaries in high-dimensional feature spaces directly from simulated training samples. Furthermore, approaches based on recurrent or attention-based architectures can operate directly on the full set of tracks and vertices within a jet, treating the jet as a variable-length sequence or point cloud rather than a fixed-size feature vector. This preserves information that is inevitably discarded when constructing the high-level summary variables on which cut-based and low-dimensional likelihood approaches depend on. The result is a significant improvement in b-tagging efficiency at fixed light-jet rejection relative to classical algorithms, with direct benefits for analyses that rely on b-jet identification for signal selection or background suppression.

\subsection{Graph Neural Networks and GN1}

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{figures/chapter4/GN1.png}

	\label{fig:GN1}

A graph neural network (GNN) represents a set of objects as nodes in a graph, with edges
encoding pairwise relationships between them. Message passing operations allow each node to
iteratively aggregate information from its neighbours, building representations that capture
both individual object properties and the collective structure of the ensemble. This is a natural
fit for the tracks within a jet, which form an unordered set with meaningful pairwise
relationships --- such as the angular separation or compatibility with a common decay vertex.

\texttt{GN1} represented a more fundamental architectural shift, moving away from the paradigm
of combining separate low-level and high-level input streams towards an end-to-end graph-based
representation of the jet. Tracks are treated as nodes in a fully connected graph, with message
passing used to build up representations encoding both individual track properties and the
collective structure of the track ensemble. Notably, \texttt{GN1} was the first ATLAS b-tagger
to operate directly on low-level inputs, incorporating raw tracker hit information rather than
relying solely on reconstructed track parameters and pre-computed vertex quantities. This allows
the network to exploit information that is lost during track reconstruction, such as hit
occupancy and local detector geometry, and represents a significant departure from all preceding
taggers. \texttt{GN1} also introduced multi-task training: in addition to the primary jet-level
$b$/$c$/light classification objective, the network was simultaneously trained to predict the
origin of each individual track --- whether it originated from the primary vertex, a $b$-hadron
decay, a $c$-hadron decay, or a hadronic interaction. This auxiliary track-classification
objective acts as a powerful regulariser, encouraging the network to build physically
interpretable representations that generalise well across different jet kinematics. The
graph-based message passing, combined with multi-task learning and the use of low-level inputs,
produced substantial improvements in both light-jet and $c$-jet rejection over \texttt{DL1d}.

The transformer architecture %~\cite{Vaswani2017} 
processes a set of objects by computing, for
each object, a weighted sum of all other object representations --- including itself. The weights
are not fixed by the network topology but are computed dynamically from the inputs: each object
produces a query vector and a set of key vectors, and the attention weight between two objects
is determined by the similarity of their query and key representations. Concretely, for a jet
with $N$ tracks, this produces an $N \times N$ attention matrix at each layer, where entry
$(i,j)$ reflects how much track $i$ attends to track $j$ when updating its representation.
Crucially, these weights are learned from data, meaning the network discovers which pairwise
track correlations are most discriminating for $b$-tagging without any prior assumption about
which tracks should be related. A transformer encoder block consists of one such self-attention
operation followed by a small feed-forward network applied independently to each track, with
residual connections and layer normalisation around both. Stacking multiple encoder blocks allows
the network to build up increasingly abstract representations of the full track ensemble.

\texttt{GN2} extended the graph neural network approach by replacing the message passing
aggregation of \texttt{GN1} with a self-attention mechanism inspired by the transformer
architecture. Rather than aggregating neighbour information through fixed pooling operations,
self-attention computes a dynamic weighted sum of all other track representations, where the
weights are learned functions of the query and key representations of each pair of tracks. This
allows the network to selectively focus on the most relevant pairwise relationships for a given
jet, rather than treating all edges equally. \texttt{GN2} retained the multi-task track-origin
classification objective of \texttt{GN1} and extended the input feature set to include
additional vertex and track quality variables. The attention mechanism provides both a
performance benefit --- by more flexibly modelling long-range correlations between tracks --- and
an interpretability benefit, since the learned attention weights can be inspected to understand
which track pairs the network considers most informative for a given tagging decision.
\texttt{GN2} currently represents the state-of-the-art b-tagger deployed in ATLAS, achieving
the best light-jet and $c$-jet rejection of any tagger in the sequence, and forming the basis
for ongoing development towards the High-Luminosity LHC era.

\section{Benchmarks and Results}

The performance of a b-tagger is evaluated using a receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve.
The tagger produces a continuous discriminant score for each jet, and a working point is defined
by placing a threshold on this score: jets above the threshold are classified as $b$-jets, those
below are rejected. By scanning the threshold continuously from its maximum to its minimum value,
one traces out a curve in the space of signal efficiency and background rejection. The signal
efficiency, or true positive rate, is the fraction of true $b$-jets that pass the threshold; the
background rejection is the inverse of the false positive rate, which is the fraction of
non-$b$-jets incorrectly accepted. In HEP it is conventional to plot the light-jet rejection
$1/\varepsilon_\text{light}$ and $c$-jet rejection $1/\varepsilon_c$ as functions of the $b$-jet
efficiency $\varepsilon_b$, rather than the true and false positive rates directly. A tagger with
no discriminating power would accept signal and background at equal rates, corresponding to a
flat rejection of unity for all efficiencies. A perfect tagger would achieve arbitrarily large
background rejection at $\varepsilon_b = 1$. In practice, the ROC curve lies between these
extremes, and the area under the curve (AUC) provides a single threshold-independent summary of
overall tagger performance. Specific working points --- typically $\varepsilon_b = 60\%$, $70\%$,
$77\%$, and $85\%$ --- are defined by fixing the $b$-jet efficiency and quoting the corresponding
background rejection, allowing direct comparison between taggers and across analyses.

Figure~\ref{fig:ROC:GN1} shows the output discriminant distributions for \texttt{DL1r} and
\texttt{GN1} evaluated on a simulated $t\bar{t}$ sample at $\sqrt{s} = 13$~TeV. The $b$-jet
distributions are peaked at high discriminant values, while the $c$-jet and light-jet
distributions are concentrated at lower values, reflecting the ability of both taggers to
separate $b$-jets from background. The \texttt{GN1} distributions show a more pronounced
separation between the three jet flavours, qualitatively indicating improved discrimination
relative to \texttt{DL1r}. The vertical dashed lines indicate the $70\%$ and $80\%$ $b$-jet
efficiency working points, illustrating how a threshold on the discriminant defines an
operating point on the ROC curve.

\section{Conclusion}

This chapter has traced the evolution of $b$-tagging algorithms in ATLAS from the early
multivariate taggers of Run~2 through to the graph- and attention-based architectures deployed
for Run~3. The progression reflects a consistent trend: each generation of taggers has moved
closer to the raw detector information, replacing hand-engineered summary variables with learned
representations operating directly on tracks, vertices, and hits.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/chapter4/performance_bar_graph.png}
	\caption{https://atlas.web.cern.ch/Atlas/GROUPS/PHYSICS/PLOTS/FTAG-2023-01/}
	\label{fig:history}
\end{figure}

The performance gains from this evolution are summarized in Figure~\ref{fig:history}. These gains have direct physics implications. Higher background rejection at fixed signal
efficiency translates to cleaner $b$-jet samples, reduced systematic uncertainties on flavor
tagging scale factors, and improved sensitivity in analyses that rely on $b$-jet identification
--- from top quark measurements to searches for new phenomena involving heavy-flavor final
states. Looking ahead, further improvements are anticipated through the incorporation of
additional low-level inputs, larger training samples exploiting the full Run~3 dataset, and
continued architectural development motivated by the demands of the High-Luminosity LHC, where
the increased pile-up environment will place even greater demands on the precision and robustness
of flavour tagging algorithms.
