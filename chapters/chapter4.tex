\section{Introduction to Tagging B-Jets}

Heavy particles produced in high-energy collisions, such as top quark and Higgs boson, decay with significant branching fractions to bottom quarks. Identification of jets originating from b-quarks is a critical task for the LHC physics program. The b quark decays via the weak force $b \to c W$.  The CKM matrix dictates lifetime of the decay. Since the $V_{cb}$ matrix element is small, the weak decay proceeds slowly relative to lighter quarks producing comparatively long lifetime ($\sim$1.5~ps). This lifetime is long enough that a B hadron produced at a typical LHC collision point travels a measurable distance, on the order of a few millimeters, before decaying, giving rise to a secondary vertex that is spatially displaced from the primary interaction vertex.

The displacement of the secondary vertex can be characterised by the impact parameters of its associated tracks. The transverse impact parameter $d_0$ is the distance of closest approach between a track and the primary vertex in the transverse plane, while the longitudinal impact parameter $z_0$ is the corresponding distance along the beam axis. Tracks originating from a displaced B-hadron decay will exhibit statistically significant non-zero values of $d_0$ and $z_0$, providing a powerful discriminant against light-flavour jets whose tracks are consistent with the primary vertex.

\section{Overview of Architectures for Modeling Jets}

The identification of b-jets at the LHC reduces to a binary classification problem. Modern machine learning methods, and in particular deep neural networks, are well suited to perform b-tagging. Given a set of input observables such as track kinematics and secondary vertex properties, NNs can learn non-linear decision boundaries in high-dimensional feature spaces directly from simulated training samples. Furthermore, approaches based on recurrent or attention-based architectures can operate directly on the full set of tracks and vertices within a jet, treating the jet as a variable-length sequence or point cloud rather than a fixed-size feature vector. This preserves information that is inevitably discarded when constructing the high-level summary variables on which cut-based and low-dimensional likelihood approaches depend on. The result is a significant improvement in b-tagging efficiency at fixed light-jet rejection relative to classical algorithms, with direct benefits for analyses that rely on b-jet identification for signal selection or background suppression.

\subsection{Graph Neural Networks and GN1}

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering

A graph neural network (GNN) represents a set of objects as nodes in a graph, with edges
encoding pairwise relationships between them. Message passing operations allow each node to
iteratively aggregate information from its neighbours, building representations that capture
both individual object properties and the collective structure of the ensemble. This is a natural
fit for the tracks within a jet, which form an unordered set with meaningful pairwise
relationships --- such as the angular separation or compatibility with a common decay vertex.

\texttt{GN1} represented a more fundamental architectural shift, moving away from the paradigm
of combining separate low-level and high-level input streams towards an end-to-end graph-based
representation of the jet. Tracks are treated as nodes in a fully connected graph, with message
passing used to build up representations encoding both individual track properties and the
collective structure of the track ensemble. Notably, \texttt{GN1} was the first ATLAS b-tagger
to operate directly on low-level inputs, incorporating raw tracker hit information rather than
relying solely on reconstructed track parameters and pre-computed vertex quantities. This allows
the network to exploit information that is lost during track reconstruction, such as hit
occupancy and local detector geometry, and represents a significant departure from all preceding
taggers. \texttt{GN1} also introduced multi-task training: in addition to the primary jet-level
$b$/$c$/light classification objective, the network was simultaneously trained to predict the
origin of each individual track --- whether it originated from the primary vertex, a $b$-hadron
decay, a $c$-hadron decay, or a hadronic interaction. This auxiliary track-classification
objective acts as a powerful regulariser, encouraging the network to build physically
interpretable representations that generalise well across different jet kinematics. The
graph-based message passing, combined with multi-task learning and the use of low-level inputs,
produced substantial improvements in both light-jet and $c$-jet rejection over \texttt{DL1d}.

The transformer architecture %~\cite{Vaswani2017} 
processes a set of objects by computing, for
each object, a weighted sum of all other object representations --- including itself. The weights
are not fixed by the network topology but are computed dynamically from the inputs: each object
produces a query vector and a set of key vectors, and the attention weight between two objects
is determined by the similarity of their query and key representations. Concretely, for a jet
with $N$ tracks, this produces an $N \times N$ attention matrix at each layer, where entry
$(i,j)$ reflects how much track $i$ attends to track $j$ when updating its representation.
Crucially, these weights are learned from data, meaning the network discovers which pairwise
track correlations are most discriminating for $b$-tagging without any prior assumption about
which tracks should be related. A transformer encoder block consists of one such self-attention
operation followed by a small feed-forward network applied independently to each track, with
residual connections and layer normalisation around both. Stacking multiple encoder blocks allows
the network to build up increasingly abstract representations of the full track ensemble.

\texttt{GN2} extended the graph neural network approach by replacing the message passing
aggregation of \texttt{GN1} with a self-attention mechanism inspired by the transformer
architecture. Rather than aggregating neighbour information through fixed pooling operations,
self-attention computes a dynamic weighted sum of all other track representations, where the
weights are learned functions of the query and key representations of each pair of tracks. This
allows the network to selectively focus on the most relevant pairwise relationships for a given
jet, rather than treating all edges equally. \texttt{GN2} retained the multi-task track-origin
classification objective of \texttt{GN1} and extended the input feature set to include
additional vertex and track quality variables. The attention mechanism provides both a
performance benefit --- by more flexibly modelling long-range correlations between tracks --- and
an interpretability benefit, since the learned attention weights can be inspected to understand
which track pairs the network considers most informative for a given tagging decision.
\texttt{GN2} currently represents the state-of-the-art b-tagger deployed in ATLAS, achieving
the best light-jet and $c$-jet rejection of any tagger in the sequence, and forming the basis
for ongoing development towards the High-Luminosity LHC era.

\section{Benchmarks and Results}

The performance of a b-tagger is evaluated using a receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve.
The tagger produces a continuous discriminant score for each jet, and a working point is defined
by placing a threshold on this score: jets above the threshold are classified as $b$-jets, those
below are rejected. By scanning the threshold continuously from its maximum to its minimum value,
one traces out a curve in the space of signal efficiency and background rejection. The signal
efficiency, or true positive rate, is the fraction of true $b$-jets that pass the threshold; the
background rejection is the inverse of the false positive rate, which is the fraction of
non-$b$-jets incorrectly accepted. In HEP it is conventional to plot the light-jet rejection
$1/\varepsilon_\text{light}$ and $c$-jet rejection $1/\varepsilon_c$ as functions of the $b$-jet
efficiency $\varepsilon_b$, rather than the true and false positive rates directly. A tagger with
no discriminating power would accept signal and background at equal rates, corresponding to a
flat rejection of unity for all efficiencies. A perfect tagger would achieve arbitrarily large
background rejection at $\varepsilon_b = 1$. In practice, the ROC curve lies between these
extremes, and the area under the curve (AUC) provides a single threshold-independent summary of
overall tagger performance. Specific working points --- typically $\varepsilon_b = 60\%$, $70\%$,
$77\%$, and $85\%$ --- are defined by fixing the $b$-jet efficiency and quoting the corresponding
background rejection, allowing direct comparison between taggers and across analyses.

Figure~\ref{fig:ROC:GN1} shows the output discriminant distributions for \texttt{DL1r} and
\texttt{GN1} evaluated on a simulated $t\bar{t}$ sample at $\sqrt{s} = 13$~TeV. The $b$-jet
distributions are peaked at high discriminant values, while the $c$-jet and light-jet
distributions are concentrated at lower values, reflecting the ability of both taggers to
separate $b$-jets from background. The \texttt{GN1} distributions show a more pronounced
separation between the three jet flavours, qualitatively indicating improved discrimination
relative to \texttt{DL1r}. The vertical dashed lines indicate the $70\%$ and $80\%$ $b$-jet
efficiency working points, illustrating how a threshold on the discriminant defines an
operating point on the ROC curve.

\section{Boosted Jet Tagging with Graph and Attention Neural Networks}

In addition to small-R jet tagging which typically classifies jets as b, c, or light, large-R jet tagging (R=1.0), classifies boosted objects. When an object is boosted, the decay products often overlap in the lab frame. Identifying these boosted jets is crucial for the Higgs program at the LHC since a boosted Higgs can hadronically decay in the following channels: $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ and $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$. The backgrounds considered for are multijet (QCD) processes and fully hadronic top-quark decays. Taking advantage of transformer and graph architecture, GN2X is capable of learning directly on track inputs to learn the correlations within the large-R jet. GN2X produces an embedding of the jet in a 4D output with a softmax function applied across the predictions to enable the output to be interpreted as a probability of four classes: $p_{Hbb}$,$p_{Hcc}$, $p_{top}$, and $p_{QCD}$. The 4D output can be reduced to a single 1D output using the formulas for the discriminant shown below, which roughly resembles log likelihood. 

\begin{align}
	D^{GN2X}_{Hbb} &= ln \left( \frac{p_{Hbb}}{f_{Hcc} \cdot p_{Hcc} + f_{top} \cdot p_{top} + (1-f_{Hcc}-f_{top})\cdot p_{QCD}} \right) \\
	D^{GN2X}_{Hcc} &= ln \left( \frac{p_{Hcc}}{f_{Hbb} \cdot p_{Hbb} + f_{top} \cdot p_{top} + (1-f_{Hbb}-f_{top})\cdot p_{QCD}} \right)
\end{align}

By cutting on the discriminant score, we can construct working points according to how much of the signal is retained by implementing the cut. Working points are defined by certain cuts on signal efficiency, $\epsilon_{sig}$, where signal efficiency is defined as the ratio of signal events passing the cut divided by the total number of signal events. Conventionally, there are 3 (or more) working points: loose, medium, and tight. A loose working point cuts the least amount of signal, but allows for the most background contamination. A tight working point cuts a lot of signal statistics, but supresses the most background. Working points are analysis dependent, and should be chosen empirically but choosing the working point that maximizes signal sensitivity in your signal region. Shown in Fig \ref{fig:GN2X_Discriminant}, we can see a cut at the 50\% signal efficiency working point which, by definition, is cutting 50\% of the signal, but heavily suppressing the background. You can notice that the dominant background in this figure is from Top decay, blue line. Therefore, this background will be important to calibrate to data, more on that later.

These taggers are trained on MC where truth labels exist. MC is not a perfect model for data. There can exist mismodeling effects at multiple different stages including many effects from: generator, showering, detector, and reconstruction. Therefore, a working point defined in MC does not perfectly extrapolate to the same signal and background efficiency in data. To quantify the differences between phase spaces after implementing a cut, we must perform a calibration technique to account lack of excess of events in data.

\section{ttbar Mistag Calibration}

Top decay is a major source of background for GN2X $H \rightarrow bb$ and $H \rightarrow cc$, therefore, it is an important background to calibrate. As part of my ATLAS authorship qualification task, I investigated the $t\bar{t}$ mistag rate which quantifies the amount of ttbar events passing the predefined working point selections. However, the mistag rate is not the same in MC and data due to mismodeling effects. Therefore, scale factors are derived which is defined as the ratio of efficiency in data and efficiency in MC.

\begin{equation}
	SF=\frac{\epsilon^{data}}{\epsilon^{MC}}
\end{equation}

Efficiency is the number of ttbar events passing selection divided by the total number of ttbar events. Since the number of ttbar events in data is unknown, we must extract it by performing a maximum likelihood fit. In each region (defined later) we can perform a counting experiment based on the simple fact that the number of expected MC events should match the number of events observed in data. Since MC does not perfectly agree with data, we have 2 degrees of freedom: $\mu$ and $SF$. Here $\mu$ is the ttbar normalization factor that accounts for the overall normalization between MC and data, and $SF$ is the factor that accounts for the discrepancy introduced by the tagger. With these two unknowns, we can construct two equations (one for SR and one for CR), to simultaneously extract $\mu$ and $SF$. 

\begin{align}
	N^{data}_{tag} &= \mu \cdot \frac{\epsilon^{data}}{\epsilon^{MC}} \cdot N^{ttbar}_{tag} + N^{other}_{tag} \\
	N^{data}_{untag} &= \mu \cdot \frac{1-\epsilon^{data}}{1-\epsilon^{MC}} \cdot N^{ttbar}_{untag} + N^{other}_{untag}
\end{align}

Or equivalently the formula can be written in terms of scale factors:

\begin{align}
	N^{data}_{tag} &= \mu \cdot SF \cdot N^{ttbar}_{tag} + N^{other}_{tag} \\
	N^{data}_{untag} &= \mu \cdot \frac{1-\epsilon^{MC} \cdot SF}{1-\epsilon^{MC}} \cdot N^{ttbar}_{untag} + N^{other}_{untag}
\end{align}

Since there are no floating normalization factors applied to the backgrounds, we must find a region in phase space where the backgrounds are heavily suppressed and the ttbar signal is dominant. To do so, we target semi-leptonic ttbar events, where the leptonic top decay is considered "tag" and the hadronic top decay is considered "probe".

\subsection{Tag-and-Probe Event Selection}

Semi-leptonic ttbar decay has a very unique signature in the detector, and reconstructing this topology suppresses many backgrounds. The most dominant background is W+jets followed by single-top, and di-Boson. We use the hadronic largeR jet for the "probe" tagging candidate. The leptonic decaying top is used to as the "tag" candidate. A lepton+MET trigger is used as a foothold into this phase space. By implementing a b-tagging requirement on the "tag" side, we can suppress many of the contributions from W+jets. The feynman diagram shown in \ref{fig:semileptonic_ttbar_diagram}.

Physics objects are reconstructed and calibrated before event selection. Electrons are required 
to have $p_\text{T} > 28$ GeV, $|\eta_\text{cluster}| < 2.47$ (excluding the barrel/endcap 
transition region $1.37 < |\eta_\text{cluster}| < 1.52$), pass tight identification, and match 
a track in the inner detector (ID). Muons must satisfy $p_\text{T} > 28$ GeV, $|\eta| < 2.5$, 
and pass medium identification by matching ID and muon spectrometer tracks. Both lepton types 
must meet tight isolation criteria and primary vertex compatibility requirements.

Small-$R$ jets are built via the anti-$k_\text{T}$ algorithm ($R = 0.4$) using particle flow 
inputs, requiring $p_\text{T} > 25$ GeV and $|\eta| < 2.5$, with pile-up suppression via the 
Jet Vertex Tagger. $B$-jets are identified using the DL1r multivariate algorithm. Large-$R$ 
jets ($R = 1.0$) are reconstructed from topological LCTopo clusters using the anti-$k_\text{T}$ 
algorithm, then trimmed to reduce pile-up and soft emission effects --- subjets carrying less 
than 5\% of the large-$R$ jet $p_\text{T}$ are removed. Only calibrated jets with 
$p_\text{T} > 300$ GeV and $|\eta| < 2.0$ are considered. Variable-Radius track-jets 
($p_\text{T} > 7$ GeV, $0.02 \leq R \leq 0.4$) are ghost-associated to large-$R$ jets for 
flavour tagging.

Overlap removal is applied sequentially to prevent double-counting: electrons within 
$\Delta R = 0.01$ of a muon are removed first, followed by jets within $\Delta R = 0.2$ of 
an electron. Electrons within $\Delta R = 0.4$ of a remaining jet are then discarded. Muons 
are rejected if within $\Delta R < 0.04 + 10~\text{GeV}/p_\text{T}(\mu)$ of a jet with three 
or more tracks; otherwise the jet is removed.

The missing transverse momentum ($E_\text{T}^\text{miss}$) is computed as the magnitude of 
the negative vector sum of all calibrated object momenta, plus a track-based soft term matched 
to the primary vertex to ensure resilience against pile-up.

Events must pass a single-electron or single-muon trigger with $p_\text{T} > 28$ GeV, and are 
further required to contain exactly one lepton with $p_\text{T} > 70$ GeV to suppress 
non-prompt lepton backgrounds. $E_\text{T}^\text{miss} > 70$ GeV is imposed to account for 
the neutrino from the leptonic $W$ decay. At least one large-$R$ jet with $p_\text{T} \geq 
300$ GeV must be present, with the leading-$p_\text{T}$ jet designated as the probe-jet 
candidate; its mass is restricted to $50~\text{GeV} \leq m \leq 300~\text{GeV}$ to ensure 
compatibility with the calibrated LCTopo jet mass range. At least one small-$R$ jet, 
specifically the closest to the lepton in $\Delta R$, must pass $b$-tagging at the 77\% DL1r 
working point and serves as the tag-jet, significantly suppressing the $V$+jets background.

\subsection{Constructing Fit Regions}

The goal of this analysis is to measure the mistag efficiency $\epsilon^{data}_{ttbar}$ for different values of $p_T^{probe}$. We can extract the mistag efficiency by fitting the two previously defined parameters: ttbar normalization $\mu$ and SF. The signal regions are defined to have events that pass a given working point for the GN2Xv01 tagger, and control regions are populated with events that fail tagging requirements. As of the time of my qualification task, working points had not yet been finalized so a flat cut at 1.76 was used to approximate the 70\% working point. In recent developments, the GN2X working points are no longer defined as flat cuts on the discriminant. To reduce background sculpting, working points are defined as a function of the discriminant, jet mass, and jet pT.

\subsection{Results}

A normalization factor and SF is derived per $p_T$ region. These parameters are extracted by maximizing the likelihood fit function. This can be interpreted as: what choice of parameters ($\mu$ and SF) yield the highest probability that the observed data matches the predicted MC. These fits only included statistical uncertainties, but a final result would also include systematic uncertainties such as: detector resolution, jet reconstruction, and flavor tagging uncertainties. The stat-only MC-16 results derived on full run 2 dataset are shown in \ref{fig:ttbar_SF:MC16}. The stat-only MC-20 results derived on full run 2 dataset are shown in \ref{fig:ttbar_SF:MC20}. The largest difference between these results are found in the ttbar normalization factors which were significantly lower in MC16 compared to MC20. Despite these differences, the SF between MC16 and MC20 are in much better agreement.

\section{Conclusion}

This chapter has traced the evolution of $b$-tagging algorithms in ATLAS from the early
multivariate taggers of Run~2 through to the graph- and attention-based architectures deployed
for Run~3. The progression reflects a consistent trend: each generation of taggers has moved
closer to the raw detector information, replacing hand-engineered summary variables with learned
representations operating directly on tracks, vertices, and hits.

The performance gains from this evolution are summarized in Figure~\ref{fig:history}. These gains have direct physics implications. Higher background rejection at fixed signal
efficiency translates to cleaner $b$-jet samples, reduced systematic uncertainties on flavor
tagging scale factors, and improved sensitivity in analyses that rely on $b$-jet identification
--- from top quark measurements to searches for new phenomena involving heavy-flavor final
states. Looking ahead, further improvements are anticipated through the incorporation of
additional low-level inputs, larger training samples exploiting the full Run~3 dataset, and
continued architectural development motivated by the demands of the High-Luminosity LHC, where
the increased pile-up environment will place even greater demands on the precision and robustness
of flavor tagging algorithms.

This chapter presented the calibration of the GN2Xv01 boosted jet tagger using a 
$t\bar{t}$-enriched region derived from semi-leptonic top quark decays. The tag-and-probe 
event selection strategy proved effective at isolating a high-purity $t\bar{t}$ sample, 
suppressing dominant backgrounds such as $W$+jets and single-top contributions to a 
manageable level. The unique detector signature of semi-leptonic $t\bar{t}$ events, combined 
with a $b$-tagging requirement on the leptonic-side tag jet, provided the clean phase space 
necessary for a reliable extraction of the mistag rate.

The GN2X discriminant was used to construct the signal and control regions, demonstrating 
good data-MC agreement across all probe jet $p_T$ bins after performing the simultaneous 
maximum likelihood fit, successfully extracting the $t\bar{t}$ normalization factor $\mu$ 
and scale factor $SF$ in each $p_T$ slice. Scale factors were derived for both MC16 and MC20 
configurations using the full Run 2 dataset, showing good mutual consistency despite notable 
differences in the extracted normalization factors between the two campaigns. The techniques 
and infrastructure developed here lay the groundwork for a full GN2Xv01 calibration to be 
deployed in future ATLAS Higgs boson analyses targeting boosted $H \rightarrow b\bar{b}$ 
and $H \rightarrow c\bar{c}$ signatures.
